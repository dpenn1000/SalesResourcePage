Selling with Integrity

Honest expectations are the whole business. A customer who knows what they're getting stays a customer, sends referrals, and never cancels. One who feels misled becomes a lawsuit. This is how we keep the first kind, and why the things you say and write in a living room matter long after you leave it.

Integrity Is the Business, Not a Box to Check

Start with the upside. An honest deal is a durable deal. Then know the stakes, because in the Northeast a misleading one carries weight you cannot afford to learn about the hard way.

What honesty builds

  • Trust earns referrals. The customer who feels respected sends you the next three doors.
  • Honest expectations stick. Set them right and the customer is happy at month 2, not calling to cancel.
  • A clean book compounds. Deals that fund, stay funded, and refer are how a career gets built.
  • You sleep fine. Nothing you said comes back on you.

What the stakes are

  • Consumer Fraud Acts bite hard. NJ, CT, MA, NY, and others can triple the award and add the customer's legal fees on top.
  • The math is brutal. A $20,000 claim becomes $60,000 before attorney fees. That is what makes a small case worth a five-day trial.
  • You can be named. A rep can be held personally liable for what they told the customer.
  • More lawyers are watching. There are more solar plaintiff attorneys hunting these cases than ever.
One idea to carry: integrity protects three people at once. The customer, the company, and you. The same honest sentence does all three jobs.
Ask the room: name a customer who referred you because they trusted you. What did you do to earn that? Now flip it. What is the one thing a rep could say tonight that would cost the company more than the deal is worth?
Why does a Consumer Fraud Act claim cost so much more than the deal?

AnswerSeveral Northeast statutes triple the damages and shift the customer's attorney fees onto the company. A modest loss turns into a large judgment, which is exactly why a plaintiff's lawyer will take a small case all the way to trial.

A Recent Case: Five Mistakes, Five Rules

A Trinity rep's deal went to a five-day Consumer Fraud Act trial. Four of five charges were thrown out. The fifth went to a jury. Nobody set out to defraud anyone. Five small habits stacked up into a case. Each one is a rule you can follow tonight.

1Stale numbers left behind

The rep corrected the loan amount on a brochure but left the tax-credit and SREC figures keyed to the old, higher number. Inconsistent math on paper reads as deception.

Rule: fix one number, fix every number it touches.

2Interest never disclosed

The written loan figure left out interest, and the rep never said so or named the rate. A number that hides its biggest cost is the most-litigated defect in solar finance.

Rule: disclose the rate, the APR, and that a loan figure may exclude interest.

3Math he couldn't defend

He could not show how he got the "cost of electricity without solar." On the stand, a number you cannot derive is treated as one you made up.

Rule: show your work, or don't write the number.

4The no-bill promise

He told the customer they'd have no electric bill, only connection fees. False. You cannot predict future usage, rates climb, and the utility relationship remains.

Rule: never promise the bill goes away.

5A 22-second signature

The customer signed a 40-page contract in 22 seconds, with no evidence the rep reviewed it first. The clock became the plaintiff's best exhibit.

Rule: let the customer truly read and sign.

The thread through all five

None of these were lies told on purpose. They were shortcuts. The next six slides turn each rule into a habit so a shortcut never costs you a deal, your company, or your name.

Ask the room: which of these five could happen on one of your appointments this week? Be honest. Pick the one you'd most want a teammate to catch you on. Alex asked legal to send these cases over so we can run them on Coffee with Closers and Tuesday trainings, so this is the first of many.
Four charges were dismissed. Why did the case still go five days?

AnswerThe fifth charge, the Consumer Fraud claim, went to a jury rather than the judge. A jury decides on what feels right or wrong, so the whole picture, the stale numbers, the missing interest, the no-bill promise, the 22-second signature, became the story. Any one habit can carry a case that far.

The One Rule That Prevents Most of It: the Bill

More cases start here than anywhere else. Promising the bill disappears is the single most-cited misrepresentation in the industry. Get this one habit right and you close off most of your exposure in a single sentence.

Never say

  • "You'll have no electric bill." You can't predict their future usage.
  • "Just connection fees from now on." Rates climb and usage shifts.
  • Full coverage of your usage. A promise you can't keep is a trap set for month 2.
  • Any guaranteed zero outcome. The customer keeps a utility relationship, period.

Say instead

  • Lead with what solar can do. Take on the part of the bill that costs the most and climbs the most.
  • Set the bar low. Roughly one in three homes even qualifies, and even covering a piece of usage can leave a homeowner ahead.
  • Name the small charge. "You'll still have a charge for being connected, and that's fine."
  • Over-deliver. Promise modestly, let the system beat the promise.
Say · the honest bill frame"A lot of homeowners feel unsure it's even worth it. The folks I've helped felt the same way, and what they found was that even covering a piece of their usage left them ahead. Here's what I can help you with: the part of your bill that costs the most and climbs the most, we take that on and lock it in. You'll still have a small charge for being connected, and that's fine."
Where to go deeper: this is the integrity reason behind Kill the Bill and Selling the Rate with Conviction. Those decks show you how to lead with the bill honestly. This is the why: a promise you can't keep is the line that ends in court.
Ask the room: have one rep pitch the bill the wrong way, then the right way. What changed? Then ask: when a customer says "so my bill goes to zero, right?" what is your exact next sentence?
A customer asks, "So I'll have no bill, right?" How do you answer?

AnswerYou don't confirm it. Reset the expectation: "I can't promise the bill goes away, and I wouldn't. What I can do is take on the part that costs the most and climbs the most, and you'll still have a small charge for staying connected." Lead with what solar can do, set the bar low, over-deliver.

What You Write Down Can Be Used Against You

A brochure, a text, an email. Anything you leave with a customer becomes their evidence. In the recent case, a few numbers scrawled on a brochure were the heart of the trial. Treat every written figure like it will be read aloud in a courtroom, because it might be.

Every number stays accurate

  • Fix one, fix all. Correct the loan and you must correct the tax credit, the SREC, and the savings that depend on it.
  • No stale figures. A crossed-out number next to a dependent one nobody updated reads as a lie.

Disclose the full cost

  • Name the interest and APR. Say plainly when a loan figure excludes interest.
  • Name any dealer or origination fee. Hidden fees baked into principal are a top enforcement target.

Label and never guarantee

  • Mark estimates as estimates. An estimate dressed as a fact is the problem.
  • Write no guarantees. Not on savings, not on the bill, not on production.
The simple test: before you hand anything over, ask, "Could I defend every number on this page under oath?" If the answer isn't a clean yes, don't leave it. When in doubt, leave the official proposal and nothing hand-written.
Ask the room: pass around a real (anonymized) leave-behind. Can every rep defend every number on it? Find one figure nobody can derive. That's the one that becomes Exhibit A.
You lower the loan amount on a brochure. What else has to change?

AnswerEvery number that depends on it. The tax credit, the SREC estimate, and any savings figure all key off the loan amount, so all of them revise together. Correcting one and leaving the rest stale is exactly the mistake that anchored the recent case.

The 2026 Tax-Credit Reality

The rules changed at the start of this year, and getting them wrong is a fast way into the exact kind of case regulators are filing right now. Know the line cold: who still gets a federal benefit, and who does not.

Owned, loan, or cash solar

  • No homeowner federal credit in 2026. The Section 25D credit ended for systems placed in service after December 31, 2025.
  • Never promise "30% back from the government" on an owned, loan, or cash system. It isn't there anymore.
  • This is the Attyx-style failure. Misstating credit eligibility is squarely what AGs are suing over.

Lease or PPA (third-party owned)

  • Still captures a 30%-style benefit. A third-party-owned system uses the surviving commercial credit (48E), and that value gets built into the customer's rate.
  • Frame it honestly. The benefit lives in the lease or PPA pricing, not as a check the homeowner files for.
  • State programs still stack. Northeast battery and storage incentives remain real. Cite the actual program, not a vague "government money."

Don't say

  • "You'll get 30% back" on a cash or loan solar deal in 2026.
  • "The government pays for" the system, the roof, or any part of it.
  • A guaranteed credit dollar amount. Eligibility depends on the customer's own tax situation.

Do say

  • "On a lease or PPA, that benefit is built into your rate." True and clean.
  • "On an owned system this year, that federal credit is gone." Honest, and it steers the right product.
  • "Check with your tax advisor" for anything that depends on their tax situation.
Ask the room: a customer says their neighbor got "30% back from the government" last year on a cash system. How do you handle it without calling the neighbor a liar, and without repeating the claim for 2026?
It's 2026. A customer wants to buy a system cash. Can you promise a 30% federal credit?

AnswerNo. The homeowner Section 25D credit ended for systems placed in service after December 31, 2025. An owned, loan, or cash system gets no federal homeowner credit this year. Only a third-party-owned lease or PPA still captures a 30%-style benefit, and that value sits in the rate, not in a check the homeowner files for.

Let the Customer Truly Review and Sign

A signature is not a finish line you rush them across. A 22-second signature on a 40-page contract is a liability, not a win, because it proves no real review happened. Slow this moment down on purpose.

  1. Show the contract

    Put the actual document on screen. The customer should see and be able to read what they're signing, not a summary.

    No blind signatures

  2. Walk the key terms

    Price, interest and APR, term, escalator, and what the bill will and won't include. Say each one out loud.

    Genuine review

  3. Give the 3-day right

    Explain the right to cancel within 3 business days, orally and in writing, with two copies of the cancellation notice.

    Cooling-off rule

  4. Let real time pass

    Don't pre-fill, rush, or sign for them. A real review takes minutes, and that time is your defense.

    The clock matters
The standard behind this: the cooling-off rule and the SEIA consumer-protection standard (ANSI/SEIA 401-2025) both exist to make sure the customer understands the terms before they commit. Never frame a binding contract as an "estimate," a "credit check," or an "application." Never reuse or pre-fill a customer's signature.
Ask the room: walk the signing moment as a role-play. How do you slow it down without killing momentum? What's the line that makes "let's read this together" feel like service, not friction?
Why is a 22-second signature a problem even if you explained everything first?

AnswerBecause the record shows 22 seconds and no evidence of a walkthrough. The clock is objective; your verbal explanation isn't on the page. A near-instant signature undercuts the contract's enforceability and corroborates the customer's claim that they never got to review it. Show the document, walk the terms, give the 3-day notice, and let genuine time pass.

Honest Savings Math

Savings claims have to hold up under scrutiny, not just sound good at the kitchen table. The rule is simple: every number ties to this home, and you can show how you got it. Anything you can't derive, you don't say.

What a defensible number looks like

  • Tied to their actual bill. Real usage, real roof, real shade, not a best-case home.
  • Built on a defensible escalator. The historical norm runs roughly 3 to 3.5% a year. Use that, and be able to show it.
  • Stated as an estimate. A projection, clearly labeled, never a guarantee of future savings.
  • Derivable on demand. If a customer or a court asks how you got it, you can walk it line by line.

What gets a rep sued

  • An inflated escalator. Projecting 6 to 8% a year to juice the savings, with no basis you can defend.
  • A "cost without solar" you can't justify. The recent case turned partly on a number the rep couldn't explain.
  • Best-case production. Real systems often come in 10 to 25% under an optimistic estimate.
  • "You'll save X, guaranteed." A guarantee on the future is a promise you can't keep.
State your assumptions out loud. "I'm using a conservative rate increase, here's the figure, and here's how I built your number." Naming the assumption is what turns a scary projection into a defensible one. Don't understate the future cost of electricity in a way you couldn't back up later either. Accurate cuts both ways.
Ask the room: pick a recent proposal. What escalator did it use? Can the rep show where that number comes from? If the answer is "the tool just does it," that's a coaching gap worth closing tonight.
What's wrong with projecting an 8% annual rate increase to show bigger savings?

AnswerIt isn't defensible. The historical norm is closer to 3 to 3.5%, so an 8% escalator overstates savings on an assumption you can't justify. If a customer or a court asks how you derived it, "rates always go up" isn't an answer. Use a conservative escalator, disclose it, and tie the number to the home's actual usage.

The Integrity That Protects You

The first seven slides are about the customer and the contract. This one is about you. The reps who last build a few quiet habits: they get out of their own way, own a miss without torching themselves, hold the price, refuse to put solar on a bad roof, and keep showing up. Each one protects your name and, over time, your confidence to sit at the next table.

Whoever offends the least makes the most

Most deals are lost by self-inflicted mistakes, not by the customer. You can grab a lot of deals just by not messing them up.

  • Don't volunteer the inverter brand. The second you name it, they Google what inverters Trinity uses, and now you're defending a detail you didn't need to raise.
  • Don't volunteer the roof-penetration warranty or any technical detail that sends them off researching instead of deciding.
  • Stay off topics that backfire. Get out of your own way and let a good deal stay a good deal.

Fall on the sword, but never say "lied"

When something goes wrong, take ownership yourself. The customer forgives the salesperson they fell in love with far more than the company or finance partner they're stuck with for 25 years.

  • Own it. Falling on the sword keeps their trust and keeps you the point of contact.
  • Never use the word "lied," even sincerely. A heartfelt "I lied to you" becomes the exact line they repeat to your manager, to Client Resources, and to the BBB.
  • Say instead: "I'm sorry I didn't explain this fully," or "I set the wrong expectation."

Every discount cuts three things

Holding price is an integrity issue, not just a commission one. A discount cuts what the company earns, what you earn, and the company's ability to stay in business and keep its 25-year promises.

  • The real question: "Can we cash the checks we've written?" Cut too deep and the answer turns to no.
  • The frame that holds: "I'd rather apologize once today for the price than for the next 25 years for the quality and service."

Never put solar on a bad roof

This is automatic integrity, and it's why they choose you. Protect the customer from every other company before you protect the sale.

  • Say it plainly: "Whatever we decide today, please don't let anyone put solar on this roof unless they replace it first."
  • That one line puts you on a different plane than almost every other solar rep at the table.
Say · when you've set the wrong expectation"I owe you an apology. I'm sorry I didn't explain this part fully when we sat down. Let me walk you through exactly how it actually works, and then let's figure out the right next step together."
Showing up is the habit underneath all of it. By install and PTO a customer may have talked to 9 to 13 different Trinity people. If you vanish, you become noise. Staying the consistent, honest point of contact, even when you don't have every answer, is what they actually wanted. Showing up at the install and at the hard call builds your own confidence, so you sit at the next table with that same level of integrity.
Ask the room: name a detail a rep volunteered that sent a customer Googling and cost momentum. Then role-play the apology: own the miss out loud without ever saying "lied." Last, who showed up to an install that wasn't going smoothly, and what did it do for the relationship?
A customer is upset about something you got wrong. Why never say "I lied," even if you feel you did?

AnswerBecause that exact word becomes the line they repeat to your manager, to Client Resources, and to the BBB: "my salesman admitted he lied to me." Fall on the sword and own the miss, the customer forgives you more than anybody, but say "I'm sorry I didn't explain this fully" or "I set the wrong expectation." Own the mistake without handing them a weapon.
