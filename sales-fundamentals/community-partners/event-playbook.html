Stage 01 · Before anyone walks up

Set up the table so it does half the work for you.

People decide whether to stop before you say a word. The table, the energy, and the first three seconds are the pitch before the pitch. You are a guest of the organization, so look the part: clean, branded, and welcoming, not a sales booth people swerve around.

01
Look like you belong.
Trinity gear, a clean cloth, the organization's name visible. Make it obvious you are here for them, raising money for them. The cause is your opener, not the panels.
02
Have the sign-up sheet ready.
Clean sheet, working pens, clipboard. Name, address, phone, email, notes. If it is messy or illegible later, the lead and the org's credit are both gone.
03
Get your state right.
Charisma is a decision you make before the first person arrives. Walk in warm, curious, and genuinely glad to be there. People match the energy you bring. Bring the energy you want back.
The mindset that wins events
You are not "selling solar" at a folding table. You are helping a homeowner help an organization they already care about, and they save money doing it. Lead with the cause, stay in service, and the appointment is a natural next step instead of a hard ask.
Stage 02 · The first ten seconds

Draw them in. Do not pounce.

The fastest way to lose a table is to chase. Open with the cause and a low-pressure invitation. Charisma is simple in practice: make the other person feel good about themselves. Smile first, be interested before you are interesting, and match their pace and energy before you try to shift it.

Open with the cause
"Hey, good to see you. We are helping raise money for [Organization] today. Are you a member, or just here for the event?"
"Cool thing is, it costs you nothing. When a homeowner does a free, no-obligation consultation, [Organization] gets $100, and $1,000 if you ever decide to move forward. This program has raised over $825,000 for groups like this one."
Notice what you did not do: you did not pitch solar, talk price, or ask them to buy anything. You handed them an easy way to support a cause and made it free. That is the whole opener.
Do
  • Smile and make them feel welcome before you say anything about solar.
  • Match their energy first. Loud crowd, bring energy. Quiet person, slow down.
  • Ask a question and actually listen to the answer.
  • Lead with the cause and the fact that it is free.
  • Stay relaxed. Relaxed is contagious, and so is pressure.
Don't
  • Monologue. The second you are talking at them, you have lost them.
  • Open with price, panels, or "are you interested in solar?"
  • Stand behind the table like a wall. Come around, be a person.
  • Chase someone who is clearly walking. Wave, smile, let them come back.
  • Pitch the install. Your only goal here is a real conversation.
Stage 03 · Build the relationship

Have a conversation, not a presentation.

The appointment sits because of the relationship you build right here, not because of anything in the CRM. People keep appointments with people they like and trust. Your job at the table is to become a person they would actually answer the phone for. You do that by listening more than you talk and controlling the narrative without dominating it.

Ask, then listen.
"How long have you been in the home?" "Are your bills creeping up like everyone's?" "Ever looked into solar before?" Let them talk. Every answer is something you use later to confirm and to close. The best reps at our events talk less than the homeowner.
Mirror them.
Match tone, pace, and body language. If they lean in, lean in. If they are skeptical, do not steamroll, acknowledge it. Mirroring builds trust faster than any fact about a panel ever will.
Run the company of yes.
Ask questions that earn easy yeses. "Want to lower your bill if it made sense?" "Want to support [Org] while you are at it?" Small yeses stack into a comfortable yes to the appointment.
Control the narrative.
Detailed questions on price, panels, and tax stuff are a trap at the table. Deflect with respect: "Great question, that is exactly what the consultation is for. Let me get you with someone who can run your real numbers."
Why this matters more than the script
A lead that is just a name on a sheet is a coin flip. A lead who liked you, laughed with you, and gave you their phone on purpose shows up. Every minute you spend being genuinely interested in the homeowner is what makes the appointment real later. The relationship is the product at this stage.
Stage 04 · Lock in the lead

Capture the lead, and get into each other's phones.

This is the single most important move at the whole event. Collect a complete, legible lead and, before they walk away, put your number in their phone and theirs in yours. The personal phone connection is what keeps the appointment alive between today and the call.

The phone exchange -- do this every time
"Do me a favor, grab your phone. Here's my number. Shoot me a quick text right now so I'm saved in there. That way when our team calls to set up your free consult, you already know who I am, and it's not some random number you ignore."
Have them text you, not the other way around. The moment they text you from their phone, three things happen at once: you confirm their real number is correct, you are saved in their phone as a person they met, and they have reached out to you first. That last part matters most. Their text to you is their permission. It is a person-to-person relationship they started, not a blast from a system or a call-center number they never agreed to. Bonus: snap a quick photo together and text it to them so the connection sticks.
Set the expectation before they leave
Say it plainly, every time: "I'll text you to find a time that works, and someone from our team may call to confirm. If you get a call from a number you do not know in the next few days, it is probably us, so please pick up. It will be quick." A homeowner who is expecting to hear from you answers. One who forgot does not. And because you are texting to set the time, you are never waiting on that call to land.
Stage 05 · Make it sit

An appointment that is set is not an appointment that sits.

Most events die here. The lead gets entered, the call center calls from a number the homeowner does not recognize, nobody picks up, and a real opportunity quietly disappears. The reps who win do not hand the relationship off and hope. They treat their own text thread as the reliable line, use it to pin down a time, and take control of the outcome all the way to the front door. The call center is your backup. You are the fail-safe.

1
Enter the lead within 24 hours.
Same night if you can. Into the portal, complete and legible, with your notes. If it is not in the portal, the organization gets no credit and the appointment may never get scheduled. Photograph the sign-up sheet and send it to your DM as backup.
2
Text within a day, and work toward a time.
From the number they already saved at the table, and use it to start finding a day. Try: "Hey [name], great meeting you at the [event] today, thanks for supporting [Org]. Let's get your free consult on the calendar. What works better this week, an evening or the weekend? I've got Wednesday around 2 or Friday around 4 open." Most homeowners will never answer a call from a number they do not know, but they will text you back a time that works. The moment they reply "Wednesday at 2" or "Friday after 4," you have a real appointment, set over text, with no cold call needed. Keep it warm and human, but keep it moving toward a time.
3
Lock the time on the schedule yourself.
The moment a homeowner gives you a window over text, own it. Enter the lead with the time already set, and if it is not on the schedule, call the office and have it added right away. Do not sit back and wait on the confirmation call to connect, assume it will not. You are the one making sure this appointment actually lives. And before you lock it, make sure both decision-makers will be home. A consult with only one spouse there is a consult that has to happen twice, if it happens at all.
4
Reconfirm the day before.
A quick text the day before, looking forward to it, here is the time. This one message saves more appointments than anything else you will do. People do not cancel on a person they like, they just no-show a stranger. Be the person.
5
Show up ready.
Reread your notes from the table before you knock. Names, kids, the bill complaint, the roof, the reason they stopped at all. Walking in already knowing them turns a cold sales call into a continued conversation.
Stay in your lane on the texting
The follow-up that works is personal, one-to-one, from the relationship you built at the table, because they gave you their number and expect to hear from you. That is different from automated marketing blasts, which we do not do. Keep it human, keep it relevant to your conversation, and stop the moment someone says they are not interested.
Stage 06 · At the kitchen table

Close the business you already half-closed at the event.

If you ran stages one through five well, you are not closing a stranger, you are finishing a conversation with someone who already likes you and already wants to help their organization. The close is the easy part when the front end is done right.

01
Reconnect to the cause.
Open by reminding them what this is about. They are saving money and putting $1,000 toward [Org] if they move forward. The decision feels good, not just smart.
02
Tie it back to their words.
Use what they told you at the table. "You said the bills keep climbing, here is exactly what that looks like over the next ten years versus going solar." Their reason, in their words, beats your pitch.
03
Handle objections, then ask.
Most objections are timing or trust, both of which the relationship already softened. Acknowledge, answer simply, then ask for the business. You earned the right to ask. Ask.

Non-negotiables

The rules that protect the lead, the org, and you.

Get the relationship right and still lose everything by skipping the basics. These are not suggestions.

Enter every lead in the portal
Within 24 hours, complete and legible. No portal entry = no credit for the organization, and often no scheduled appointment. This is the one rule that, if broken, undoes the entire event.
Photograph the sign-up sheets

Set the call expectation
Tell every homeowner: "Someone will call you in the next few days, please answer." A warned homeowner picks up. A surprised one does not.
Referrals go through the portal
Always. Enter "Referred by [name]" in notes. Mention the W9 if it installs. We never want a partner to feel a lead was taken from them, and the org only gets paid if it is logged correctly.
Keep follow-up personal and consented
The texts that work are one-to-one, from the number they saved, about the conversation you had. Stop immediately if they opt out. No automated blasts, no spam.

Before you work an event solo

Earn your event certification.

Reading this once is not the same as being ready to run a table. Three steps to certified, signed off by your DM.

01
Know the playbook.
Walk your DM through the six stages and the payout numbers from memory. If you can teach it back, you know it.
02
Role-play the table.
Run the opener, the conversation, the phone exchange, and the reconfirm as a live role-play. Get coached on the parts that feel stiff.
03
Shadow a real event.
Work alongside a certified rep at an actual event before you run one alone. Watch how they draw people in and lock the phone exchange.
