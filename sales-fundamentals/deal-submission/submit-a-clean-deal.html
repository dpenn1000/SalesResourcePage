Submit a Clean Deal: Stay Off Track C

A clean submission installs faster and pays faster. A Track C deal stops and sits for days. Most of what trips it is yours to control, and most of it takes minutes to fix if you catch it before you submit.

This deck reflects the CT Traditional program. The rules here are the CT submittal reality: historical usage plus the state's legislated adders, photo sufficiency over checklist-completeness, the LightReach bill rules, and battery location confirmed at the electrical site visit rather than at the table. Partner-specific lines are marked as such, so nothing here reads as a universal rule when it isn't.

A clean deal moves. A Track C deal sits.

Every deal you submit gets reviewed against the finance partner's checklist. Pass it clean and the build moves and your pay moves with it. Miss one required item and the deal stops on Track C until you fix it.

Clean moves you forward

Nothing missing means nothing stops. The deal flows to design, permitting, and install, and your commission flows with it.

Track C stops the deal

A Track C deal typically sits 4 to 6 days, even when the missing piece is a single photo. Stopped is stopped.

The heads-up is your save

Before Track C, you get a Teams message flagging the missing item. Answer it fast. Getting the item in within about 30 minutes can keep the deal moving.

The 30-minute math: turn a missing photo in within 30 minutes off the Teams heads-up and the deal keeps moving. Let it become a Track C email and that same 30-minute fix costs you 4 to 6 days. Same work, very different wait.
From the field: a CT rep had one missing item that took about 30 minutes to track down. The problem wasn't the item. It was that the deal had already hit Track C, so a 30-minute fix sat for several days before it moved. The clock changes from minutes to days the moment it lands on C. Submit complete the first time, and answer the Teams heads-up fast.

The three tracks at a glance

Sales Ops reviews your contract, photos, combined packet, design, and special requests. Where it lands tells you whether the build keeps moving.

  • A

    Track A: Clean

    Validated, nothing missing. Contract complete, all photos in, packet signed, design within setbacks. The deal moves on its own.

  • B

    Track B: Minor, build keeps moving

    Small stuff that does not hold the build. A missing page, signature, or date. ACH. The validation call. A title item. A change-order set. An NTP stipulation. The build proceeds while it clears.

  • C

    Track C: Stopped

    The deal is halted until the item is fixed. Track C exists to protect the electrical and structural review. If a required piece is missing, the build cannot move.

    • Your goal is Track A. Track B is survivable because the build keeps moving. Track C is the one you design your submission to avoid. The next slide is the list of what trips it.
    Quiz the room: name one Track B item and one Track C item. Why does one stop the build and the other doesn't?
    What separates Track B from Track C?

    AnswerTrack B items are minor and the build keeps moving (missing page, ACH, validation call, title item, change-order set, NTP stipulation). Track C stops the deal because a piece the electrical or structural review needs is missing.

    Photos done right

    Photos are the single most common Track C trigger. Two habits fix it: keep them in Site Capture, and shoot them so the reviewer never has to chase you.

    Keep them in Site Capture

    • Site Capture is the source. The Track C team pulls your photos straight out of it.
    • Do not download into Box or SalesOps Docs. They handle that move for you.
    • Hit re-sync. Site Capture does not always sync, so refresh and confirm the photos actually landed.

    Cover the required set

    • All sides of the house.
    • The meter, with the area in front of it clear (move the milk crate).
    • The electrical panel, shot open so they don't chase a closed-door photo.
    • Attic rafter measurements.

    Shoot for sufficiency

    Clear the area, open the panel, get every angle. A photo set that lets the team scope the work means no follow-up and no hold.

    Leave them guessing

    A blocked meter or a closed panel door turns a finished deal into a Track C hold while they email you for the shot you already had access to.

    Role-play the panel shot: cluttered garage, door closed. What does the rep do so the photo scopes the work the first time?
    Do you need to download Site Capture photos into Box or SalesOps Docs?

    AnswerNo. Keep them in Site Capture. The Track C team pulls them straight out and files them where the other departments need them. Just confirm they synced.

    The electric bill rule (LightReach)

    Two things hold deals here: a past-due balance and a missing bill on a new build. Set the expectation at the first sit and neither one surprises you later.

    Past-due balance

    • Under $1,000 is taken for NTP, so the build can start.
    • The balance must clear to zero before PTO. Turn-on waits on a clean account.
    • It has to be an actual bill, not a screenshot and not a proof of payment.

    New build, no bill yet

    • A welcome letter alone isn't enough. LightReach needs the bill for a fully executed contract.
    • These sit on Track C. Escalate them up the chain rather than guessing your way forward.
    • Recover the bill as soon as it exists, then the deal can move.
    Set it at the first sit (Feel / Felt / Found). "I get how that past-due feels like one more thing. Other homeowners have felt the same. What they found is that clearing it to zero before turn-on is just part of qualifying for the program, so we lay the groundwork early and it never becomes a scramble at install." Make the customer aware around time of install: zero past-due, an actual bill, not a screenshot.
    Townhomes are now in play. Townhomes that do not show clear delineation are accepted. Condos identified as condominium on title are still out. Check the title type before you build the deal.
    From the field: a CT rep's deal got put on Track C for a utility past-due under $1,000. That should not happen. LightReach takes a past-due under $1,000 for NTP, so the build can start. Know the threshold. If a deal is held for a sub-$1,000 past-due, it's wrongly held: escalate it to the Track C team to push it forward rather than chasing it yourself.
    From the field: a CT rep had a new-construction buyer with a deed and a utility welcome letter, but no electric bill yet. The deal sat on Track C, because LightReach needs the actual bill for a fully executed contract. The move is to escalate it and set the customer's expectation early. Do not assume the old "recover the bill later" path still carries the deal forward.
    A rep has a new-construction deal sitting on Track C with only a welcome letter. What's the move? (Escalate, don't guess. LightReach needs the bill.)
    A customer has a $400 past-due. Can the deal move forward?

    AnswerYes. Under $1,000 is taken for NTP, so the build starts. The balance has to be cleared to zero before PTO, and it has to be an actual bill at turn-on, not a screenshot or proof of payment. Set that expectation at the first sit.

    Usage accuracy

    Enter the customer's true 12-month usage. Real numbers protect the deal and protect you. Inflated numbers create change-orders, reorders, and legal exposure. This is about honesty, not a cap: CT lets you design to historical usage plus the state's legislated EV and heat-pump adders, so an accurate number is the floor you build the right system on, not a ceiling.

    Enter the real number

    Use the customer's actual 12-month usage. A true number sizes the system right the first time and keeps the deal clean through review.

    Don't inflate to chase coverage

    Padding usage to make production look bigger triggers oversize change-orders and reorders that delay the deal. Writing numbers that aren't real is what creates legal exposure.

    Inflation costs you twice

    An oversize flag means a change-order. Then a reorder of design and electrical. The deal you tried to make bigger sits longer and may shrink anyway.

    If a flag looks wrong, send it up

    Reviewers should size off your entered usage, not re-scan the bill to overrule you. If a deal gets flagged oversize without the bill being scanned, send the example up the chain so we can fix it with Ops.

    From the field: a CT rep entered an approximate usage number, and the design came back flagged oversize and change-ordered off that entered number without the bill ever being scanned. Enter the true 12-month usage so the system sizes right the first time. If a deal gets flagged oversize and no bill scan happened, send that example up the chain so we can fix it with Ops.
    This is the integrity line. Honest usage is both the fast path and the right one. More on why accurate numbers protect you and the customer in the Selling with Integrity training.
    Ask the room: where does usage padding come from? Walk a real example of an oversize change-order that traced back to an inflated entry, and the days it cost.
    Why does inflating usage backfire?

    AnswerIt triggers oversize change-orders and reorders of design and electrical, which delay the deal, and it creates legal exposure because the numbers aren't real. Enter true 12-month usage. If a reviewer flags oversize without scanning the bill, send the example up the chain.

    The habits that keep a deal clean

    A clean submission is not paperwork at the end. It is a handful of habits that start at the table and run all the way to the roof. Build these in and the file walks itself through review.

    "No bill, set a go-back" is usually on you

    A note that says the customer had no utility bill is rarely logistics. It means the problem was not built strongly enough for them to want to dig the bill out. Build the pain and walk the concept, and it is funny how that bill comes right out of the drawer next to them. Hold yourself accountable instead of accepting the smoke screen.

    In the home"Before we even look at numbers, let's talk about what that summer bill actually costs you over the next few years." Build the why, and the bill appears.

    Lock the shingle color at the table

    Turn the tablet to the customer and have them physically pick the color. It is already on the contract, but about 4 in 10 customers forget what they chose by the PM check-in and swear it is wrong. The physical pick is a retention touch point that heads off a Track C color dispute later.

    Ask roof age every consultation

    Ask the roof's age on every sit and log it in the Salesforce notes. We are Trinity Total Home, not just solar. An unclosed lead with a 15-year-old roof is a deal you can re-engage in 3 months, because the note told you the opening was there.

    Work the back end after the sale

    • Get into the action items of your own deals. Monitor the dates that move an install: the structural visit, the two-stage permit, a credit fail.
    • Stay in your lane. Watch and nudge without stepping on the other departments that own those steps.
    • Proactive shortens time-to-roof. Reps who track their own dates have turned a deal around in as little as 17 days to install.

    Be the deal Ops expects to clear

    • Build the relationship so tight that Ops expects your deals to reach the roof, the way you expect a referral lead to close.
    • The better the relationship in the house, the easier the pull-through. A clean file plus a bought-in customer rarely stalls.
    • Reputation compounds. When your name is on the submission, the goal is for review to think "this one clears," not "here we go again."
    These habits bookend the whole deal. Build the problem so the bill comes out, lock the color, log the roof age, then work the back end so the install moves. Every one of them is a clean-submission habit, and most cost you a minute.

    Run This Before You Submit

    Clean-submission checklist

    • Contract complete: signed, dated, right equipment.
    • Required photos in Site Capture: all sides, meter (clear), panel (open), attic measurements. Re-synced.
    • Combined packet present and fully signed.
    • Bill is a full year, current, past-due path set with the customer.
    • True 12-month usage entered. No padding.
    • Certified, or assist noted in the file for battery or roof.
    • Answer the Teams heads-up fast. Beat the Track C email.
