Coaching to a Clean Submission

Two jobs sit with you. Build the clean-submission habit on your team, and escalate the process gaps that are not your reps' to fix. Pair this with the rep deck, Submit a Clean Deal.

This companion reflects the CT Traditional program. The gaps and thresholds here are the CT submittal reality: the LightReach sub-$1,000 past-due rule, photo sufficiency over checklist-completeness, oversize flags off entered usage, and the new-construction bill requirement. The "From the field" scenarios are real CT leadership-call cases, anonymized.

Coach the habit, not the incident

A clean submission is a pre-flight routine, not a personality trait. Reps who run the same checklist every time stop tripping Track C. Your job is to make the routine automatic.

Make it a checklist, every time

Same items, same order, before every submit. Contract, photos in Site Capture, packet signed, true usage, bill path set. Run it with the rep until it's muscle memory.

Coach to the pattern

One rep, three photo-misses this week is a habit gap, not bad luck. Pull their Track C reasons and coach the recurring line, not the one-off.

Celebrate the fast saves

Name the rep who cleared a Teams heads-up in 30 minutes. Reward the behavior you want repeated. Fast turnaround is the whole game.

The win you're coaching toward: a deal that turns a missing photo around in 30 minutes off the heads-up keeps moving. The same item left to a Track C email costs 4 to 6 days. Coach the 30-minute reflex.
From the field: a CT rep had a missing item that took about 30 minutes to get. But the deal had already landed on Track C, so that 30-minute fix sat for several days. The lesson to coach: the clock changes from minutes to days the moment a deal hits C. Submit complete the first time, and answer the Teams heads-up fast.
Pull one rep's current Track C deals live. What's the recurring reason? Build the one habit that prevents it.
What are you actually coaching when you coach clean submissions?

AnswerA repeatable pre-submit routine. Same checklist, same order, every time. Coach the recurring Track C reason for each rep, not the one-off, and reward fast Teams turnarounds.

The daily Teams heads-up cadence

The Track C team sends a Teams message before a deal hits a Track C email. That heads-up is the olive branch, not part of the official process. Treat catching it as a daily team workflow.

Why the window is tight

  • The team tracks through the end of shift, then sends the missing-item messages in the last hour.
  • So some land mid-afternoon with a same-day ask. That timing is staffing-driven and isn't changing soon.
  • The heads-up is help, not policy. The real fix is submitting clean so it never fires.

Build the catch into the day

  • Own the watch. Someone on the team checks Teams through the afternoon so a 2:45 message doesn't sit.
  • 30 minutes beats 6 days. Make sure the rep knows a fast turn keeps the deal moving.
  • Same as not-updated leads: a missed heads-up is a process gap on the desk, not a rep failing.
Frame it like the not-updated-leads workflow. If heads-ups are getting missed, that's a cadence problem to solve at the manager desk, not a rep to scold. Build the daily check, assign the watch, and the saves take care of themselves.
Who on your team owns the Teams watch each day? If the answer is "nobody," that's today's fix. Assign it now.
Is the Teams heads-up part of the official Track C process?

AnswerNo. It's an olive branch from the Track C team, sent in the last hour of their shift before a deal would hit a Track C email. The official fix is submitting clean. The heads-up is a save you build a daily cadence to catch.

The escalation path

Process gaps only get fixed when the right people see the pattern. The currency is the example. Collect it, send it up, and the Track C team can train their reviewers and fight the BT tickets.

  1. 1

    Capture the example

    The specific deal, the Track C reason, the date. A real case beats a complaint. "This deal, sub-$1,000 past-due, tracked anyway" is actionable.

  2. 2

    Send it up the chain

    To the Track C team, to Jess, or up your management chain. Jess has said: send the examples, she'll get them resolved and train her people.

  3. 3

    Close the loop

    Track whether it got resolved, and tell the rep when it does. That's what keeps reps reporting instead of quietly absorbing.

Know who you're escalating to. Open the Org Chart at Sales Operations, with the Track C owner's detail open, for the exact owner of Track A/B/C and document integrity, the one-thread Reply-All rule, and the Sales Ops front-door contact.
The Track C team wants the examples. They've said openly they don't always get it right and want the cases so they can train better. Escalating isn't friction. It's how the process improves for the whole division.
Role-play the escalation message to Jess: one deal, one reason, one ask. Keep it to three sentences.
What makes an escalation actionable?

AnswerA specific example: the deal, the Track C reason, the date. Send it to the Track C team or Jess, then close the loop with the rep. Patterns get fixed; complaints don't.

The Manager's Two Jobs

Key takeaways

  • Coach the pre-submit checklist as a habit, every rep, every time.
  • Assign a daily Teams watch so heads-ups don't sit past 2:45.
  • 30 minutes beats 4 to 6 days. Reward the fast save.
  • Escalate the gaps: sub-$1,000 past-due, the BT signature bug, oversize without a bill scan.
  • iPad workaround beats waiting on the Adobe signature bug.
  • Collect the example, send it to the Track C team or Jess, close the loop.
