The Roofing Authority

Make yourself the most credible person on any roof. Know the system, know the warranty, and know what you can read from the ground. Do that, and the customer trusts you before you ever quote a number. This is Michael Slaninka's product knowledge, in his own words.

We Sell a System, Not a Shingle

The other guy sells a shingle off a shelf. We sell an engineered roof where every layer has a job and works with the layer above and below it for the life of the warranty. That frame is the foundation of every roof conversation, so lead with it.

Every layer has a job

Ice-and-water, synthetic underlayment, starter strip, drip edge, plywood decking, ventilation. Each piece is engineered to work with the others. Pull one out and the system stops functioning the way it was designed to.

The competitor sells one part

Most roofers sell you a shingle and call it a roof. They never explain the decking, the underlayment, or the ventilation, because they are not engineering a system, they are covering a hole.

The system is the differentiator

You do not win on the shingle alone. You win because you can walk the customer through the whole assembly and explain why each layer is there. That is what makes you the expert in the room.

The Proof Tools You Carry In

A spec sheet tells. A demo shows. Slaninka built a kit of concrete proofs you can put in a customer's hands, plus cautionary stories that handle the product objections before they harden. Each one gives you something to do or say at the kitchen table.

The shingle pull-test

An IKO Dynasty shingle nailed to a board. Hand it to the customer and ask them to pull it free. They cannot. The armor-zone nailing strip gives Dynasty a 130 mph wind rating, and they feel it in their own hands.

Plywood, not OSB

OSB is ground-up wood chips pressed with glue, the cheapest decking, and it swells when wet. Plywood is laminated full sheets, much stronger and more impact resistant. Trinity uses plywood as standard.

Synthetic, not 30-pound felt

Old tar paper degrades from day one: as it heats and cools, the tar migrates down the roof. Synthetic underlayment breathes, letting moisture exit instead of enter, with the same protection and none of the decay.

The moisture sandwich

When a customer demands ice-and-water on the whole roof, tell the ZZ Top story. One customer overrode the advice, got full ice-and-water, and a year later had condensation, mold, and a ceiling leak. The thing meant to protect them is what hurt them.

Michael Slaninka"You're going to offer the customer the chance to try to pull it off that piece of wood. It's not budging. The customer will be physically unable to pull the shingle free."
Michael Slaninka"When you ice and water the entire roof, you've created a moisture sandwich. The very thing they thought was going to protect them was the thing that hurt them."
Ask the room: a customer insists on full ice-and-water across the whole roof. Walk the moisture-sandwich story live, then land the right answer.
What are the 4 proof tools, and what does each prove?

AnswerThe pull-test (Dynasty armor-zone, 130 mph, the customer cannot pull it free), plywood over OSB (laminated sheets vs glued chips that swell), synthetic underlayment over 30-pound felt (breathes, and tar migrates as felt degrades from day one), and the moisture-sandwich story (full ice-and-water traps moisture and caused mold plus a ceiling leak). Show, do not tell.

Read the Roof From the Ground

You become a roofing expert in the first 2 seconds, before you ever set up. A handful of visible tells let you call out the roof's real condition from the driveway, and the customer instantly trusts that you know what you are looking at.

What you seeWhat it tells youWhat you say
Curled shinglesA hot, poorly ventilated attic baking the roof from below. Bad ventilation can cut a brand new 30-year roof down to 5 to 8 years."Those curled edges tell me the attic is running hot. That heat is aging the roof from the inside."
Fish-mouthing / humpingShingles set too tight, with no room to move. They lift and buckle instead of lying flat."See how those are humping up? Those were laid too tight. That is an install problem, not a weather problem."
Dark fascia stainsDrainage or condensation escaping where it should not. Water is finding a way out the wrong place."Those streaks on the fascia mean water is getting out somewhere it shouldn't. Let's find where."
Shingles on a low slopeA pitch around 5 degrees or less should not carry shingles. Odds are it already leaks and needs rolled rubber or SBS."Shingles on a slope that flat will leak. That section needs rubber, not shingle. Whoever did this got it wrong."
Michael Slaninka"Bad ventilation can take a brand new 30-year roof and cut it down to 5 to 8 years. If the attic is roasting, that roof does not have proper ventilation. There's no question of it. None."
The payoff: calling a defect from the ground earns instant credibility. The customer stops wondering whether you know roofs, and that trust carries the rest of the appointment.

Over, Under, Around, or Through the Wall

Product knowledge makes you credible. Mindset is what carries you when the appointment hits a wall. Slaninka's rule is simple: your first thought decides the outcome, so train the first thought.

The thought that loses

"I failed." The moment that is your first reaction to an obstacle, the outcome is decided. You stop looking for the path and start narrating the loss.

The thought that wins

"How do I get around this?" Adversity becomes an opening for a clever solution. You find a way over, under, around, or through the wall, because you assumed there was one.

Michael Slaninka"If your first thought is 'I failed,' you did, guaranteed. If your first thought is 'how do I fix the problem, how do I get around this,' you'll be successful. Adversity is an opportunity for a clever solution."

Carry these into every roof appointment

  • Lead with the system, not the shingle.
  • Show a proof, do not recite a spec.
  • Call one defect from the ground for instant credibility.
  • Open the hood on the competitor's warranty with facts.
  • Frame the customer as covered twice, one phone call.
  • When you hit a wall, ask how to get around it.
Stay honest. Open the hood on the competitor's coverage with facts and questions. Do not call another company a liar or guarantee a claim outcome. Let the homeowner read their own fine print and reach the conclusion.
Role-play: a roof appointment just stalled on price. Walk the room through finding a way "around the wall" instead of folding.
