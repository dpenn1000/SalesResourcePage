Closer Playbook · Kevin McSorley

The Closing Mechanic

Kevin protects the price, controls the number, and never becomes an order-taker. The mechanic does not race to the bottom. He finds the need, creates the need, makes them feel the pain, then steps in and closes. Here is exactly how he does it.

One Total Number, Then Peel Back

The number you say first sets the whole frame. Lead with one combined number against a future bill they predicted themselves, never a line-item menu of products that lets them back out the add-ons.

Give one total, not a menu

Hold the products together. One combined monthly number is easier to swallow than a list, especially measured against a future bill that does not exist yet.

Write it down and circle it

Have them predict the future bill, then write it where they can see it. Those are their words. Keep pointing back. If they give 2 numbers, write both and circle the higher one.

Throw a price feeler

Tip your toe in the water before the real number. Float a trial figure tied to their own bill and read the room. People do not have a good poker face.

Kevin McSorley"Don't say the battery is $165 and solar is $129. Give the total number. It's a lot easier to swallow, especially compared to a future bill that doesn't even exist yet."
Kevin McSorley"There's a lot of power in writing things down. When you write 350 that they think their bill will be, those are their words. You keep pointing back, and they believe their bill is 350."
Kevin McSorley"Tip your toe in the water. They'll let you know by their body language if that number's acceptable. People don't have a good poker face, they'll tell you right away."
Try this next appointment: ask the customer what they think their bill will be in a few years, write that number on the paper in front of them, circle it, and refer back to it 3 times before you ever say your price.
Ask the room: who line-items their products at the close? What happens to the battery and roof the second you read them as separate prices?
Why give one total number instead of pricing each product?

AnswerOne combined number is easier to swallow and is measured against the customer's own projected future bill, which they spoke and you wrote down. Line items invite them to peel off the add-ons. You also circle the higher bill number they predicted so they fixate on it.

Order-Taker vs Closer

Half of buyers will sign with anyone. The skill is the rest. If you start at price and keep dropping until yes, you gave away the profit before you ever found the real reason they buy.

The order-taker

  • Races to the bottom. Starts with price and keeps lowering it until they hear yes.
  • Gives away the profit on a deal that had nothing to do with money.
  • Closing at 18, 19, 20% and calling it selling.

The closer

  • Find the need. It may be control, hating the utility, a roof, or fear of the future.
  • Create the need, then have them feel the pain of doing nothing.
  • Then step in and close, on the emotion, not on a discount.
Kevin McSorley"If you're closing at 18, 19, 20%, you're just an order taker. We race to the bottom and give away the profit, when it had nothing to do with money. Find the need, create the need, have them feel the pain, then step in and close."
Try this next appointment: before you talk price, name out loud the real need you uncovered (control, the utility, a failing roof, the future), and make them feel the cost of leaving it unsolved. Close on that, not on a lower number.
Ask the room: think of the last deal you discounted. What was the real need, and did you ever actually find it before you dropped the price?
What separates a closer from an order-taker?

AnswerAn order-taker races to the bottom on price and gives away profit on a deal that was never about money. A closer finds the real need, creates it, makes the customer feel the pain of doing nothing, then closes on that emotion. Closing at 18 to 20% is order-taking, not selling.

Slow Is Confidence

The rep who jumps into the iPad 7 minutes in is chasing the commission before earning the sale. Create the problem first. Without a problem, you have nothing to sell.

Create the problem first

Do the walk-around before you ever open the iPad. Look at the roof, the attic, the missing generator. Build the pain. Without a problem you have nothing to sell.

You did not earn it yet

Slow the process down. The longer you are there, the more comfortable they get. Reps fail when they rush to "pay me" before earning the sale.

Make it interactive

Pull everyone into the design. Kids, the mother-in-law, both spouses. It is more fun in the game than watching it, and the room sells with you.

Kevin McSorley"Without a problem you have nothing to sell. Slow the process down. You didn't earn that sale yet. It's a lot more fun when you're in the game than watching the game, so make it interactive for everybody in the house."
Try this next appointment: see how long you can go without opening the iPad. Do the walk-around, build the problem, and get every person in the house into the conversation before a single number appears on a screen.
Ask the room: when "the client didn't have a utility bill" shows up in your notes, was that really logistics, or did you skip building the problem? The bill comes out of the drawer once the pain is real.
Why slow the process down before opening the iPad?

AnswerWithout a problem you have nothing to sell, so you create it first with the walk-around and discovery. Slowing down builds comfort, makes the room interactive, and earns the sale. Jumping to the iPad early is chasing the commission before you have earned it.

The Competitor-Agreement Teardown

When a customer waves a competing offer, do not panic and do not spin. Ask for the agreement, break it down calmly, and you become the professional in the room. The exposed "free roof" inside a PPA is where you separate yourself.

Get the agreement, break it down

  • Ask for a copy and read through it in front of them, line by line.
  • Stay calm, no spin, no defense. You look like the ultimate professional.
  • Now they trust you, and they are never going to cancel.

Expose the "free roof"

  • A roof bundled into a PPA is the cheapest one they can get away with.
  • No separate warranty, no documentation. Nothing in writing that is yours.
  • If it leaks and you go to court, you have nothing to hand a judge.
Kevin McSorley"Not panicking and just breaking it down. Let me get a copy of the agreement, let me look through it. You look like the ultimate professional, and now they're never going to cancel with you."
Kevin McSorley"They're giving you the cheapest roof they can get away with, and you have no documentation. If you have a problem and go to court, you have nothing to give a judge. You put yourself in a really vulnerable position."
Stay honest. Break the agreement down with facts and questions. Do not call the other company a liar or guarantee an outcome. Let the customer read their own paperwork and reach the conclusion that a written, backed product is the safer one.
Role-play: a customer hands you a competitor PPA with a "free roof." Walk the request for the agreement and the documentation question, start to finish, without bashing the other company.
A customer brings a competing offer. What is the move?

AnswerDo not panic or defend. Ask for a copy of the agreement and break it down calmly in front of them, which makes you the professional and kills the urge to cancel. Then expose the "free roof": it is the cheapest roof with no separate warranty or documentation, so if it leaks they have nothing to hand a judge.

Live Proof Beats Any Rebuttal

The fear of bad reviews dies when you show, not tell. Dial your hardest existing customer on speaker, in front of the prospect, and let the truth do the work.

Call the problem-child on speaker

Dial your worst customer live, in the room. She vents, then says she knows you will help, and even hands over a referral. The prospect signs.

A bad review is one rep

Not a reflection of Trinity. It is often one rep who did not get back to a homeowner, and it falls under the shingle of the whole company.

Show up when it goes wrong

That is all the comfort they need. Someone who knows solar and will hold their hand if something breaks beats any 5-star promise on paper.

Kevin McSorley"I called my worst problem child on speaker in front of the homeowner. She vented, then said, Kev, I know you're going to help. And she gave me a referral after all of that. The prospect signed."
Kevin McSorley"A review might not be a reflection of Trinity. It might be one rep who didn't get back to a homeowner, and it just falls under the shingle of Trinity. I'm not afraid to call the problem people, because we're going to help."
Try this next appointment: when reviews come up, do not argue them. Pick a real customer who had a hiccup, call them on speaker, and let the prospect hear you handle it live.
Ask the room: who do you trust enough to dial on speaker in front of a prospect right now? If the answer is nobody, what does that say about your follow-up?
How do you handle a prospect worried about bad reviews?

AnswerShow, do not tell. Call your hardest existing customer on speaker in front of the prospect and let them hear you handle it live, which often earns a referral and the signature. Reframe a bad review as one rep who did not follow up, not the company, and prove you are the rep who shows up.

Be Unique, and Read the Room

When 5 solar companies sound the same, the unique rep wins. Land the whale without skipping your process, and change your angle the moment one stalls.

Be unique (the Pollock model)

Stand out, say the off-the-wall thing. A top closer tells people he is too busy to come back. Be a little goofy, take a side, and be different from the other 4.

Land the whale, keep the process

The layup comes a handful of times a month. Skip half your process to sign it fast and you leave the add-ons, battery, and referrals on the table.

Change tactics when one stalls

The clue is usually there. Kevin closed a 5,300 sq ft roof only after taking the wife outside, where she mentioned a winter ice leak. Do not step over the opening.

Kevin McSorley"If you don't sign, I'm never coming back, I'm too busy. He says off-the-wall things and people eat it up. Be unique, identify yourself as different from the other four, and stand out."
Kevin McSorley"You only get a handful of those deals a month. You have to land the whale. You can't skip the process. When you feel you have a layup and you skip half your process just to sign it because it's done, you leave it on the table."
Kevin McSorley"Sometimes a rep gets the clue and steps right over it, not realizing that was the opening. She said we had a little leak in that area in winter. That was my entry point. That was where I was going to die on that hill."
Try this next appointment: on your easiest deal, run your full process anyway. And when one angle stalls, change the setting (step outside, take a walk) to surface the clue the customer has not said yet.
Ask the room: what makes you different from the other 4 reps this homeowner will talk to? Name it in one sentence you would actually say at the table.
What 3 habits keep a closer out of order-taker mode here?

AnswerBe unique so you stand out from the other reps, land the whale by running your full process even on layups so you do not leave add-ons and referrals on the table, and change your angle or setting when one stalls to surface the clue (like the winter ice leak that closed a 5,300 sq ft roof).
