Understanding Title Work

Know who owns the home before anyone signs. Match the deed to the signatures and the deal funds clean. Miss it and you stall at processing, or at install. You can confirm ownership from your phone before you knock.

Who Owns What, and Why You Care

A solar agreement binds one property. The people who sign must legally own it. Deed names and signatures have to match, or the deal cannot fund.

Ownership got complicated

More homes sit in an LLC or Trust, have multiple owners, or just changed hands by divorce, inheritance, or sale. Each one changes who signs.

Wrong signer = stall

Miss a co-owner and processing kicks it back. Catch it in the living room: two-minute fix. Catch it at install: weeks lost.

You can verify it

Ownership is public record. Don't take their word for it. Confirm who's on title before you knock.

Why does a co-owner who isn't on the loan still matter?

AnswerThe deed decides the signer, not the loan. Anyone on the deed must sign the agreement, even if they never borrowed a dime.

Deeds, Owners, and the Words That Matter

You don't need to be a title attorney. Recognize a few terms and you can read a property record and know who signs.

How ownership transfers

  • Grantor gives, grantee gets, through a deed.
  • Warranty Deed: the strong one. Seller guarantees clear ownership. Most home purchases.
  • Quitclaim Deed: transfers whatever interest exists, no guarantees. Common in family transfers, divorce, and moving a home into a Trust or LLC. Look closer when you see one.

Owner vs. borrower

  • The note is the loan; the mortgage is what's recorded against the property.
  • All owners are on the mortgage, but not all owners are borrowers.
  • The deed decides the signer. Being on the loan does not make someone a signer. Being on the deed does.

Where the record lives

Deeds get recorded with the town or city clerk and assigned a Book and Page number, the document's address. Give that number to anyone and they can pull the exact deed. That's why ownership is something you verify, not just ask about.

You see a recent Quitclaim Deed. What does it tell you?

AnswerOwnership likely changed outside a normal sale, a divorce, a family transfer, or a move into a Trust/LLC. Confirm who's actually on title now before assuming.

Confirm Ownership From Your Phone

The most useful habit in this guide. Two minutes before an appointment, look the property up. Google the town name plus any source below.

  1. Vision Appraisal

    The assessor's database. Owner name, property details, sale history. Fastest first look.

    "[Town] Vision Appraisal"

  2. Town Clerk

    The land records. Deeds, mortgages, Book/Page numbers. Confirm the actual deed and how it's held.

    "[Town] Town Clerk"

  3. My Tax Bill

    Who's taxed for it. Quick cross-check on the name and that taxes are current.

    "[Town] My Tax Bill"

  4. Town GIS

    The mapping system. Owner, parcel, satellite view. Great way to confirm the right home before you go.

    "[Town] GIS"
The two-minute habit: pull the property, note every name on title, and check whether the owner is a person or an entity (you'll see "Smith Family Trust" or "123 Main LLC"). Now you walk in knowing who signs and what to ask for.
Pick a real upcoming appointment. Look it up live on the screen right now. Who's on title?
Name two sources to confirm an owner, and what each adds.

AnswerAny two: Vision Appraisal (fast owner + history), Town Clerk (the actual deed + Book/Page), My Tax Bill (name cross-check), Town GIS (parcel + confirm the right house).

When the Home Is Held in an LLC

The owner on title is a company, not a person. The deed alone isn't enough. You need proof of who can sign for the company, and it isn't at the town.

What's different

  • The deed shows the LLC owns the home, not the people living there.
  • A person with authority signs for it, usually a managing member.
  • Proof isn't recorded at the town. It's in the formation papers.

What you collect

The deed plus the LLC's formation documents (Articles of Organization, operating agreement if any) showing who's authorized. Get them from the homeowner, not the clerk. Ask early.

Rep · asking for the docs"Looks like the home's in an LLC, totally common. To set this up clean, I'll need a copy of the LLC's formation paperwork so the right person signs. Can you dig that out before we meet?"
The home is in an LLC. Who signs, and what do you need?

AnswerAn authorized member (usually the managing member) signs, plus the LLC's formation documents proving that authority. You get those from the homeowner.

When the Home Is Held in a Trust

Common with estate planning. Same idea as an LLC: the Trust owns the home, and you need its paperwork to know who signs.

What's different

  • The deed names a trust, often the "Smith Family Trust."
  • The trustee signs. Often the homeowner in front of you, but confirm it.
  • Trust documents aren't recorded at the town. Get them from the homeowner.

What you collect

The deed plus the trust documents naming the trustee and their powers. Treat it gently. This paperwork can tie to a loss or to aging, so ask with care.

Rep · asking for the docs"I see the home's in a family trust, smart planning. To make sure we have the right signer, I'll need a copy of the trust paperwork at some point. No rush tonight."
You don't read these documents. You collect them and hand them to the title team. Know one is needed and ask for it without making it awkward.
Who signs for a Trust, and how is that different from an LLC?

AnswerThe named trustee signs (you need the trust documents). An LLC needs an authorized member plus formation papers. Both sets of documents come from the homeowner, not the town.

Pre-appointment title checklist

  • Looked up the property; noted every name on title.
  • Confirmed owner is a person or an entity (LLC / Trust).
  • If an entity, asked for the formation or trust documents.
  • Confirmed all deeded owners will sign, or made a plan.
  • Checked for recent changes: Quitclaim, divorce, passing, sale.
  • Condo/HOA? Confirmation form ready.
  • Flagged anything unusual to the title team.

The Field Manual

Key Takeaways

  • Everyone on the deed signs. That one rule prevents most title problems.
  • The deed decides the signer, not the loan.
  • Ownership is public record. Confirm it yourself: Vision Appraisal, Town Clerk, My Tax Bill, GIS.
  • LLC or Trust means extra documents, and they come from the homeowner.
  • Trust = trustee signs. LLC = authorized member signs. Confirm who.
  • You gather and flag. The title team rules on hard cases.
  • Catch it in the living room, not at install.
