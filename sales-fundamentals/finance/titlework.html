Understanding Title Work

Part 1 · The Frame

Who Owns What, and Why You Care

A solar agreement is a contract tied to a specific property. The people who sign it have to be the people who legally own that property. If the name on the deed and the name on the agreement don't line up, the deal cannot fund. Title work is simply making sure those names match before you get there.

"Everyone on the deed signs the agreement. Not most of them. Not whoever's home. Everyone." The One Rule That Prevents Most Title Problems

Why title trips deals up

  • Ownership has gotten complicated. More homes than ever are held in an LLC or a Trust, owned by multiple people, or recently changed hands through a divorce, an inheritance, or a sale. Each of those changes who has to sign.
  • The wrong signer means a stall. If a co-owner isn't on the agreement, processing kicks it back. Catch it in the living room and it's a two-minute fix. Catch it at install and you've burned weeks and goodwill.
  • You can check it yourself. Property ownership is public record. You do not have to take the customer's word for who owns the home. You can confirm it before you knock.
Your lane: gather and flag, don't rule. You are confirming who is on title and collecting the right documents. You are not giving legal advice and you are not deciding whether a Trust or an estate "counts." When anything looks unusual, get it to the title / quotes team and let them make the call. That is what keeps a complex deal clean.
Part 2 · The Vocabulary

Deeds, Owners, and the Words That Matter

You don't need to be a title attorney. You need to recognize a handful of terms so you can read a property record and know who has to sign. Here's the whole vocabulary in plain English.

How ownership transfers

  • The grantor gives the property. The grantee receives it. The transfer happens through a deed.
  • A Warranty Deed is the strong one: the seller guarantees clear ownership. Most normal home purchases use this.
  • A Quitclaim Deed just transfers whatever interest the person had, with no guarantees. Common between family, in divorces, and when moving a home into a Trust or LLC. When you see one, look closer.

Owner vs. borrower (don't mix these up)

  • To buy, most people borrow money. The loan itself is the note. The mortgage is what's recorded against the property as security.
  • Key distinction: all owners must be on the mortgage, but not all owners are borrowers. Someone can be on the deed without being on the loan.
  • For us, the deed is what matters. Being on the loan does not make someone a signer. Being on the deed does.

Where the record lives

When a home changes hands, the deed is almost always recorded with the town or city clerk. There's a recording fee, and the document is added to the town's land records and assigned a Book and Page number. That Book/Page is the address of the document: give it to anyone and they can pull the exact deed. This is why ownership is something you can verify, not just ask about.

Part 3 · The Field Skill

Confirm Ownership Yourself, From Your Phone

This is the most useful habit in this whole guide. Before an appointment, take two minutes and look the property up. You'll know who's on title, whether it's held in an entity, and even confirm you're going to the right house. Google the town name plus any of these four sources.

Vision Appraisal

The assessor's database. Owner name, property characteristics, and sale history at a glance. Usually the fastest first look.

"[Town] Vision Appraisal"

Town Clerk

The land records themselves: deeds, mortgages, and the Book/Page numbers. This is where you confirm the actual deed and how it's held.

"[Town] Town Clerk"

My Tax Bill

Who the property is taxed to. A quick cross-check on the name and that taxes are current.

"[Town] My Tax Bill"

Town GIS

The mapping system. Owner and parcel info, plus a satellite/parcel view. A great way to confirm you have the right home before you go.

"[Town] GIS"
Two-minute pre-appointment habit: pull the property up, note every name on title, and check whether the owner is a person or an entity (you'll see something like "Smith Family Trust" or "123 Main LLC"). Now you walk in already knowing who needs to sign and what to ask for. That is a professional, and customers feel it.
Part 4 · The Entity Cases

When the Home Is Held in an LLC

Sometimes the owner on title isn't a person, it's a company. That's fine, it just means the deed alone isn't enough. You need to prove who is allowed to sign on the company's behalf, and that proof isn't at the town.

What's different

  • The deed shows the property is owned by the LLC, not by the people living there.
  • A company can't pick up a pen. A person with authority in that LLC has to sign for it, usually a managing member.
  • Proof of that authority lives in the company's formation papers, which are not recorded at the town.

What you need to collect

You will need the deed showing the LLC owns the home, plus the LLC's formation documents (Articles of Organization / Incorporation, and an operating agreement if there is one) showing who is authorized to act for the company.

You get these from the homeowner, not from the clerk. So ask for them early, before the appointment if you can.

Rep · asking for the docs "Looks like the home's held in an LLC, which is totally common. To set everything up clean on the back end, I'll just need a copy of the LLC's formation paperwork so we have the right person signing. Can you dig that out before we meet?"
Part 5 · The Entity Cases

When the Home Is Held in a Trust

Trusts are extremely common, especially with older homeowners doing estate planning. Same idea as an LLC: the deed shows the Trust owns the home, and you need the Trust's own paperwork to know who's allowed to sign for it.

What's different

  • The deed shows the property is held by a trust, often named for the family (the "Smith Family Trust").
  • The person who signs for a trust is the trustee. That's frequently the homeowner sitting in front of you, but not always, so confirm it.
  • As with an LLC, the trust's documents are not recorded at the town. You get them from the homeowner.

What you need to collect

The deed showing the trust holds the home, plus the trust documents that name the trustee and the powers they have. The title team uses those to confirm the right person is signing.

Treat this gently. Trust and estate paperwork can be tied to a loss or to aging, so ask with care.

Rep · asking for the docs "I see the home's in a family trust, which is smart planning. To make sure we have the right signer, I'll just need a copy of the trust paperwork at some point. No rush tonight, whenever you can find it."
You don't have to read these documents. You're not interpreting a trust or an operating agreement. You're collecting them and handing them to the title team. Your job is to know one is needed and to ask for it without making it awkward.
Part 6 · Edge Cases & the Checklist

Other Snags, and What Each One Needs

Beyond LLCs and Trusts, a handful of situations change who signs or what's required. You don't have to solve these, just recognize them and flag them with the right document so the title team can finish the job.

SituationWho signs / what's neededFlag it because
Multiple owners on the deed Every owner on title signs the agreement. Missing a co-owner's signature is the single most common reason a deal kicks back.
An owner has passed away Depends on the estate / probate. Title team determines the legal signer. You cannot just have the surviving spouse sign and assume it's fine. Flag it.
Recent divorce Whoever the deed says owns it now. Look for a recent Quitclaim Deed. The deed may not have been updated yet. Confirm before assuming the ex is off title.
Condo or HOA The HOA Confirmation of Ownership and Access form, signed. The association controls access and approvals; we need it documented up front.
Held in an LLC Authorized member signs + LLC formation documents. Proof of signing authority isn't public record. Collect it from the owner.
Held in a Trust Trustee signs + trust documents. The signer must be the named trustee, and the docs prove it.

The pre-appointment title checklist

Two minutes before you go. Run it every time and title stops being a surprise.

  • Looked the property up (Vision Appraisal / Town Clerk / GIS) and noted every name on title.
  • Confirmed whether the owner is a person or an entity (LLC / Trust).
  • If an entity, asked the homeowner to gather the formation or trust documents.
  • Confirmed all deeded owners will be present to sign, or made a plan for the one who isn't.
  • Checked for recent changes: a Quitclaim Deed, a divorce, a passing, a sale.
  • Condo or HOA? Have the HOA Confirmation form ready.
  • Anything unusual flagged to the title / quotes team rather than guessed at.
