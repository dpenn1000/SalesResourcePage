Understanding Title Work

The agreement needs one person on title who passes credit. Not every owner, just one who qualifies. Confirm who's on title from your phone before you knock, and get the signer right so the deal funds clean.

Who Owns What, and Why You Care

A solar agreement needs at least one person who is on title, and the signers have to pass credit. You don't need every owner, just one who qualifies, or the deal cannot fund.

One qualified signer is enough

One person on title who also passes credit can carry the whole agreement. You don't need every owner to sign.

The relative exception

If the credit-passer isn't on title, that's allowed only when they're a direct relative living in the home. Then one title-holder must also be on the agreement.

Always one title-holder

At least one signer must be on the deed. That part never bends. Confirm who's on title before you knock, it's public record.

Deeds, Owners, and the Words That Matter

You don't need to be a title attorney. Recognize a few terms and you can read a property record and know who signs.

How ownership transfers

  • Grantor gives, grantee gets, through a deed.
  • Warranty Deed: the strong one. Seller guarantees clear ownership. Most home purchases.
  • Quitclaim Deed: transfers whatever interest exists, no guarantees. Common in family, divorce, and Trust/LLC moves. Look closer.

Owner vs. borrower

  • The note is the loan; the mortgage is recorded against the property.
  • All owners are on the mortgage, but not all owners are borrowers.
  • Title decides who can sign, not the loan. The required title-signer has to be on the deed, not just the mortgage.

Where the record lives

  • Deeds get recorded with the town clerk.
  • Each gets a Book and Page number, the document's address.
  • Anyone can pull it, so ownership is something you verify, not just ask about.

Confirm Ownership From Your Phone

The most useful habit in this guide. Two minutes before an appointment, look the property up. Google the town name plus any source below.

  1. Vision Appraisal

    The assessor's database. Owner name, property details, sale history. Fastest first look.

    "[Town] Vision Appraisal"

  2. Town Clerk

    The land records. Deeds, mortgages, Book/Page numbers. Confirm the deed and how it's held.

    "[Town] Town Clerk"

  3. My Tax Bill

    Who's taxed for it. Quick cross-check on the name and that taxes are current.

    "[Town] My Tax Bill"

  4. Town GIS

    The mapping system. Owner, parcel, satellite view. Confirm the right home before you go.

    "[Town] GIS"
The two-minute habit: note every name on title and check whether the owner is a person or an entity ("Smith Family Trust" or "123 Main LLC"). Now you walk in knowing who signs.
Pick a real upcoming appointment. Look it up live right now. Who's on title?
Name two sources to confirm an owner, and what each adds.

AnswerAny two: Vision Appraisal (fast owner + history), Town Clerk (the deed + Book/Page), My Tax Bill (name cross-check), Town GIS (parcel + confirm the right house).

When the Home Is Held in an LLC

The owner on title is a company, not a person. The deed alone isn't enough. You need proof of who can sign for the company, and it isn't at the town.

What's different

  • The deed shows the LLC owns the home, not the people living there.
  • A person with authority signs, usually a managing member.
  • Proof isn't recorded at the town. It's in the formation papers.

What you collect

  • The deed showing the LLC owns the home.
  • Formation documents (Articles of Organization, operating agreement) showing who's authorized.
  • From the homeowner, not the clerk. Ask early.

The home is in an LLC. Who signs, and what do you need?

AnswerAn authorized member (usually the managing member) signs, plus the LLC's formation documents proving that authority. Get those from the homeowner.

When the Home Is Held in a Trust

Common with estate planning. Same idea as an LLC: the Trust owns the home, and you need its paperwork to know who signs.

What's different

  • The deed names a trust, often the "Smith Family Trust."
  • The trustee signs. Often the homeowner in front of you, but confirm it.
  • Trust documents aren't recorded at the town. Get them from the homeowner.

What you collect

  • The deed showing the trust holds the home.
  • Trust documents naming the trustee and their powers.
  • Ask with care. This paperwork can tie to a loss or to aging.
Rep · asking for the docs"I see the home's in a family trust, smart planning. To make sure we have the right signer, I'll need a copy of the trust paperwork at some point. No rush tonight."
A widow tells you the home is in her late husband's trust. What's your next move?
Who signs for a Trust, and how is that different from an LLC?

AnswerThe named trustee signs (you need the trust documents). An LLC needs an authorized member plus formation papers. Both come from the homeowner, not the town.

Pre-appointment title checklist

  • Looked up the property; noted every name on title.
  • Confirmed owner is a person or an entity.
  • If an entity, asked for the formation or trust documents.
  • Confirmed one title-holder who passes credit will sign (or a relative-in-home credit-passer plus one title owner).
  • Checked for recent changes: Quitclaim, divorce, passing, sale.
  • Condo/HOA? Confirmation form ready.
  • Flagged anything unusual to the title team.

The Field Manual

Key Takeaways

  • One title-holder who passes credit can carry the agreement. You don't need every owner.
  • If the credit-passer isn't on title, they must be a direct relative in the home, and one title owner also signs.
  • Confirm ownership yourself: Vision Appraisal, Town Clerk, My Tax Bill, GIS.
  • LLC or Trust means extra documents, and they come from the homeowner.
  • Trust = trustee signs. LLC = authorized member signs. Confirm who.
  • You gather and flag. The title team rules on hard cases.
