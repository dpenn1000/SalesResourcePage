The Credit Conversation

Sometimes credit doesn't approve, and you find out at the kitchen table. The finance company won't soften it. You deliver the news. The next ninety seconds decide whether the customer feels judged or understood, and whether they stay on your team.

Why This Moment Is Yours

Credit is tied to a person's sense of worth. When it comes back short, they don't hear "the application didn't approve." They hear "you're not good enough." Your whole job is to separate the person from the number.

It's on you to deliver it

The finance company won't call and explain in the moment. You see the result, the person reads your face. Stay calm, your reaction sets theirs.

Separate person from number

A credit result is one lender's formula, not a verdict on their character or future. Say it out loud, they won't believe it on their own.

Embarrassment is the obstacle

If they feel ashamed, they shut down and the appointment is over. If they feel understood, most paths stay open.

The compliance line, know it cold. You deliver the outcome with empathy. You do not invent or recite the legal reasons for the decline. By law (ECOA / FCRA), the lender issues the formal adverse-action notice with the actual reasons, afterward. Never guess "it's probably your car loan" or read numbers off your screen. This protects the customer and you.
Rep facts (LightReach, solar). Minimum FICO is 650. It's a soft pull, so it never dings their credit. LightReach pulls Equifax and TransUnion (not Experian). You can re-run daily, but if they're near 650, wait about 30 days, the bureaus update monthly. These are for your head, not numbers to read to the customer.
Have a rep deliver a decline to the room, cold. Where did their tone or wording lose the customer?
What can you tell the customer about why they didn't approve?

AnswerThe outcome and the path forward, with empathy. Not the specific legal reasons, those come from the lender's adverse-action notice, by law. Never guess.

Nobody Has One Credit Score. They Have About Sixteen.

Teach this before you ever need it. There are 16 FICO versions in active use, each at all three bureaus. Add the VantageScores the free apps show, and one person carries 16 to 20+ scores at once. All real, all different, built for different purposes.

Base

FICO 8, 9, 10, 10T

Everyday general-purpose scores. FICO 8 is most common; newer ones treat paid collections more gently.

Mortgage

FICO 2, 4, 5

The old "classic" versions still required for home loans. One per bureau: 2 (Experian), 4 (TransUnion), 5 (Equifax).

Auto

FICO Auto 8 & 9

Tuned for car loans. A dealership pulls one of these, not the number on the customer's app.

Bankcard

FICO Bankcard 8 & 9

Tuned for credit cards. Same person, same day, different model, different result.

The takeaway to hand them: different industries pull different models on purpose. None is "the real one." So when their app and our number don't match, that's normal, not a sign anyone did anything wrong.
A customer asks "so what's my real score?" What do you say?

AnswerThere isn't one. They have around sixteen, each built for a different purpose. The only one that matters here is the one the finance company uses.

"But My Credit Karma Says 720"

The hardest version of the conversation. They show you a high number and now they feel wronged. Here's the mechanic, and here's where you put the frustration on the system, never on them.

Why their app looks higher

  • It's a different score. Free apps show a VantageScore, not a FICO. Two companies, two formulas, built to disagree.
  • It runs higher. VantageScore reads 5 to 15 points above the comparable FICO, sometimes 20 to 80.
  • It's softer. VantageScore forgives paid collections and recent inquiries; the lender's FICO leans on payment history and account age.
  • It's the model, not the bureau. LightReach pulls Equifax and TransUnion, the same bureaus Credit Karma uses, so the gap is FICO vs VantageScore.

Put the frustration on the system

You're not defending the lender or correcting the customer. You're agreeing the system is genuinely confusing, and standing next to them against it.

Rep"Honestly, this part of the system is a mess, and you're right to be annoyed. The score on your app is real, but it's the friendly version. The finance company uses a stricter model built just for approvals. You were looking at a real number, just not the one that makes this decision."
Do not argue the number. Never call their app "wrong" or "fake." It isn't. Validate that it's real, then explain it's simply not the one being used here. Correct their number and you're on opposite sides.
The customer is angry their 720 didn't qualify. Your move?

AnswerAgree the system is confusing and stand next to them. Their number is real, it's the friendly VantageScore, not the FICO the lender uses. Never tell them their app is fake.

The Live Script: Feel, Felt, Found

The centerpiece. Deliver the short result in real time with Feel, Felt, Found, genuine empathy in three beats. Slow down, warm tone, eyes on the person and not the screen.

  1. 1

    Feel

    Acknowledge it honestly. Don't rush past it.

    "Okay, so the approval didn't come back the way we wanted today. I know how that feels, and it's more common than you'd think."

  2. 2

    Felt

    They're not alone. You've sat with this moment before.

    "I've had plenty of homeowners sit right where you are and feel embarrassed, with nothing to be embarrassed about. The system surprises good people all the time."

  3. 3

    Found

    The turn. Others in this spot got there.

    "Here's what I've found though. A lot of those same folks qualified down the road once we knew what to aim for. This isn't a no. It's a not-yet, and I'll show you what moves it."

Put together, out loud, it sounds like this

Rep · the full beat"So the approval didn't land where we wanted today. I want you to hear me on this: that number is not a grade on you as a person. I've had good, hardworking people sit right where you are and feel embarrassed, with no reason to be. The credit system is strict and confusing, and it weighs recent stuff heavier than it should. Here's the part that matters. A lot of those same folks ended up qualifying once we knew what the formula wanted to see. So I'm not closing the door on you tonight. Let me show you what we're working with and the simplest path to a yes."
Each rep delivers the full Feel-Felt-Found beat to the room. Coach the pace and the eye contact.
What three beats carry the live delivery?

AnswerFeel (you understand), Felt (they're not alone), Found (others here qualified later). Empathy, the system as the obstacle, and a path. No denial reasons, no blame.

Say This, Not That

Wording matters more here than almost anywhere in the appointment. The wrong phrase reads as judgment and shuts the person down. The right phrase keeps their dignity. Memorize the left column.

The MomentSay ThisNever Say This
Before the pull "Heads-up so nothing surprises you: the finance company uses its own scoring model, and it almost never matches the app on your phone. That's normal. We're not grading you, and it's a soft check, so it won't ding your credit." "Let's run your credit and see if you even qualify."
Delivering the result "The approval didn't come back the way we wanted today, and that's not a reflection of you. Let me walk you through what's going on." "Yeah, you got denied." / "Your credit's too low."
They look embarrassed "You have nothing to be embarrassed about. I see this with great people all the time. The system is strict, not you." "It's fine, don't worry about it." (dismissive, moves on too fast)
They show a higher app score "That number is real, it's just the friendly version. The lender uses a stricter model built for approvals. You weren't wrong, you were looking at a different score." "That app is fake / wrong / doesn't mean anything."
They ask why "The finance company will send the official details on what factored in. What I can do now is show you the things that usually move it most." "It's probably your credit cards / that collection / your car loan." (guessing)
They get frustrated "I'd be frustrated too. This part of the system is genuinely confusing, and you're right to be annoyed. Let's beat it together." "Calm down, there's nothing I can do." / "That's just how it is."
Closing the moment "This is a not-yet, not a no. Here's the path, and I'll be here when you're ready to take it." "Well, maybe try again sometime." (leaves them stranded)
The tone rule underneath all of it: never read the result like a diagnosis, never say "denied," never imply the free app tricked them. Slow down, keep your voice even, keep your eyes up.
Drill the table: a manager reads a "never say," the rep fires back the matching "say this."

Never Leave Them at "No"

The difference between a crushed customer and a future sale is whether they leave with a path. Be honest tonight didn't approve, and in the same breath give them something to do and a reason to believe. Most credit results are a snapshot, not a sentence.

"I've gotten others qualified later"

Say it with conviction, it's true. Many who don't approve on the first pull approve later once a few things shift. Frame it as a team effort with a finish line.

Rep"I've had homeowners who didn't approve the first time go solar a few months later. We just lined up a couple of things. If you're willing, I'd like to be the one who helps you get there."

Things that commonly move it

  • Pay a card below about 30% of its limit.
  • Let a recent hard inquiry age out.
  • Clear or settle one specific item.
  • Add an immediate-family co-applicant (LightReach: needn't live in the home; the title-holder still runs credit).
  • Wait about 30 days before re-checking; bureaus update monthly.

Lock the follow-up before you leave

A path with no date is just a let-down with extra steps. Set a specific time to reconnect, and make it easy to say yes to that even when they had to say not-yet to solar.

Rep"Let's do this. I'll check back in about 30 days. Watch for that letter from the finance company, it spells out the specifics. You take care of the one or two things we talked about, and let's get you approved next round."
The decline lands. What two things must they leave with?

AnswerA specific path (one or two levers they can act on) and a dated follow-up. A path with no date is just a let-down.

The Field Manual

Key Takeaways

  • In the home, you deliver the news. Your calm is the customer's calm.
  • Separate the person from the number. It's one formula's math, not a verdict on their worth.
  • Nobody has one score. They have about sixteen, all real, all different.
  • When their app reads higher, agree the system is confusing and stand with them. Never call their number fake.
  • Feel, Felt, Found: you understand, they're not alone, others here qualified later.
  • Deliver the outcome, never the specific reasons. The lender's notice does that, by law.
  • Never leave them at "no." A path plus a date turns a hard night into a future yes.
