Finance · The Hard Conversation

The Credit Conversation

Sometimes the credit doesn't approve, and you find out while you are sitting at the kitchen table. The finance company won't soften it for you. In that moment, you are the one who delivers the news.

How you handle the next ninety seconds decides whether the customer feels judged and embarrassed, or understood and still on your team. This is how you carry someone through it with their dignity intact.

Part 1 · The Frame

Why This Moment Is Hard, and Why It's Yours

Money and credit are tied to a person's sense of worth. When credit comes back short, people don't hear "the application didn't approve." They hear "you're not good enough." Your whole job in that moment is to separate the person from the number.

"The number didn't say anything about you. It said something about a formula that wasn't built for your situation." The Reframe That Carries Everything

Three things to hold in your head

  • It is on you to deliver it. In the in-home process, the finance company does not call the customer and explain anything in the moment. You see the result on your screen, and the person is sitting right there waiting on your face. Stay calm. Your reaction sets theirs.
  • Separate the person from the number. A credit result is a math output from one specific lender's formula. It is not a verdict on their character, their effort, or their future. Say that out loud, because they will not believe it on their own.
  • Embarrassment is the real obstacle, not the credit. If they feel ashamed, they shut down and the appointment is over. If they feel understood, they stay in the conversation and most paths stay open.
The compliance line, know it cold. You deliver the outcome with empathy. You do not invent or recite the specific legal reasons the application didn't approve. By law (ECOA / FCRA), the finance company is the one that issues the formal "adverse action" notice listing the actual reasons, and it comes to the customer afterward. So never guess "it's probably your car loan" or read numbers off your screen like a diagnosis. Deliver the news, point them to the path, and let the lender's notice handle the specifics. This protects the customer and it protects you.
Part 2 · The Education

Nobody Has One Credit Score. They Have About Sixteen.

This single fact defuses most of the tension, so teach it before you ever need it. A person is not walking around with "a credit score." There are 16 different FICO versions in active use today, and each one exists separately at all three bureaus. Add the VantageScore models the free apps show, and one person is realistically carrying 16 to 20+ different scores at once. All real. All different. Built for different purposes.

Base

FICO 8, 9, 10, 10T

The everyday general-purpose scores. FICO 8 is the most common. The newer ones treat things like paid collections more gently.

Mortgage

FICO 2, 4, 5

The older "classic" versions lenders are still required to use for home loans. One per bureau: 2 (Experian), 4 (TransUnion), 5 (Equifax).

Auto

FICO Auto Score 8 & 9

Tuned for car loans. A dealership pulls one of these, not the number on the customer's phone app.

Bankcard

FICO Bankcard 8 & 9

Tuned for credit cards. Different industry, different model, different result for the same person on the same day.

The takeaway to hand them: different industries pull different models on purpose. A mortgage uses one, a car dealer uses another, a credit card uses a third, and the finance company on a solar agreement uses its own. None of them is "the real one." So when the number on their app and the number we see don't match, that is normal and expected, not a sign anyone did anything wrong.
Part 3 · The Upset Moment

"But My Credit Karma Says 720"

This is the hardest version of the conversation. The customer pulls out their phone, shows you a high number, and now they are not just disappointed, they feel wronged. Here is the honest mechanic, and here is where you put the frustration where it belongs: on the complexity of the credit system, never on them.

Why their app looks higher

  • It's a different score entirely. Credit Karma and most free apps show a VantageScore, not a FICO. Two different companies, two different formulas, built to disagree.
  • It usually runs higher. A VantageScore typically reads 5 to 15 points above the comparable FICO, and gaps of 20 to 80 points happen.
  • It weighs things differently. VantageScore is easier on paid collections and recent inquiries. The lender's FICO leans harder on payment history and account age.
  • It may be a different bureau. The free apps usually pull Equifax and TransUnion. The lender may pull a different file. No two bureau files are identical.

Put the frustration on the system

When they are upset about the higher number, you are not defending the lender and you are not correcting the customer. You are agreeing that the system is genuinely confusing, and standing next to them against it.

Rep "Honestly, this part of the system is a mess, and you are right to be annoyed by it. The score on your app is real, but it is the friendly version. The finance company uses a stricter model built just for approvals. You were looking at a real number. It just wasn't the one that makes this decision."

You just turned a fight with you into a shared frustration at the system. That is the whole move.

Do not argue the number. Never tell them their app is "wrong" or "fake." It is not. The instant you correct their number, you are on opposite sides. Validate that the number is real, then explain that it is simply not the one being used here.
Part 4 · The Delivery

The Live Script: Feel, Felt, Found

This is the centerpiece. When the result comes back short, you deliver it in real time using Feel, Felt, Found. It works because it is not a tactic, it is genuine empathy in three beats: you understand, they are not alone, and there is a way forward. Slow down. Warm tone. Look up from the screen and at the person.

1

Feel

Acknowledge it honestly. Do not rush past it.

"Okay, so the approval didn't come back the way we wanted today. I know how that feels, and I want you to know it is more common than you'd think."

2

Felt

They are not alone. You have sat with this exact moment before.

"I've had plenty of homeowners sit right where you are and feel embarrassed about it, and they had nothing to be embarrassed about. The system is strict and it surprises good people all the time."

3

Found

The turn. Others in this spot got there eventually.

"Here is what I've found though. A lot of those same folks qualified a little down the road once we knew what to aim for. So this isn't a no. It's a not-yet, and I'll show you what moves it."

Put together, out loud, it sounds like this

Rep · the full beat "So the approval didn't land where we wanted today. I want you to hear me on this: that number is not a grade on you as a person. I've had good people, hardworking people, sit right where you are and feel embarrassed, and they had no reason to be. The credit system is strict and confusing, and it weighs recent stuff heavier than it probably should. Here is the part that matters. A lot of those same folks ended up qualifying once we knew exactly what the formula wanted to see. So I am not closing the door on you tonight. Let me show you what we are actually working with and the simplest path to a yes."

Notice what is missing: no specific denial reasons, no numbers read off the screen, no blame. Just empathy, the system as the obstacle, and a path.

Part 5 · The Words

Say This, Not That

The exact wording matters more here than almost anywhere else in the appointment. The wrong phrase reads as judgment and shuts the person down. The right phrase keeps their dignity and keeps them in the conversation. Memorize the left column.

The Moment Say This Never Say This
Before the pull "Quick heads-up so nothing catches you off guard: the finance company uses its own scoring model, and it almost never matches the app on your phone. That's normal. We're not grading you." "Let's run your credit and see if you even qualify."
Delivering the result "The approval didn't come back the way we wanted today, and that's not a reflection of you. Let me walk you through what's going on." "Yeah, you got denied." / "Your credit's too low."
They look embarrassed "You have nothing to be embarrassed about. I see this with great people all the time. The system is strict, not you." "It's fine, don't worry about it." (dismissive, moves on too fast)
They show a higher app score "That number is real, it's just the friendly version. The lender uses a stricter model built for approvals. You weren't wrong, you were looking at a different score." "That app is fake / wrong / doesn't mean anything."
They ask why "The finance company will send you the official details on what factored in. What I can do right now is show you the things that usually move it most." "It's probably your credit cards / that collection / your car loan." (guessing reasons)
They get frustrated "I'd be frustrated too. This part of the system is genuinely confusing, and you're right to be annoyed by it. Let's beat it together." "Calm down, there's nothing I can do." / "That's just how it is."
Closing the moment "This is a not-yet, not a no. Here's the path, and I'll be here when you're ready to take it." "Well, maybe try again sometime." (leaves them stranded)
The tone rule underneath all of it: never read the result like a diagnosis, never say the word "denied," never imply the free app tricked them. Slow down, keep your voice even, and keep your eyes on the person and not the screen.
Part 6 · The Turn

Never Leave Them at "No"

The difference between a crushed customer and a future sale is whether they leave the table with a path. You are honest that tonight didn't approve, and in the same breath you give them something to do and a reason to believe it can change. Most credit results are a snapshot, not a sentence.

"I've gotten others qualified later"

This is true, so say it with conviction. Many people who don't approve on the first pull approve later once a few things shift. Frame it as a team effort with a finish line.

Rep "I've had homeowners who didn't approve the first time end up going solar a few months later. We just had to get a couple of things lined up. If you're willing, I'd like to be the guy who helps you get there."

Things that commonly move it

Keep this general and honest. You are pointing at levers, not promising a number.

  • Paying a credit card down below about 30% of its limit
  • Letting a recent hard inquiry age out
  • Clearing up or settling one specific item
  • Using a direct relative who lives in the home as the credit-passer (one title-holder still signs)
  • Simply waiting and re-checking after things update

Then point them to the lender's official notice for the exact factors, and set a real follow-up.

Lock the follow-up before you leave

A path with no date is just a let-down with extra steps. Set a specific time to reconnect, and make it easy for them to say yes to that, even when they had to say not-yet to solar.

Rep "Let's do this. I'll check back with you in about [30 / 60] days. In the meantime, watch for that letter from the finance company, it'll spell out the specifics. You take care of the one or two things we talked about, and let's get you approved next round."
The Field Manual

Key Takeaways

In the home, you deliver the news. The finance company won't soften it for you, so your calm is the customer's calm.
Separate the person from the number. A credit result is math from one formula, not a verdict on their worth.
Nobody has one score. They have about sixteen, all real, all different, built for different purposes.
When their app reads higher, agree the system is confusing and stand next to them against it. Never call their number fake.
Feel, Felt, Found: you understand, they're not alone, and others in this spot qualified later.
Deliver the outcome, never the specific reasons. The lender's official notice does that by law, and it protects you both.
Never leave them at "no." A path plus a date turns a hard night into a future yes.
