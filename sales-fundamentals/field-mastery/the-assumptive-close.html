Closing Technique · The One-Call Craft

Get the Yes

90% of our sales are one-call closes. Either you close them, or they close you. The framework lives in One-Touch Close. This is the craft: how the best closers in the company actually get the yes at the table.

~20%Sign With AnyoneGood company, good product. They were always going to sign.
~60%The Real JobWinnable, but only if you find the need. This is where order-takers become closers.
~20%Genuine NoNot today, for real reasons. Still worth every swing.
Part 1 · The Mindset

People respond to direction, not information

Information sits there. Direction moves people. "The garbage is full" changes nothing at home. "Take the garbage out" gets it done. The same gap decides whether you close.

Give direction. "Here's what we're doing: a soft credit check, get you enrolled, take a few photos." A decision is an action, and you lead the action.
Give information. "Your bills are high, here's how solar works, let me know what you think." You handed them facts and left the deciding to a room you're not in.
"If they can't take the information you gave them and make a decision while you're there, how effective will they be at making that same decision when you're gone? People need direction, not information."Ken Ebbert · Coffee with Closers

Begin with the end in mind

One-call closing starts as an intent, before you knock. Order-takers only close the people who were already buying. Closers walk in knowing a decision gets made in this house today, and they make sure the customer knows it too.

Ask the room: think of your last lost deal. Were you giving direction, or just good information?
Recall What is the difference between an order-taker and a closer?

Intent. The order-taker only closes people already in a buying position. The closer walks in to help the customer make a decision today, and gives direction instead of information.

Part 2 · Keep It Simple

Less education, more closing

Solar is an emotional buy. Every inverter, optimizer, and production curve you explain turns it back into a big, scary decision. Overeducating is the number-one thing that kills a one-call close.

What the top closers do

  • Talk less about solar. One closer broke down the bill maybe 5 times in 200 installs. Another sold a deal last night without really talking about solar at all.
  • Lead with rapport. Become a genuine friend. Attack something you see in the home, ask where they used to live. Less is more, and it has to be sincere.
  • Keep the decision small. Make it simple, make it feel easy, keep them on one clear path forward.

What overeducating does

  • Adds objections you never needed to raise. Mention the inverter brand and they are Googling it instead of signing.
  • Makes it a big decision. The more technical detail, the more it feels like a major purchase to research, not a simple switch.
  • Stalls the close. A confused mind says "let me think about it" every time.
Role-play: pitch the program in 60 seconds with zero technical words. What did you cut, and did anything important get lost?
Recall Why does overeducating cost the close?

It turns an emotional, simple decision into a big technical one and raises objections you never needed to bring up. Less is more.

Part 3 · The Foundation

Create the problem first, or nothing else lands

On paper, solar fits almost everyone you sit with. So why does only about 30% sign? Complacency. Before you arrived, no one in that house thought their electric bill was a problem. Your first job is to make the problem real.

"Imagine a tsunami hits your island in 3 hours and someone comes door to door selling life rafts for $1,000. You are not saying 'let me think about it.' You are buying the raft. If you have not made them feel that, you have not earned the close."David Spagnoli · Coffee with Closers

Make the choice a no-brainer

You are fighting a monopoly and you are fighting complacency. Once the problem is real, the close gets simple:

Say"As long as you live here, you will always need electricity. You can pay more, or you can pay less. Is that really a choice you need a slideshow to make?"

Put it on yourself

If they still are not sure, take ownership. It builds trust and reopens the real objection.

Say"If this does not feel like a no-brainer yet, that's on me. I clearly have not done my job explaining it. Let me fix that."
Make inaction specific. The pain is not "rates go up." It is their own number: every month they wait, that $300 to $800 bill keeps coming, and it never comes back. Tie urgency to the bill on their table, not a slogan.
Ask the room: in your own words, what is the problem you create before you ever talk price? Say it out loud.
Recall Why do 70% walk without signing when solar fits on paper?

They never felt a problem. Complacency, not price, is the obstacle. Create the problem first, or every technique after it falls flat.

Part 4 · Discovery

Find the pain, and make them say it

You cannot sell something to someone who does not feel a need. The pain is rarely just the bill. It might be control, a hated utility, a fear of outages, a roof, or worry about the future. Find the one that's real, and let them name it.

<20%Order-TakerYou only close the people who walked in ready. Price is your only tool.
20 → 40%+Real CloserYou find the need, build the pain, and win the winnable middle.
Raceto the BottomCut price until yes, and you give away the profit on a deal that was never about money.

Dig past the first answer

  • "Why solar?" "To save money." Everyone says that. Great, you will. Now ask: what does saving money actually look like for you?
  • Ask three follow-ups before you move off a topic. One answer is a smokescreen; the third gets the truth.
  • Listen for the real driver: control, the utility, outages, the roof, the future. Money is often the cover story.
  • Then stay in the pain. Once they feel the need, hold there until they are ready to act. Do not rush off it.
Ask the room: name a deal where the real pain was not money. What was it, and how did you find it?
Recall What separates a 20% closer from a 40% closer?

Finding and building the real need instead of racing to the lowest price. Sub-20% means you are only taking the orders that were already placed.

Part 5 · The Assumptive Close

Set the agenda, then assume the sale

Be so assumptive that you are genuinely surprised when they don't move forward. But you only earn that assumption once you have built rapport and found the pain. Assume too early and it's pressure; assume after you've earned it and it's leadership.

  1. 1. Set the agenda up front

    Tell them at the start they can sign up today. "I'm excited, I can take you A to Z today as long as you qualify." Now the close is no surprise.

  2. 2. Walk in with momentum

    Bring the energy of someone here to close. One closer's mind is already in the attic planning the photos before he knocks.

  3. 3. Get them on the train

    Move to the next step as a given: "We'll do a soft credit check, get you enrolled, and take a few photos." Direction, not a request.

  4. 4. Use "program" and "qualify"

    Two of the most powerful words at the table. "This is a program. Honestly, most people don't qualify. We submit and find out in 30 to 60 days." It lowers pressure and raises value.

Make the concept click

When a customer cannot grasp the PPA, drop the jargon and use a picture they know:

Say"A store buys chicken wholesale and sells it to you at retail. Right now you buy power at retail from the utility. We cut out the retail markup, so you buy your electricity at the wholesale price instead. That's the whole program."
Ask the room: what is your exact agenda-setting line in the first 5 minutes? Does it tell them a decision happens today?
Recall What earns you the right to assume the close?

Rapport plus a real, discovered pain. Setting the agenda early makes the close expected. Assuming before you've earned it reads as pressure.

Part 6 · The Language Craft

Three phrases, and where you sit

The assumptive close lives in small words. Three phrases carry most of the weight at the table, and two physical moves do the rest. None of it is a trick. It's how you lower the walls so they can decide today.

"May I"

Ask permission for the small things, and the walls come down. "May I sit next to you? May I take my shoes off?" You're a guest who respects the home, not a salesperson who barged in.

"In my opinion"

It gets you out of any number. You never guaranteed a figure, you gave a professional read. "In my opinion, with a household like yours, this is the better move right now."

Feel, felt, found

The objection handler that does more than any other line. "I know how you feel. I felt the same way. Here's what I found." It joins them first, then turns the corner with you on their side.

"May I, in my opinion, and feel felt found. Feel felt found has made me more money than any phrase I use."Coffee with Closers · In the home

Sit next to them, not across

Across the table, people put the guard up. They associate it with a car-sales standoff. Beside them, they associate it with a tutor or a teacher, and the guard drops. Show up a few minutes early so the "I'm here to get your solar set up" expectation is already in the room.

Say"May I sit next to you? It's easier to show you everything from here."

High-five the signature

The second they sign, mark it with a genuine high five and real excitement. You watch them go from on the fence to locked in, because they see you're honestly happy for them. You're not celebrating your commission, you're celebrating their summer bill going from 600 to a fixed rate they can count on.

Say"Congrats. I'm genuinely excited for you. That bill is not running your summer anymore."
Feel, felt, found, live. On "I was told to buy a system": "I know how you feel, I like to own things too. I felt the same way. But once we lost the tax credit, in my opinion this is the better option for you right now. Let's look at the numbers." Acknowledge, join, then turn the corner with them.
Role-play: run feel, felt, found on a live objection, sitting beside the rep instead of across from them. Did the phrase land, or did it sound scripted?
Recall What do the three phrases each do at the table?

"May I" asks permission and drops the walls. "In my opinion" protects you so you never guarantee a number. Feel, felt, found joins the customer first, then turns the objection. Sit beside them like a tutor, and high-five the signature.

Part 7 · The Move

"I need to think about it" is not a no

Most reps panic at "let me think about it." That reaction is exactly what the customer expects. Do the opposite: agree completely, and keep moving. It defuses the standoff and surfaces the real objection.

"We need some time to think about it."

Don't flinch. "Of course you should think about it. I would hope so. I'm going to step outside, take a few photos and measurements, and enter some data. Back in 20 minutes." Then get up and do it. They think while you work, and you return to their real questions instead of a wall.

Why the move works

  • Removes the standoff. You agreed with them, so there's nothing to push against.
  • Surfaces the truth. When you come back, the actual concern is usually waiting for you, and now you can clear it.
  • Stays a porch-light move. Use it when you need it, not as a scripted finish line. If you created the problem well, you may not need it at all.
  • Pairs with a tie-down. "Next month that utility bill still shows up. What we're doing today replaces it. Does that part make sense?"
See One-Touch Close for what "I need to think about it" actually means underneath, and the decision-maker rule that goes with it.
Role-play the move end to end: they stall, you agree and step out, you come back. What real objection showed up?
Recall What do you do the moment a customer says "I need to think about it"?

Agree with them, don't react, and keep moving. Step out to take photos and measurements, let them think, and come back to the real objection.

Part 8 · Get in the Battle

Go get the no

Not closing is usually fear of rejection. Reps would rather leave with hope than draw the line and hear a real answer. But if they're not in a yes, they're already in a no. You have nothing to lose by asking.

80%Of salespeople never even ask for the close
3 to 5No's most people give before a yes
AllLost if you walk out without asking
"Someone is getting closed in that house every time you go. Either you're closing them, or they're closing you. Take every swing, and never try to bunt."Ken Ebbert · Coffee with Closers

Be prepared to hear no

You prepare your calendar, your iPad, your bill breakdown, your design. Prepare for the no too. When it comes, you are not rattled. You ask another question, find what you missed, and turn it into a yes.

Say"I clearly missed something, because this should be an easy yes for you. Help me understand what's really holding you back."
Ask the room: when you get a no, what is your very next sentence? If you don't have one, that's the gap.
Recall Why is asking for the close a free shot?

If they're not already a yes, they're a no, so you've lost nothing by asking. Most people give 3 to 5 no's before a yes, and 80% of reps never even ask.

Part 9 · Stay Out of Your Own Way

Don't mess up the deal you already had

A lot of houses are yours to lose. Who offends the least makes the most. The skill is not just saying the right thing, it's not saying the wrong one, and reading who you're actually selling.

Protect the sale

  • Skip the landmines. No politics, no religion. One closer lost a ready deal the second a stray photo flashed on his phone.
  • Don't volunteer objections. Don't bring up the roof warranty or inverter brand unprompted. You'll start a worry that wasn't there.
  • Read the decision-maker. If he checks her face after every answer, she decides. Sell to her.

Sell the confidence

  • Stand firm on price. Start higher, hold it, and give ground only for a real reason. Reps who stopped racing down on price closed more, and sold more batteries.
  • Be the premium brand. We'll be here in 20 years. Confidence in that, not the lowest number, is what sells.
  • Be unique. Five reps sound the same. Be a little bold, a little fun, take a side. Standing out beats blending in.
Ask the room: what is one thing you used to say in the home that created an objection? When did you stop?
Recall What does "who offends the least makes the most" mean in practice?

Many deals are yours to lose. Avoid landmines (politics, unprompted roof or inverter talk), read the real decision-maker, and don't talk yourself out of a sale that was already closing.
