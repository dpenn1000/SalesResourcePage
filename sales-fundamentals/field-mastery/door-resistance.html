Part 1 · The Field Mindset

Every Door Is a Role Play

Resistance is not rejection. Resistance is the start of a conversation. Your job is not to "win" -- your job is to extend the conversation until you reach a clean outcome. There is always new information to give the homeowner. Get it out early.

"It's a problem when you make it a problem. It's not a problem when you don't." Field Truth

The Five Rules of the Door

  • Extend the conversation. No matter what -- angry, busy, confused -- the job is to keep the conversation going until you have a real outcome.
  • Listen, then redirect. Whatever they give you is data. Acknowledge it, then bring something new to the table.
  • Bring new information early. A new program. A new opportunity. A reason to re-engage. Don't lead with what they already know.
  • An appointment is just an opportunity. The OR rep handed you a door. What you do with it is on you.
  • Use silence as a tool. The pauses you take after key statements teach the customer that discomfort is okay. They learn that you are here and you are safe.

What the Rep Wins

The rep wins by extending the conversation and getting to the root cause with following questions. The role play continues -- strictly -- until an outcome is achieved.

Don't "win" the argument. Win the next interaction.

Part 2 · The Visual

The Door Resistance Decision Tree

Three things happen when you arrive: customer doesn't answer, customer tries to cancel, or customer answers and you head to the table. Each branch has a clear path to a clean disposition.

Customer Not Answering
Call the Call Center to report the no-show from the door.
Call Center verifies the no-show, updates Skedulo on-site as No Show.
-- OR -- Call Center gets customer ETA.
15-MINUTE THRESHOLD
After 15 min, fail to beat resistance → mark as Sit, No Sale.
Opp stays in your name.
If never came → Appointment removed from your name.
Customer Tries to Cancel
Combat the door resistance to get in the door.
Fail → mark as Cancelled at the Door.
Beat the resistance → get to the table → Restart from Arrival.
AT THE TABLE
Customer hears pitch but is not interested → Sit, No Sale.
Customer Signs Up → mark as Contract Signed, include great Outcome Notes. Congratulations.
Customer hears pitch, sets a Go-Back → Sit, Go-Back Scheduled. Choose date and time, include great Outcome Notes.
Customer Answers -- You're Headed to the Table
DQ Factor discovered → Send in a DQ Ticket and call the Call Center with the ticket #.
Mark as Sit, no sale. Opp stays in your name.
GO-BACK PATHS
Customer keeps Go-Back → Restart from Arrival.
Customer cancels Go-Back → contact CC, remove from calendar.
The 15-minute rule. If they're not answering, you wait fifteen minutes -- not five, not twenty. The rule exists so reps don't ghost the door early and so they don't sit too long. Use the time to call the Call Center, not to scroll your phone.
Part 3 · How You're Scored

The Six Outcomes

Every door interaction lands in one of six places. Five of them are wins. Even "no" is data -- but only if you got there after extending the conversation, not before.

🏠

1. They get in the house

Beat the resistance, got to the table. Best possible outcome -- the appointment is now yours to run.

📅

2. In-person Go-Back

Both decision-makers on a confirmed time. Your second swing -- make it count.

💻

3. Virtual Go-Back

Video call set with both deciders. Treat it like the in-person sit.

📞

4. OK to call

Permission to call at a specific time. Lower bar, but still a foot in the door.

💬

5. OK to text

Permission to text. Lowest "yes" -- keep the conversation alive.

🚫

6. NO

A clean no, after the conversation was extended. Not a win -- but worth knowing why so the next door is better.

What "winning the door" looks like in your notes

A win is not "got in the house." A win is any of outcomes 1–5 with notes specific enough that your manager can read them and know exactly what happened, what was tried, and what's next.

Part 4 · How Managers Train This

The Role-Play Framework

Door Resistance is a strict, manager-led role play. It runs until an outcome is achieved -- not until the rep gives up. The framework is the same every time.

1

The setup

Rep "knocks." The manager plays the homeowner with resistance -- friendly, hostile, confused, busy, distracted. The resistance is real. The role play is strict.

2

The rep extends the conversation

The rep wins by extending the conversation, getting to the root cause with following questions. "Following questions" means asking the next question after the answer -- not moving on. The role play continues until a real outcome (1–6) is reached.

3

Achieve a clean outcome

The exercise is over when the rep lands on one of the six outcomes -- not when the rep says "I think I'd say X." Make them get there.

4

Ask the rep what they'd write

Once the outcome is reached, ask: "What would you write in Skedulo outcome notes?" Listen carefully. This is where you find out what the rep actually heard versus what happened.

5

Compare to a real note

Read the original outcome note from the library out loud. Discuss together: what was missing? What would have set up a stronger close on the next interaction? This is the highest-leverage moment of the exercise.

What strong manager facilitation looks like

  • Don't break character. The rep must work the resistance, not the manager.
  • Don't end early. Let it get uncomfortable -- that's the practice.
  • Pick scenarios that match the rep's gap. A rep who runs from credit objections gets credit-resistance scenarios.
  • Debrief on notes, not on talk track. The note is the artifact. If the note is sharp, the conversation was sharp.
Part 5 · The Artifact

Note Format & Discipline

The note is the only thing that survives the door. Write it like the next person on this account is going to read it -- because they are.

The Format

Every note follows the same shape. It saves time, it builds team continuity, and it creates an audit trail across follow-up attempts.

Initials (date) -- what happened, what's next, when
Av(10.15.25) -- left vm for homeowner. Will follow up Thursday 10/16.

Compounding effect: when the next rep, the manager, or the analytics team looks at this account, they know exactly what's been tried and what to try next.

What a Strong Note Contains

  • Customer situation -- what was the resistance? What did they actually say?
  • The rep's response -- what was tried? What was new information shared?
  • The outcome -- one of the six. Be specific.
  • The next step -- date, time, channel, and who owns it.
  • Anything weird -- outreach mismatch, household situation, credit signal, hostility. Future-you will thank you.

Common Note Anti-Patterns

  • "Cancelled" -- no context, no next step, no value.
  • "Not interested" -- what specifically? In what? Why?
  • "Will follow up" -- when? How? You don't even own this anymore.
  • "She'll call me" -- false hope. The rep owns the next step.
The ghost text. For people who go silent, send one simple text: "Have you given up on your solar project?" That's it. Keep it simple. Keep it human. It surprisingly works.
