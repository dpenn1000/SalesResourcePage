You're Talking to a Homeowner

Walk in as a solar rep and you cast the smallest net. US residential solar is roughly a $15 billion market. Storage is bigger, near $25 billion. Roofing is bigger still, near $70 billion. The person across the table owns a whole home, not a solar account, so introduce the whole home every single time.

A Homeowner, Not a Solar Customer

Sizing the real market reframes the whole appointment. If you only talk solar, you fish the smallest pond in the house. You are statistically more likely to sell a roof or a battery, and Trinity can help the customer pay for either from money already in their budget.

Solar, roughly $15B

The smallest net you can cast. Real, but the narrowest slice of what the homeowner in front of you actually buys and owns.

Storage, roughly $25B

A bigger market than solar. More homeowners want backup and resilience than you assume, and most have never been offered it.

Roofing, roughly $70B

The largest market of the three. Every home has a roof, every roof ages, and you can see the problem from the driveway.

In the home"I'm not just here about solar. You own a whole home, and my job is to look at the whole thing: the roof over your head, keeping the lights on in an outage, and your electric bill. Let's start outside and look at all of it together."
The reframe: introduce yourself as a whole-home consultant, not a solar rep. The moment you are the home expert instead of the solar expert, you have earned the right to talk about the roof and the battery.
Ask the room: when you knock or sit down, do you sound like a solar rep or a home consultant? What is the first sentence that changes that?
Why does leading with solar cast the smallest net?

AnswerSolar is roughly a $15 billion market, storage roughly $25 billion, and roofing roughly $70 billion. You are talking to a homeowner who owns all three needs, so a solar-only opening ignores the two larger opportunities sitting in the same house.

Don't Let the Customer's Heart Get Broken

Not offering a product is not a neutral act. It is a decision you made for the customer without telling them. If you never raise the battery or the roof, you are betting their home stays lucky, and a competitor will sell them the thing you left on the table.

What "I'll just do solar" can cost them

  • A power outage with damage after you never offered a battery. That is on you, not on bad luck.
  • A roof failure in a few years you saw and never raised, now a full out-of-pocket replacement.
  • A competitor's call. One storage company already phoned Trinity solar-only customers and sold them the batteries Trinity left behind.

Offering it is how you serve

  • Present every product so the homeowner chooses with full information, not a gap you decided for them.
  • Let them say no. A declined battery is a served customer. A battery never mentioned is an unserved one.
  • You become the reason they were helped, not the reason they were not.
In the home"I'd rather you tell me no than have me never bring it up. If the power goes out next winter and your basement floods, I don't want that to be the day you find out I could have helped and stayed quiet. So let me show you everything, and you decide."
Ask the room: name a deal where you only sold solar and later found out they needed the roof or the battery. How would you run that appointment today?
Why is failing to present a product not "neutral"?

AnswerSkipping the battery or the roof is a choice you made for the customer without telling them. If they later take a loss you could have prevented, that is on you, and a competitor will happily sell the product you left on the table. Offering everything and letting them decline is how you actually serve them.

Stop Spending Their Money for Them

Reps trained as "solar equals savings" people instinctively quote the cheapest option and talk the customer out of value. Lead with the higher number, the self-powered home, and let them back down if they want. You can always remove the battery. It is very hard to add $150 a month back in after you anchored low.

Pay the utility, get nothing

Roughly $300 a month to the utility, forever, with nothing to show for it and a rate that keeps climbing.

Solar, lock the rate

Roughly $240 a month for solar, a locked rate the homeowner controls instead of one the utility raises.

The self-powered home

Roughly $350 net for solar plus a whole-home battery. Some homeowners find the backup story more appealing than the savings story, and they never get there if you never offer it.

Rep · presenting the options"You can pay the utility around $300 a month and get nothing back. You can do solar around $240 and lock your rate in. Or for about $350 net you get the self-powered home, solar plus a battery, so an outage stops being your problem. I'll lay all three out and you tell me which fits."
Get your hands out of their pockets. When you pre-decide that they cannot afford the battery, you have spent their money for them. Present the higher number with conviction. You can always back the payment out. You can rarely add it back.
Ask the room: how often do you quote the cheapest option first because you assumed the budget? What does anchoring on the self-powered home change?
Why lead with the higher number instead of the cheapest option?

AnswerYou can always back the battery out and drop to solar-only, but it is very hard to add $150 a month back after you anchored low. Leading with the self-powered home lets the customer decompress to a smaller package and still feel they chose it, and some buyers prefer the backup story to the savings story.

Let Solar Become the Attachment

Reps carry a fear of "attachments" that bump the deal cost and scare the customer. Flip it. Lead with the roof or the battery, because if you can sell those, sliding solar in is easy. Every product is still the savings story, just in a new form.

The old fear

  • Solar first, everything else "extra." The roof and battery feel like upsells that threaten a clean solar deal.
  • You flinch at the bigger number, so you never raise the products with the biggest markets.

The flip

  • Lead with the roof or the battery. They are visible, urgent, and easy for the homeowner to see the need.
  • Solar becomes the easy slide. Once they trust you on the roof, adding solar is the attachment, not the fight.
  • The roof is the path of least resistance: it gets the homeowner outside and connected to the house with you.
On the walk-around"Before we talk panels, how do you feel about your roof? Can I show you what I see? Let's look together. Once we've got a plan for the roof, here's the part most people love: there's a way to help pay for it, and your electric bill, at the same time."
Still the savings story. A roof saves a future replacement, a denied insurance claim, a lost policy. A battery saves a lost workday, a hotel night, spoiled food, basement water damage, and future time-of-use rates. Every product saves the customer money, just in a new form.
Ask the room: which product are you most afraid to bring up first? What would change if you opened with it on the stoop?
What does "let solar become the attachment" mean in practice?

AnswerLead with the roof or the battery, which are visible and easy for the homeowner to see the need for. Once you have sold one of those, adding solar is an easy slide instead of a separate fight. The dragon to slay is the fear of leading with anything other than solar.

Good, Better, Best: The Total Home Tiers

This is the Men's Wearhouse strategy: offer everything, and the customer still leaves with something. You will not get all three products every time, but naming the full ecosystem reinforces the sale instead of threatening it. A high tier gives buyers a decompression path so they can decline a piece and still buy, feeling they got the value option.

TierWhat's in itThe story
Best · Trinity Total HomeRoof, battery, solar.The whole home handled, with the avoided future roof replacement mapped out. One trip, one trusted installer.
Better · Peace of MindBattery plus solar.The standard. Locked rate plus whole-home backup, so an outage stops being the customer's problem.
Good · SolarSolar only.A la carte. A locked rate and real savings, but the customer hears what they passed on and chose it knowingly.
Rep · naming the tiers"Our Total Home package is the roof, the battery, and the solar, the whole home handled at once. The middle is the peace-of-mind package, battery and solar, and that's the one most people land on. You can go a la carte with just solar, that's fine, I just don't want you to short yourself."
The decompression path: a customer who sees the high tier can step down to the middle and still feel they got the smart deal. With no high tier to step down from, solar-only feels like the ceiling instead of the floor. Offer everything and you leave with a signed contract of some kind.
Ask the room: when did naming all three tiers turn a solar-only deal into a battery-and-solar deal? Walk it back: what made them step up, or feel good stepping down?
Why present a "best" tier you know most won't buy?

AnswerThe high tier gives buyers a decompression path: they can decline the roof or battery and still buy solar, feeling they chose the value option rather than hit a ceiling. Offering the full ecosystem (the Men's Wearhouse strategy) means the customer almost always leaves with a signed contract of some kind.

Make Multi-Product a Habit

The bigger opportunity only pays off if it becomes automatic. Build two small habits on every appointment: introduce the whole home, and ask the roof age and log it in the notes. A logged roof age turns an unclosed lead into a warm re-engagement three months later with a different product.

1. Introduce the whole home

Open as a whole-home consultant, not a solar rep, so you have earned the right to discuss the roof and the battery.

Every appointment

2. Ask roof age, log it

Ask the roof age and known issues in discovery, then write it in the Salesforce notes. It costs 10 seconds and it never expires.

In the notes

3. Re-engage in 3 months

An unclosed lead is not dead. A logged roof age, a new finance partner, or a battery is a fresh reason to call and a fresh way for them to say yes.

The follow-up

The every-appointment habit

  • Introduced yourself as a whole-home consultant, not a solar rep.
  • Walked the roof and asked how they feel about it.
  • Asked the roof age and any known issues.
  • Logged the roof age in the Salesforce notes.
  • Presented the self-powered home before the cheaper option.
  • Named the good, better, best tiers out loud.
On solar, set the bar low and over-deliver. Only about 1 in 3 homes qualifies for solar, so lead with what solar CAN do and let the result beat the promise. A customer covering even part of their usage still comes out ahead. Never imply solar erases the entire bill.
Role-play: a solar-only appointment from last quarter that didn't close. Re-engage it today with a roof age you logged and a different product. Run the call live.
Why log the roof age even when you don't sell a roof today?

AnswerAn unclosed lead is not dead. A roof age logged in the notes lets you re-engage that homeowner in 3 months with a different product (a roof, a battery, or a new finance option), giving them a fresh reason to say yes. It costs 10 seconds at the appointment and never expires.
