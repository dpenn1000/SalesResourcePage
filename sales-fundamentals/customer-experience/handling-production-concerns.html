When the Bill Comes In

The call after PTO is where reps lose referrals and create cancels. "My bill went up, my solar isn't working." 9 times out of 10 the system is fine. Your job is to stay calm, listen, and deescalate. This is the first real test of whether you are still in the customer's corner.

Listen First, Do Not Be Dismissive

Treat a production complaint like a fresh referral: respond fast, get in person if you can, and let the homeowner fully explain before you offer a single answer. Prescribing before you have listened is how you lose them.

The dismissive reflex

  • Jumping to the answer before they finish venting.
  • Solving over text, when the moment calls for your voice or your face.
  • Sounding rushed, which reads as "he does not want this call."

The doctor's discipline

  • Diagnose before you prescribe. Ask, listen, then answer.
  • Get in person if you can. Be factual, be present.
  • Take the hard call first. Being there when something goes wrong is what makes you their guy.
The principle"If a doctor prescribes a solution before you tell him what is wrong, are you going to trust him? Show up in person, be factual, and listen before you present the solution."
Why it matters: most escalations are tiny in dollars ($30, $10) but huge in feeling. The real complaint underneath is "my voice is not being heard." Let them be heard and you create a friend who refers.
Ask the room: what is the call you least want to make this week? Why is that the one to take first?
Why is "diagnose before you prescribe" the rule for a production call?

AnswerIf you jump to an answer before the homeowner finishes explaining, you lose their trust, the same way you would not trust a doctor who prescribes before hearing the symptom. Respond fast, get in person if you can, and let them fully explain first. Often the real complaint is being heard, not the dollar amount.

Show What They Would Have Paid, Not Last April's Bill

The single biggest deescalator. Customers compare today to last year's bill, but that is apples to oranges, because the utility rate may have climbed 20 to 30% since then. Show them what they would be paying right now with no solar.

The trap they fall into

"My bill is higher than last April." They are comparing this year to a rate that no longer exists. Last year was 20 to 30% cheaper, so of course the raw number looks worse.

The comparison that calms them

What you DID pay versus what you WOULD have paid. Compare current usage on solar against the current utility rate, not last year's. That is the honest, like-for-like number.

Set the bar low, then beat it

Solar does not erase a bill. Frame the win as the gap against today's utility rate. Even covering a meaningful slice of usage at a locked rate puts them ahead of where the utility would have them.

On the phone · deescalating"Most folks compare to last April. The catch is the rate was 20 to 30% lower then, so that is apples to oranges. Let me show you what you actually paid this month versus what you would have paid the utility at today's rate. That is the real comparison."
Ask the room: a customer is sure their bill went up. Walk the "would have paid" reframe out loud in two sentences.
Why is comparing to last year's bill misleading, and what do you show instead?

AnswerThe utility rate may have risen 20 to 30% since last year, so last April's bill is apples to oranges. Instead, show what they DID pay on solar versus what they WOULD have paid the utility at today's rate. It is the honest like-for-like comparison and the strongest deescalator.

It's the Fear, Not the Bill

The unexpected bill is real, but the bigger thing in the room is the fear: "did I make the worst mistake of my life, and will I be double-billed for 25 years?" PTO is the first test of whether they have someone in their corner. The word that resets them is "normal."

What they are really feeling

  • "I got talked into this." The dollar is the trigger; the fear is the mistake.
  • "Am I on my own now?" This is the moment they find out if you disappear after the sale.
  • Acknowledge the feeling, but do not over-validate that something is broken.

Be the calm in the storm

  • Breathe. This is normal. Everything is working the way it should.
  • Stay calm and confident. While they are losing their minds, you are the steady authority they lean on.
  • 9 times out of 10 the system is fine, so you can hold that calm honestly.
On the phone · steadying them"I hear you, and I get why that is stressful. Take a breath, because this is normal. Let's look at it together. I am still your guy on this, the same as the day we sat down."
The billboard truth: every PTO customer is a billboard, a raving fan or someone quietly advocating against you. This call decides which one they become.
Ask the room: how do you acknowledge the fear without over-validating that the system is broken? Say the line.
What is the fear underneath "my solar isn't working," and what word resets it?

AnswerThe fear is "I made the worst mistake of my life and will be double-billed for 25 years," and whether they still have someone in their corner. The reset word is "normal": breathe, this is normal, everything is working the way it should. Acknowledge the feeling but stay calm and do not over-validate that something is wrong.

The Second Conversation

You rarely solve a charged issue in the first call, the same way you do not settle a fight with your spouse in one conversation. The first call's only job is to acknowledge and let the emotion settle. The answer lands in the second call, on your terms.

  1. 1. Acknowledge at peak heat

    Let them vent fully. Do not try to win the facts while they are at their highest emotional state.

  2. 2. Get the bill, get off the phone

    "Can you send me the bill so I can look into it and call you back?" End the call while they are hot. Their state comes down.

  3. 3. Call back on your terms

    Now you have the facts, they are calm, and you control the conversation. The answer lands because they can finally hear it.

On the phone · buying the second call"I want to get this right for you, so let me pull the actual numbers. Send me a photo of the bill and I will call you back this afternoon with a clear answer. Sound good?"
Ask the room: why does ending the first call at peak heat help you more than pushing through it? What changes by the callback?
Why do you get off the phone at peak heat and call back?

AnswerYou rarely solve a charged issue in the first conversation, like a fight with a spouse. Ask them to send the bill and schedule a callback so their emotional state settles. On the second call you have the facts, they are calm, and you control the conversation, so the answer actually lands.

Read the Bill Correctly First

Roughly half of "my bill is huge" complaints are bill-reading errors, not the system. Get the bill in hand and verify the cycle and the charges before you assume anything underproduced. Often the current charge for power is near zero.

What looks like a huge billWhat is actually going on
A back balanceMoney they already owed carries forward and keeps adding to the statement. Their current charge for power can be zero; the utility is asking for the old balance, not for new usage.
A statement before energizationThe bill predates the system turning on, so it covers a period when solar was not yet producing. The cycle does not match the PTO date.
Budget or balanced billingA level-pay plan was never removed, so they are paying a flat averaged amount that has nothing to do with this month's solar production.
On the callback · walking the bill"Good news, I see what is happening. This line here is a balance you already owed, it is not new power. Your actual charge for electricity this cycle is almost nothing. Let's get that old balance cleared up and you are set."
Ask the room: name the three bill-reading errors that fake a "huge" bill. How do you check each one on the statement?
Name two bill-reading errors that look like underproduction.

AnswerAny two: a back balance the customer already owed that keeps carrying forward, a statement dated before the system was energized, or a budget/balanced-billing plan that was never removed. About half of "my bill is huge" calls are one of these, so verify the cycle and charges before assuming the system underproduced.

Find the Hidden Load

Customers swear nothing changed while a major new electric load sits in plain sight. Walk the house and ask, because the answer is usually a mini-split, an EV, a hot tub, or someone who moved in. Solving it is often an add-on sale, not a problem.

Walk the house, ask the question

"Has anything new gone in since we sized this?" A mini-split, an EV charger, a hot tub, a basement tenant. People genuinely forget the load they added.

Weather alone swings usage

Even set-and-forget, usage moves 10 to 20%. A winter 5 degrees colder or a summer 5 degrees hotter changes consumption on its own. That is a no-fault, no-blame explanation.

The hidden load is an add-on

Do not accuse, normalize. "You used more power, let's figure out if we need more panels." The world is electrifying; more usage is expected, and more panels plus referrals follow.

In the home · the palm-tree question"Are those palm trees real? How do you keep them alive through a Connecticut winter? Electric heating coils. They had not even thought about it. That load was the whole story, and we added panels to the house."
Ask the room: a customer insists nothing changed. What questions walk the house and surface the hidden load without sounding like an accusation?
A customer swears nothing changed but usage is up. What do you do?

AnswerWalk the house and ask what is new: a mini-split, an EV, a hot tub, someone who moved in. People forget the load they added. Remember weather alone swings usage 10 to 20%. Normalize it instead of accusing, then frame more panels as the fix, which is often an add-on sale and referrals.

Own It, Without the Word "Lied"

Sometimes you did set the wrong expectation. Fall on the sword, because the customer forgives the salesperson they fell in love with far more than the company they feel stuck with for 25 years. But never say "lied," even sincerely, and lean on the production guarantee when the system genuinely underproduces.

You took ownership of a missed expectation.

Fall on the sword, never say "lied." "I'm sorry I didn't explain this fully," or "I set the wrong expectation." Even an honest "I lied" becomes the line they repeat to your manager, Client Resources, and the BBB. The word you use is the word they keep.

The system actually is underproducing.

Lean on the production guarantee. "I know how that feels, and other folks have felt the same when a bill surprised them. What I have found is that we have a production guarantee for exactly this. Let's put it to work for you." It exists for this moment, so use it.

Stay honest and specific. Take ownership without blaming the company or the finance partner, and never admit to lying. State facts, lean on the real guarantee, and let the numbers do the reassurance. Do not promise an outcome you cannot deliver.
Role-play: a customer says "you lied to me about my bill." Take ownership, avoid the word "lied," and land on the guarantee, start to finish.
Why "fall on the sword" but never say "lied"?

AnswerCustomers forgive the salesperson they fell in love with far more than the company they feel stuck with, so owning the miss keeps the relationship. But "lied," even said sincerely, becomes the exact word they repeat to your manager, Client Resources, and the BBB. Say "I'm sorry I didn't explain this fully," and lean on the production guarantee when the system truly underproduces.
