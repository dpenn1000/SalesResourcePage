Part 1 · The Case for Touches

Silence Is What Loses the Deal

The longest stretch of a solar project is the wait. Months pass between the signature and the switch-on, and an uninformed customer fills that silence with doubt. The cadence replaces doubt with progress.

"The sale is not finished when they sign. It's finished when they refer you." The Customer-Touch Standard

What the cadence buys you

  • Fewer cancels. The 24-hour call catches buyer's remorse before it hardens into a cancellation.
  • A calm customer through a long build. Five to six months of silence breeds doubt. Scheduled updates replace it with a sense of forward motion.
  • Reviews and referrals. A customer who felt handled says yes when you ask for the Google review and the neighbor's name.

The one rule

Never let the customer wonder what's happening. If a stage will take weeks, tell them that before they ask. A heads-up about a delay reads as competence. A delay they discover on their own reads as a problem.

Part 3 · The One That Matters Most

The 24-Hour Buyer's Remorse Call

This is the single most important touch in the journey. After the excitement of signing wears off, the customer is at their most vulnerable to second-guessing. Your job is to get there first.

Why it carries the whole cadence

Remorse creeps in within a day. A friend plants a doubt, a number gets re-read, the quiet after a big decision starts to feel like a mistake. A proactive call closes that gap before it opens. A reactive call, made only after they reach out worried, is already a step behind.

1

Schedule it before you leave the home

Set a specific time while you're still at the table. Naming the time builds trust and puts you on the hook to follow through.

2

Follow through exactly when promised

Call at the time you named, not around it. The kept promise is the message. It tells them the rest of the project will run the same way.

3

Listen, then lead

Ask how they're feeling. Address whatever surfaced overnight. Reaffirm why solar is the right call for their home and their numbers, in their words, not a pitch.

The goal of the call: they hang up feeling seen, supported, and confident. They didn't just sign paperwork. They made a smart, future-focused decision with someone they can count on. Leave them sure of both.
Part 4 · The Other Critical Moment

The Home Stretch: Post-Install to PTO

The panels are up, but the system isn't on yet. This stretch runs two to four months, most of it outside our control, and it's where an excited customer turns into an impatient one. Set the timeline early and the wait becomes a process they trust instead of a mystery they stew on.

Set the real timeline up front

From the last panel going up to the switch turning on is usually two to four months. Most of that is the town and the utility working independently of us. A delay the customer expected reads as competence. A delay they discover on their own reads as a problem. Name the number before they ask for it.

What's actually happening

1
Internal

Quality control and the as-built

QC checks run first. Then we create the "as-built," the document of exactly what was installed on the roof. Everything downstream is built off it.

2
Vendor

The system plaque

The as-built drives a custom engraved plaque from the vendor. Inspection fails without it, so this step gates the inspection date. It's the most common reason the timeline feels slow.

3
Town

Inspection

Once the plaque is in hand, the town inspector and a Trinity electrician meet at the home. The plaque goes on at inspection.

4
Town and utility

Municipal release and net meter

The inspector sends approval to the utility and the town issues a municipal release. The utility then sends a tech to install the new net meter, on their own schedule, often unannounced. The customer may never see them come.

5
Switch-on

Permission to Operate

The utility emails PTO to the homeowner and to Trinity. That's the official green light to turn the system on. We don't issue the notice until the utility confirms.

The unannounced meter: the utility swaps the net meter on their own schedule, usually with no warning. Tell the customer ahead of time so a meter change they didn't book reads as progress, not a problem.

When they call upset about the wait

Lead with empathy, not the process. They aren't angry at the steps, they're excited to start saving on sunny summer days. Get there first.

  • Acknowledge the excitement. "I get it. You're ready to start producing, and I want that for you too."
  • Listen all the way through. Let them say the whole thing before you explain anything.
  • Then the why. Walk the five steps and name who owns each one. Most of the wait is the town and the utility working on their own timelines, not ours.
  • Reaffirm the decision. Remind them this was a smart, custom project, and they chose the company that's 30 years strong and second to none at clearing obstacles.

Texts for the stretch

The Message Library already covers What's to Come, On to PTO, and Permission to Operate. Add this heads-up before the meter swap so it never lands as a surprise:

Quick heads-up [Customer]: the next step is the utility installing your new net meter. They schedule this themselves and usually show up unannounced when a crew is in the area, so don't worry if you see a meter swap you didn't book. That's a good sign. Once it's in, PTO is right behind it. 🔌

The PTO celebration call

A call, never a text. When PTO lands, this is the moment the whole journey was building toward.

I called right away to let you know that your system has been approved to start generating clean green solar electricity for your family! I would love to come out to celebrate this moment with you. Thank you for being so patient, and congratulations!

Customer-facing version (copy, paste, forward)

A homeowner can have this in writing. Send it as an email or a long text right after install day so they have the map.

Hi [Customer], now that your panels are installed, here's what to expect before your system turns on. This part usually takes two to four months, and most of it is in the hands of your town and your utility, so a little patience here is normal.

1. Quality check. Our team reviews the install and documents exactly what's on your roof.
2. Your system plaque. We order a custom plaque that the inspector requires on site.
3. Inspection. Your town inspector and a Trinity electrician meet at your home.
4. Net meter. Your town clears the project and your utility installs a new meter. They schedule this themselves and often show up unannounced, so don't worry if you see a meter swap you didn't book. It's a good sign.
5. Permission to Operate. Your utility sends the official approval to turn the system on. The moment we get it, I'll call you so we can flip the switch together.

I'll be checking in the whole way. Any question at all, call or text me anytime. [Your name], Trinity Solar

Part 7 · The Mechanics

The Smart Touch

The cadence is the what. This is the how: small mechanics that make every touch land like a person who is paying attention, keep a quiet customer from going cold, and turn a passing comment into the next deal months later.

"Type 'how's Cosmo doing' and hit send later, planned to send exactly when you told them. Because you're doing exactly what you said, it builds credibility. And I don't have to remember everybody's dog's name." In the home · the follow-up promise

Automate the promise you made

The strongest follow-up is the one you swore to send and then actually sent, on the day you said. Use your phone's send-later feature to keep that promise without trusting your memory. Before you leave the table, write the text, set the pet's name and the detail you just heard, and schedule it for the exact day you named.

Doing precisely what you said you would do is the whole point. It tells the customer the project will run the same way, and you never have to remember whose cat is named Cosmo.

Hey [Customer], thinking of you both. How's [pet] settling in? Counting down to your install. Reach out any time. [Your name], Trinity Solar

Match the channel and the style to the person

Your natural texting voice reads differently to someone who has never met you. Exclamation points and a wall of emojis can land as yelling to a customer who has no idea who you are yet. One rep got back a flat "why are you yelling at me?" from a homeowner who had never spoken to him. You cannot be that familiar that fast.

Ask how and when they want to hear from you, and then follow it. "Should I text you or call you? Mornings or evenings? Does 6pm work?" Now you know a lot. A customer who says "text after 6" and then goes quiet at 6 is a different problem than one you called at 2pm. Most "unresponsive" customers are just being reached on the wrong channel at the wrong hour.

When they ghost, drive over and text your ETA

A customer who stops answering texts and calls has usually gone quiet for a harmless reason, not a bad one. Do not let the silence write a story in your head. Send a light, friendly message first, then start driving and text your ETA. Naming that you are on the way forces a response in a way another unanswered text never will.

One rep did exactly this and got back "thanks, my kids are asleep" and a phone call. Another customer had simply had a phone problem the whole time. The drive solved both. Do not be afraid to just go back.

The re-entry question is everything: "Still interested?" invites a no. "How did it hold up?" reads as genuine concern and lets the customer realize the answer for themselves. Same psychology, opposite result.

Keep storm-trigger lists

Not every customer is ready the day you meet them. Keep two standing lists: people who passed on a battery, and people who mentioned a leaking or aging roof. These are warm contacts waiting for a reason.

The reason is the weather. After every storm or outage, work those lists with one low-pressure, genuine question, never a sales push:

  • Battery-interest list, after an outage: "How long were you out? Everyone okay over there?"
  • Roof-leak list, after a storm: "How'd your roof hold up in that one?"

A rep asked that exact roof question and got back "not good, when can you come out?" from someone he had talked to six months earlier. Because he asked how it held up instead of whether they were still interested, the customer felt he genuinely wanted to know, and admitted the truth on their own.

The 24-hour call is a phone call, on purpose

The single touch you never downgrade to a text is the day-after reassurance. A call pre-empts buyer's remorse before it sets, because the voice is the reassurance and a text cannot carry it. The full playbook is in The 24-Hour Call. The mechanic to remember here: it is a call, every time, and you schedule the exact time before you leave the home.

Part 8 · The Discipline

Running the Cadence

The templates are a floor, not a ceiling. What separates a rep whose customers stay calm from one whose customers panic is how the cadence gets run.

Personalize every send

Fill the brackets. Their name, the real date, the town's actual timeline from the Salesforce AHJ page. A template sent raw reads as a mass blast. The same words with their details read as a person who's paying attention.

Match the channel

Status updates go by text. The 24-hour reassurance goes by phone, every time. That call is the one touch you never downgrade to a text, because the voice is the reassurance.

Schedule, don't wing it

Set the next touch before you finish the current one. The 24-hour call gets a specific time named before you leave the table. A cadence you run from memory is a cadence you'll drop the week you get busy.

Sound like you

Energy and emojis are fine; the brand uses them. The message should still sound like the same person who sat at their table, not a system update. Read it out loud before you send. If it sounds like a robot, rewrite it.

Be reachable. Half of remorse is a customer who can't get an answer. Stay responsive between touches. A customer who knows you'll pick up doesn't spiral, and doesn't go looking for a competitor to calm them down.
