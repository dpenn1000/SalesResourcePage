The Referral Engine

Take care of a small base of customers better than anyone in the industry, and they lead you to everyone they know. After a few hundred installs you should not need a single cold lead. Every install is a billboard, and install day is the loudest moment to harvest it.

Build a Business Off 50 Customers

You do not need a constant lead feed. You need a base you serve so well it becomes self-sustaining. Take care of 50 customers the way no one in this industry does, and they become the engine that replaces your calendar.

The math of a bloodline

  • 50 customers, roughly 250 connections each, is about 12,500 relationships you already sit on.
  • They lead you to the next 50, and that 50 leads you to the next. The base compounds.
  • After a few hundred installs, referrals plus self-gen should fill the calendar on their own.

No more crutches

  • Company leads and outreach are a crutch that hides the business you could build yourself.
  • Ask the hard question: if there were no leads coming tomorrow, how would you treat every customer today?
  • Cold market is the penalty for not developing your warm. A 90-minute drive to a no-show is a referral you never planted.
Mindset to carry in"All you need is 50 customers. If you take care of 50 customers in a way no one in this industry does, they lead you to everyone they know. 50 customers, 250 connections each, that is 12,500 connections. You are telling me you cannot find the next 50?"
Ask the room: count your PTO base right now. If outreach went to zero tomorrow, which 5 customers do you call first, and what do you say?
Why is 50 well-served customers enough to stop needing cold leads?

Answer50 customers carry roughly 250 connections each, about 12,500 relationships. Served relentlessly, they lead you to the next 50 and the one after that, so the referral and self-gen bloodline replaces the lead feed. Leaning on company leads is a crutch that prevents you from building that self-sustaining base.

The Install Is the Pinnacle Moment

You earn nothing on the first appointment. You get paid at install, and install day is the loudest, most emotional touchpoint a customer ever has with you. Run a fixed install-day progression every single time, because its whole job is to prove one promise: I say what I will do, and I do what I say.

Run the manifesto in order

Log a personal detail at discovery, bring a tailored gift, reserve a half hour, gift the crew, ask how communication has gone, ask for specific neighbors, capture a review, then commit to the next dated call. Same sequence, every install.

Your word is bond

Customers are quietly testing whether you keep your word. Skip the install and you get the "I thought you were going to be here" text, and you forfeit the referral. By install a customer may have talked to 9 to 13 Trinity people; be the one who stays.

You get paid at install

The first appointment pays nothing. The project pays at install, which alone makes it the one touchpoint you never miss. The pizza and the gift are trivial against the thousands the job earns and the referrals it seeds.

At the install, committing to next contact"I am going to call you next Thursday at noon. Does that work for you? Because I want to prove to you over and over: I am going to say what I am going to do, and do what I say. That is why you are dealing with me."
Ask the room: what is your fixed install-day progression? Walk it start to finish. Where do most reps quietly skip a step?
Why is install day the single touchpoint a rep can never miss?

AnswerIt is the most emotional, highest-leverage moment a customer has with you, and it is when you actually get paid (the first appointment pays nothing). A fixed install-day progression proves "I say what I will do and do what I say," and customers quietly test that. Miss it and you get the "I thought you were going to be here" text and lose the referral.

Get Your Face in Their Phone, Then Call in 24 Hours

A solar decision is emotional backed by logic, and the emotion starts dissipating the moment you walk out the door. Two cheap moves protect it: put your face in their phone so they recognize you instantly, and pre-schedule a phone call (not a text) within 24 hours to catch buyer's remorse before it festers.

Photo-text yourself in

  • Send a photo of yourself so you are a face in their phone, not a number they forget by next week.
  • Instant recognition means the next text or call lands warm instead of cold.
  • Small effort, long memory. You stay a person to them, not a vendor.

The 24-hour call, not a text

  • Pre-schedule it before you leave: "I will call you tomorrow at 6 after work, does that work?"
  • Tell them to write down questions so the call picks off concerns before they grow.
  • Make it a call. Voice catches the emotion and the doubt that a text never surfaces.
The result: reps who ran the photo-text plus the pre-scheduled 24-hour call moved their pull-through from roughly 50% to 78%. The emotion you protect in the first day is the deal that survives to the roof.
Leaving the home, setting the next call"I am going to call you within 24 hours. Write down any questions that pop into your head between now and then. This is a big decision, and I want to be the one who answers every question, not leave you Googling at midnight."
Ask the room: who pre-schedules the 24-hour call by name and time before leaving? What changed in your pull-through when you started?
Why a phone call within 24 hours instead of a text a few days later?

AnswerThe sale is emotional backed by logic, and the emotion fades the moment you leave. A pre-scheduled call within 24 hours catches buyer's remorse and concerns before they fester, and voice surfaces doubt a text never will. Paired with photo-texting yourself into their phone for instant recognition, it lifted pull-through from about 50% to 78%.

The Apology-Tour Install Knock

When the crew trucks are in the driveway, the whole street is watching. Knock the visible neighbors and lead with an apology for the noise, not a pitch. The company presence behind you supplies most of the legitimacy that cold canvassing has to earn from scratch.

Knock as the business

  • Lead with the apology: "Sorry for the noise today, we are putting in your neighbor's system."
  • The trucks do ~80% of the legitimacy work that cold canvassing has to build from nothing.
  • The knock-to-lead ratio collapses: cold it takes 25 to 30 doors for a lead, here it is 8 to 10.

Make them come to you

  • Step back two paces from the door so the curious neighbor has to come out to see what is going on.
  • Neighbors are nosy. "Have you seen what is going on over here?" starts the conversation for you.
  • Pre-knock the night before if you can, to learn who is actually home and when to return.
At the neighbor's door on install day"Hi, I am with Trinity, we are putting in your neighbor's system today. I wanted to apologize for the noise and the trucks. While I am here, have you seen what is going on over there? A lot of folks on this street have been asking."
Ask the room: on your last install, how many neighbor doors did you knock? What stops most reps from knocking when the trucks are right there?
Why does the install-day knock convert so much better than cold canvassing?

AnswerYou knock as the business with the business visibly behind you, so the company presence does about 80% of the legitimacy work cold canvassing has to earn. Leading with an apology for the noise, not a pitch, lands as courtesy. The knock-to-lead ratio drops from 25 to 30 doors cold down to roughly 8 to 10.

Ask Permission, and Ask Referrals For Them

Most reps ask for referrals like a favor to themselves, and that is exactly why they do not get them. Set the cadence early, then frame every ask around the customer's benefit, not yours. The referral is a second sale, built on emotion, that also reinforces the first.

Ask permission first

At the first meeting, ask how and how often they want you to stay in touch. Call monthly? Quick text? A drive-by? Then narrate the pipeline (approval, install date, inspection, PTO screenshot) as natural reasons to reach out.

Ask FOR them, not for you

"We are not asking referrals for ourselves, we are asking for them." When it comes off like it is for you, people hold back. Frame it as the same help you just gave: a lower bill for a neighbor they care about.

Knock their first one for them

"I will get your first $1,000 referral done for you on install day." Now every referral they send afterward is worth $2,000, and they are already thinking past the finish line.

Setting up the referral, install day"I am going to knock your street on install day and get your first referral out of the way for you, so everybody you send me after that is the full amount. You did not just lower your own bill, you can lower your neighbor's too, and that is exactly why you signed up."
Ask the room: say your referral ask out loud. Is it framed for you or for them? Rewrite it live so it is for them.
What is the difference between asking referrals for you versus for them?

AnswerAsking for yourself sounds like a favor and people hold back. Asking for them frames the referral as the same help they just received: a lower bill for someone they care about, which also reinforces their own decision (the second sale buttons up the first). Offering to knock their first one out for them on install day proves you work for them and gets them thinking past the finish line.

Re-engage the Silent and the Lost

A customer going quiet for years is almost always life, not avoidance. Stop waiting on a text reply and get in the car. The same is true for deals you lost to a competitor: a low-stakes check-in keeps you in the relationship until their other deal wobbles.

Get in the car and knock

  • Years of silence is usually a life change (a move, a divorce, a new number), not anger at you.
  • Drive over and knock: "I was in the neighborhood, how is your solar?" One door produced a same-day referral.
  • Any relationship is repairable. Apologize for being out of touch and rebuild it. It is never truly broken.

Stay in play on lost deals

  • They signed with a competitor, not the grave. A no-pressure "just seeing how your project is going" costs nothing.
  • They are going solar anyway. Half a neighborhood converts over a decade regardless, so it might as well be you who earns the check.
  • Welcome the problem. When you hear "I am having an issue," lean in, give free value first, and the referral follows.
Re-engaging a lost deal, low pressure"No pressure at all, I just wanted to see how your solar project is going. If anything ever feels off or you have a question, I am still right here for you. Honestly, half this neighborhood is going solar over the next few years anyway, so I want you taken care of either way."
Ask the room: name one customer who went silent and one deal you lost. What is the lowest-pressure reason you could give to reach out this week?
How do you re-open a customer who has gone silent for years?

AnswerAssume it is life, not avoidance (a move, a divorce, a new number), and get in the car instead of waiting on a text: "I was in the neighborhood, how is your solar?" Apologize for being out of touch and rebuild the relationship, because it is never truly broken. For lost deals, stay in play with a no-pressure check-in and the "you are going solar anyway, might as well be you" frame.

Every Install Is a Billboard

Every PTO customer is advertising for you or against you, every day. Manufacture memorable touches so the billboard stays a raving fan, and treat the angry "my bill went up" customer as your next add-on, not a problem to dodge.

The SolarEdge app as a daily touchpoint

Get installed customers onto the SolarEdge app, richer than the finance portal. They check it daily, so you are with them every time they open it, they have something real to show neighbors, and you can re-educate them on how good their rate is. One rep generated 10-plus closed referrals in a few months this way.

Manufacture memorable touches

Frame a photo of the family in front of their finished system and bring it back. Write down their birthday at credit-run and stop by on the day. No utility company calls a customer on their birthday, which is exactly why it works.

The angry customer is your next sale

"My bill went up, my solar is not working" is wrong 8 to 9 times out of 10. Usage changed, rates changed, or they are comparing this April to last April. Read the bill correctly first, then turn that same upset customer into an add-on and a fan.

Take the hard call first

The call you least want to make is the one to take first. Being there when something goes wrong is what makes you their guy. Most escalations are tiny in dollars and huge in feeling: the real complaint is "my voice is not being heard."

Handling "my bill went up," staying their guy"I hear you, and I am glad you called me first. Let us read this bill together before we assume anything, because nine times out of ten something changed in the house or the rates, not the system. Either way, I am the one who is going to figure it out with you."
Stay honest and humble. When something looks wrong, fall on the sword and figure it out with the customer. Never tell a customer they were lied to, and never promise a specific bill outcome. Lead with reading the actual bill correctly, then let the facts settle them.
Role-play: a PTO customer calls angry that their bill went up. Take the call live, read the bill, and turn it toward an add-on.
Why is an angry "my bill went up" customer an opportunity, not a problem?

Answer8 to 9 times out of 10 it is not the system: usage changed, rates changed, or they are comparing different months. Reading the bill correctly resolves the fear, and that same customer (whose real complaint is "my voice is not being heard") becomes an add-on and a raving billboard once you show up and figure it out with them. Take the hard call first, because being there when it goes wrong is what makes you their guy.
