Part 1 · The Reframe

You are talking to a homeowner, not a solar customer

Walk in thinking "solar appointment" and you fish the smallest pond. The same family has a roof that ages and a grid that fails. Storage and roofing are the larger markets and the problems they feel first.

"If you walk in only talking about solar, you are casting the smallest net. The bigger buckets are the ones that help you sell the rest."Ken Ebbert · Coffee with Closers

Why the battery mindset wins more jobs

  • Bigger market, bigger need. Storage and roofing dwarf residential solar. The demand is already in the home before you arrive.
  • Saves dead leads. Door resistance like "I do not want solar" is not a no to backup power. Pivot: "We are not here to sell you solar. We are here to talk batteries and your roof."
  • A different sale. Backup is a visible, physical product. Walk the house, find the meter, picture the battery. Solar is a math sale; backup is a peace-of-mind sale.
  • Diversifies the deal. More products lower our cost per customer and grow the contract, even when the family takes only one.
The pivot line. When the notes say "did not want the solar appointment," you still have a reason to be there. Lead with the roof and the battery, and the door opens.
Ask the room: what is the last deal that died on "not interested in solar" that a battery or roof could have saved?

Why the higher number lands

Some families are doing fine. Saving $60 is nice, but it does not move them. Backup security does. The team keeps finding the $350 home is more appealing than the $240 home for those buyers.

Say"Our standard package is solar plus two Powerwalls, so the whole house keeps running when the grid does not. Why would you not want that? If you would rather, we can pull the batteries back out and just do the solar."
Lead high. Present the self-powered home as the standard, then subtract if needed. The battery is in the room from the first number.
Lead low. Quote solar-only first, then try to add $150 a month for a battery. That extra now feels like a penalty.

Part 3 · Trinity Total Home

Good, better, best: three real choices

Frame every consultation in the Total Home language you already use. Three tiers give the customer a choice of yes instead of a single yes-or-no, and the top tier gives the rest room to land.

Best

Trinity Total Home

Roof + Battery + Solar

Handle the future roof now, beat the cost of waiting, and add backup. One crew, one plan, one number.

Better

Peace-of-Mind Package

Battery + Solar

Skip the roof for now. With the CT program this strong, the battery is close to free to add. Hard to pass up in good conscience.

Good

Trinity Solar

Solar only

The a-la-carte option. Real savings, but they are leaving value on the table. "You do not want the standard? That is fine."

Why three tiers beats one offer

  • Decompression. When the top option is the priciest, "no" to the battery becomes "yes" to solar instead of "no" to everything.
  • They leave with something. Like a suit shop: not every customer buys the jacket, but they walk out with a belt and a tie. Multi-product means the house is rarely a zero.
  • Solar can be the add-on. Sell the roof or the battery first, and solar becomes the easy attachment on top.
  • Bigger, better deals. One rep booked 4 batteries on 7 deals in a month just by presenting all three every time.
Standard, then a-la-carte. Total Home is the standard you present. "We are roof, battery, and solar. You can go a-la-carte, but you would be doing yourself a disservice."
Have each rep say their good/better/best out loud in 30 seconds, in Total Home language. Where does the top tier feel bolted on instead of standard?
Recall What does the highest tier do for the sale, even when they decline it?

It decompresses the decision. Saying no to the top option lets them say yes to a middle one, so they leave with something instead of nothing.

Part 4 · Discovery

The questions that close a battery

You cannot sell backup you have not made them feel. Build the benefit before you ever name the cost. Ask, then ask the next question, until the outage is personal.

Walk them into the outage

  • "When the power goes out, what is the first thing you do?"
  • "Does it tend to happen in the summer, or the winter?"
  • "What happens to the fridge and freezer when it is out for a day?"
  • "Anyone in the home on medical equipment, or working from home?"
  • "How do your days change when you lose power for two or three of them?"
"I finally realized they were not losing power in the winter. It was every summer, and four years in a row they lost the food in their fridge. The battery plus the spoiled food each year? You are going to come out ahead. They signed right then."Chris Wyant · Coffee with Closers

"I want my bill as low as possible."

Lay out every option with pros and cons, then let them choose. People who say "lowest bill" often pick more once they see the value. Do not decide for them. Scared money makes no money, and stuffing product down a throat raises your decay rate. Show, do not shove.

Earn it before you cost it. Most reps who skip multi-product are afraid of the cost or never built the benefit. Do the discovery first. Value minus cost equals value, and the value has to exist in their head before the price shows up.
Ask the room: what is the one discovery question that gets a customer most emotional about losing power? Why that one?
Recall What has to happen before you name the battery's price?

Build the benefit. Walk them through their own outage history until the loss is specific and felt. Value has to exist in their mind before the cost lands.

Part 5 · The Value Frame

A battery is insurance that pays you back

Families spend $800 to $1,000 a month on insurance they may never use, all for peace of mind. For a fraction of that, a battery protects them from real losses and can put money back in their pocket. That is a better deal than any policy they own.

$2,000Food lost in the fridge and freezer in a long outage
$600A night in a hotel during a hurricane, times several nights
$10,000Water damage when the sump pump dies in a basement
1 to 2Days of lost work when there is no power to work from home

What backup actually protects

  • The food and the freezer through every multi-day outage, not just the scary ones.
  • The basement when the sump pump needs power it does not have.
  • Work and income for anyone running their day from home.
  • The medical and the comfort load, the equipment and the heat or AC that cannot just stop.
  • The rate structure ahead, the time-of-use and tiered pricing already live in California and coming here.

Feel, felt, found

For the family that calls it a luxury:

Say"I hear you, it can feel like an extra. A lot of folks I have helped felt the same way. What they found is that the first time the grid went down and their house stayed on, the battery stopped feeling like a cost and started feeling like the smartest thing they bought."
Frame the incentive honestly. The CT program pays an upfront incentive plus ongoing dispatch value, but the dollars depend on the home and the program rules. Use the ESS calculator for real figures. Never promise a guaranteed monthly check. Sell the protection and the avoided losses, and let the incentive be the bonus.
Ask the room: which avoided-loss number lands hardest in your towns, the food, the hotel, the basement, or the lost workdays?

Part 6 · The Emotional Close

Solve it for future you, and lock it in now

Solar is the logical sale. The battery is the emotional one, and that is its strength. Put the family in the future moment when the power is out, then let them decide today whether they want to be the house that stayed on. And get it on the lease now, while the coverage runs the full 25 years.

Put them in the future moment

The battery sits idle most of the year, and that is fine. Name it, then walk them to the night the grid goes down and they look at each other and wonder if they should have checked the box. You are not selling a gadget. You are solving a problem for the version of them that has not had the outage yet.

Say"You will lose power at some point. When you do, you will look at each other and think, did we make a mistake? Decide right now whether you want to be the house running on backup, and let me solve that for future you."

Insurance you are glad to never use

Most families spend around $1,000 a month on insurance that only pays out after something bad happens. For roughly $150 a month, a battery prevents the bad thing instead of reimbursing it. That is the better side of the trade.

Say"I pay to insure my golden retriever and I hate it, because he is fine. Then he tore his ACL chasing a cat and they covered $8,000, and I was glad I had it. That is what this is. You hope you never need it, and you are thankful the day you do."
The reframe in one line. You already spend around $1,000 a month on insurance that pays you back only after the loss. Spend a fraction of that on the one thing that prevents the loss, the outage that will leak into your daughter's room or spoil the freezer, and you come out ahead.

Lease it now, not as a retrofit later

  • Now: 25 years, all in. Add the battery on the lease today and it is covered for the full 25 years right alongside the solar. One agreement, one coverage window.
  • Later: 10 years, on your own. A retrofit after the fact means a 10-year warranty, a weaker loan, and no one in your corner once it lapses. Same hardware, half the protection.
  • The escalator does the heavy lifting. The 2.9 escalator on the lease carries a battery that would otherwise only carry a 10-year warranty across the whole 25 years. The math only works in your favor if it goes on today.
Say"If you are even thinking about a battery, the time is now. On the lease it is 25 years all in. Wait and retrofit it later and you get a 10-year warranty and you are on your own. Same battery, do it now and you are covered the whole way."
Role-play the future-you close on a customer who calls the battery a luxury. Where does it stop feeling like a pitch and start feeling like you are protecting them?

The outage is coming, so decide before it does and protect the version of them that has not lived it yet. On the lease the battery is covered for 25 years and the 2.9 escalator carries it the whole way; a later retrofit is a 10-year warranty on their own.

Part 7 · The CT Window

It will never be better than it is today

Connecticut's storage program is the strongest it has ever been, and the most money in any program is always available early. Urgency here is real and specific, not the vague "rates always go up."

Why now is the window

  • Best money is early. Incentive budgets are richest at the start of a program and shrink as they fill. Waiting a year is leaving real dollars behind.
  • A better program than before. The new dispatch model gives the customer more control than the old one did. This version is worth getting into.
  • Demand is outrunning supply. AI and electrification are driving consumption up fast on a New England grid never built for it. New power plants take years, and almost none are being filed.
  • Incentive today, mandate tomorrow. Governments incentivize first, then stop, then require. The golden years are now.

The grid story, simply

Solar is a peak-load solution, not a total one. Pair it with storage and you cover peak and base load both.

The utility has two choices: build trillion-dollar battery farms far away and fight congestion, or put a battery on every house and feed the grid from the street. The home battery is the better answer, and the state is paying to build it.

Say"You are not just protecting your house. On the hottest days, your battery helps feed the block. That is why the state is paying so well to put one in right now."
Make inaction specific. Tie the cost of waiting to this customer: the incentive dollars that shrink next cycle, and the bill that keeps climbing on their own statement. Skip the generic "rates go up." For the program mechanics, eligibility, and the calculator, send them and yourself to the CT ESS Program Training and the Grid Edge Checker.
Ask the room: what is the specific cost of waiting one program cycle for a customer in your territory? Name the dollars, not the slogan.

Incentive money is richest early and shrinks as budgets fill, and the program will not stay this generous. Tie it to the specific dollars this customer loses by waiting, plus their own climbing bill.

Part 8 · Handle the Cost

The dragon to slay is the fear of the price

Most reps lose battery sales they never lost, because they never offered. The trepidation is yours, not the customer's. When you lead with price, value drops. When you build value first, the price has somewhere to land.

"That is too expensive, I just want the cheapest option."

Reframe to value, do not defend the number. A $1 pen and a $100 pen look the same until the $100 one grants any wish you write. Cost is not the question; value is. When value exceeds price, people buy every time. Lay out the avoided losses again, then let them choose.

"I will just add the battery later if I need it."

Name the cost of later. Customers who buy solar-only and call back a month after the next outage pay more and wait longer, and competitors call them first. Build the value while you are in the home, the day you are there, not after their heart gets broken in the next storm.

"I need to talk to my spouse before we spend that much."

Both decision-makers, or sell the present one the concept and lock a dated go-back. Skipping this is where most of these cancel. Treat "let me think" as a trust shield, not a no, and answer the real worry underneath it.

Lead with the battery, let solar attach. If you can sell the roof or the battery, solar becomes the easy slide on top. Lose the trepidation, build the muscle, and stop talking yourself out of the sale before the customer ever does.
Role-play the "I will add it later" objection. How do you make the cost of waiting specific without pressuring? Where does it tip into pushy?
Recall Who usually kills the battery sale?

The rep, by not offering it out of fear of the cost. Build value before price, present every product, and let the customer decide.
