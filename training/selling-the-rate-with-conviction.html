Part 1 · The Foundation

Mindset — Where It All Starts

Belief is upstream of every technique. If you treat the rate as a problem, it becomes one. If you believe what you're selling, the customer follows.

"The customer will never be more convinced than you are. Their confidence is a direct mirror of yours." The Mirror Effect

Five rules that change everything

  • Your conviction transfers. Treat the rate as a problem and it becomes one. State it as fact, move on.
  • Don't apologize. Hesitation creates a flag the customer wasn't going to raise. Subconscious apologies kill deals.
  • Don't bend. When you hit resistance, make your case stronger — never drop the rate. Reducing it confirms their instinct that something was off.
  • Don't create the objection. Full belief means rate concerns rarely surface unprompted.
  • It's a problem when you make it a problem. It's not a problem when you don't.

Presenting from Fear

  • You're unsure they're truly convinced
  • You go lower hoping the number saves it
  • Body language shows hesitation
  • You signal the price wasn't fully justified
  • Your nervousness becomes their nervousness

Presenting from Conviction

  • You built real value in discovery
  • You know solar is the right answer for them
  • You believe in the long-term picture
  • You present with calm confidence
  • The customer feels it's fair and earned

The Premium-Brand Rule

A Mercedes salesperson never apologizes for not being a Toyota. And they never sell a Mercedes at a Toyota price — because that signals "you're not really a Mercedes."

Trinity is the premium brand. If we offered 0%, something would be wrong somewhere. Our transparent rate is our credibility.

Part 2 · Earn the Right

Discovery — Earn the Rate Conversation

The rate conversation is won or lost before you ever say "2.99." If discovery is thin, the rate becomes the headline. If discovery is deep, the rate is the final piece.

The Five Pillars of Discovery

If you can't answer all five before you present, you haven't earned the right to prescribe.

  • The Pain — what do they hate about the bill?
  • The Fear — what are they protecting against?
  • The Vision — what does the solution feel like?
  • The Timeline — how long do they plan to stay? What's the urgency?
  • The Decision — who makes it, and are they here?

Pain-Anchoring Questions That Do the Work

"How long have you been frustrated with your Eversource bill?"
"What's your highest electric bill in the last year?"
"Where do you think your bill will be in 10–15 years?"
"If rates doubled again in the next 10 years, how would that impact your household?"
"What would it mean to have a predictable, fixed energy cost each month?"

Translate Their Language Before Solar Comes Up

Most customers haven't told you they were worried about rate. We focused them there. Get them fluent in big percentages on every other rising cost first.

  • They say "double" → echo back "100% increase"
  • They say "triple" → "200% increase"
  • They say "quadruple" → "300% increase"

Now when you reach the rate conversation, the language is already theirs. Big percentages feel concrete. The 2.9% cap feels small.

Make small numbers big and big numbers small. Most customers fixate on the rate because we put it in front of them too early. Build the pain on the bill — the rate becomes the answer, not the problem.

Timing — When the Rate Comes Up

The rate should not enter the conversation until the customer has mentally said yes to:

  • The equipment
  • The service model
  • Trinity as the company
  • The decision-making framework

If the rate comes up before they've said yes to those — you've handed them a stall. By the time you get there, if you've done the job, they're not buying the rate. They're buying Trinity.

Part 3 · The Moment of Truth

How You Reveal the Rate

Control the narrative. Don't let them discover it as a surprise. The way you say the number determines how they hear it.

Bring it up before they do

Pause. Look at them. Then own it.

"I want to be upfront with you about something. Our rate is not 0%. The reason I'm bringing it up is because I believe in it and I can explain it to you."

By proactively raising it, you control the narrative. They feel you're being honest — not hiding something. That's where credibility comes from.

Anchor high before the number lands

Reference equipment quality, the warranty, service history, total cost of ownership — before the rate appears. Properly anchored, the 2.9% feels like a bargain.

Anchoring is the most powerful move in your toolkit. The first number a customer hears becomes the reference point for every number that follows.

Vocabulary discipline — words matter

Don't say "escalator." That word implies going up.

Say "capped at 2.9% — half a penny a year."

Frame the cap as protection, not as an increase. You're not asking them to accept a rate that climbs. You're offering them a ceiling no other utility provides.

Reframe the rate as a qualification

During the credit step, build it up. When credit comes back clean, celebrate it.

"You qualified for the 2.9% — not the 3.9 or the 4.9. You must have great credit, or this must be a really good site. Congratulations."

High-five. Fist-bump. Sign. The rate is no longer a price tag — it's a status they earned.

Prescribe one solution — not a menu

The rate becomes negotiable when there are three options to compare. One prescribed solution removes the comparison entirely.

"Based on everything you've shared — your usage, your goals, your timeline — I'm going to recommend the best solution for you and your family."
"Not every homeowner qualifies for this program. For your situation, it's the right call."
"I'd be doing you a disservice giving you a bunch of options to sort through tonight."

Information is not integrity. Confidence is. If we don't know what's best for them after our training, how can they know after a 30-minute consult?

Don't Over-Explain the Rate

  • Three minutes on financing
  • Defending the number
  • Justifying every basis point
  • Talking yourself into objections

One Sentence, Then Payment

  • Name the rate as fact
  • Move directly to monthly payment
  • Payment is what they live with
  • Rate is just how we got there
Two valid delivery modes: matter-of-fact (no tone change, just present it) or celebratory (lean in, get excited). Both work. What kills you is hesitation in between.
Part 4 · Proof on Paper

The Math Wins When You Show It

Numbers actually win when you put them on paper in front of the customer. Don't tell them — show them.

"Where does the 0% come from?"

This is the most powerful single question in a competitive situation. Either ask it for them, or hand them the question to ask the competitor.

"That's a great question — you should ask. If a company is offering 0%, the financing cost is baked into a higher system price. The customer is paying it either way; one company is just hiding where it lives."

The 25-year side-by-side

Trinity at 2.9% versus competitor at 0% — over 25 years, on total dollar outlay, we win almost every time. Especially in NJ and CT. The math doesn't lie.

Pull out the system cost. Pull out the loan amount. Pull out the year-25 total. Lay them side by side. Let the numbers do the work.

Napkin Math — Draw It Live

Line down the middle of a notepad. Left side: a generic 0% competitor. Right side: Trinity at 2.9%.

Fill in system cost · loan amount · monthly payment · year-25 total — in front of them, with your own pen.

Watching you write it builds trust no pre-loaded slide can match. It's not a graphic. It's live math happening in their kitchen.

Zoom out to total cost of ownership

Frame against doing-nothing — the utility cost over 25 years. Now the rate becomes one variable in a much bigger picture, instead of the headline.

When zoomed out, the Trinity number is almost always lower than the do-nothing number. Sometimes dramatically lower. The rate becomes almost irrelevant — they're saving money either way.

Fixed vs. Escalator — End-State Equivalence

If the customer is comparing fixed vs. escalator pricing, give them the secret:

"At year 25, it's all the same. You spend the same money either way. So the question is — do you want to front-load it or not?"

That single line defuses the entire fixed-vs-escalator debate.

"Why are you scared to sell the rate? Know your fixed price and your escalator price cold — both make money. The rate is only a problem if you make it one."
Part 5 · The Language

Reframes & Analogies

Translate the rate into language homeowners already understand. The right analogy makes the number disappear.

Insurance Policy

0% means cost is hidden somewhere — cheaper equipment, padded system price, or a company on thin margins. Our 2.9% is the insurance that we'll be there in year 15 when your system needs service.

Long-Term Investment

The cheapest stock or the cheapest house is rarely the best investment. Quality always comes with a premium. Solar is the most important energy decision they'll make for 25 years — frame it like one.

Locked-In Rate

Utility rates rise. Yours doesn't move for the life of the loan. The predictability is the product. They're not buying solar — they're buying control.

Cup of Coffee

"Do you want an $80–$90 utility increase next year, or a $6 increase that costs the same as your daily coffee?" Translates the abstract number into something everyone understands.

Rate-Protection Urgency

Trinity's own rates have moved before. Locking in today's rate protects them against the next Trinity rate change too. Real urgency, real lever.

The First-Home Anchor

For older customers who don't believe rates will rise: "What did you pay for your first house?" The shock unlocks their own life experience as proof that prices climb relentlessly.

"Solar is your customer's personal utility. Simple. Predictable. Theirs. The utility company is exposed to 12+ external volatility factors — your solar system isn't exposed to any of them."
Part 6 · The Real Fear

Trinity Is the Real Insurance Policy

Customers fear making a bad decision far more than they fear a higher rate. The rate objection is almost always a trust objection in disguise.

Reframe rate → risk

The customer's biggest fear is not the rate. It's that they're going to choose the wrong company.

Move the conversation from "rate" to "risk and peace of mind." Now you're competing on something no 0% offer can match.

32 Years & Counting

"We were here before a lot of these companies existed, and we'll be here after a lot of them are gone."

Many competitors haven't even outlived their own equipment warranty. Trinity has.

Healthy Margins = Service in Year 15

A company at 0% with no margin can't be there when the system needs work. Our rate is what funds our ability to show up.

"I just ran into a rep last Sunday who left Trinity for a 0% competitor. They've had two rounds of layoffs this year. He wishes he never left."

Third-Party Credibility

When a customer is anxious about choosing the wrong company, hand them independent proof.

  • BBB accreditation — A+ rating
  • 4.8 stars on Google with thousands of reviews
  • Decades in business through every market cycle
"Yes, we cost a little more. And here's exactly why that extra cost is worth it. We're not just selling solar — we're selling the company that will still be here in year 25."
The real value isn't saving money. It's control. Predictability. The guarantee that the $500 summer bill won't become a $1,000 summer bill that crushes the family. Sell that, not pennies per kWh.
Part 7 · When Push Comes

Objection Handling

Respond with questions, not rebuttals. Curiosity, not defense. The objection contains the answer.

"Your rate is too high."

Don't Drop the rate immediately. Defend the number.

Do Respond with curiosity. "That's a fair concern — can you help me understand what you're comparing it to?" Most of the time, they don't actually know — or it's a 0% offer you can dismantle in 90 seconds.

"I need to think about it."

Don't "No problem, take your time." (Then leave with no plan.)

Do "Think about it" is rarely about time — it's unresolved doubt. "I hear you. Help me understand — what's the main thing on your mind?" or "What would need to be true for you to feel confident moving forward today?"

"What if rates go down?"

Don't "They probably won't." (Dismissive, confrontational.)

Do "That's a fair question — and exactly what we should explore. Where do you think rates will be in 15 years? Let's look at what the utilities themselves are saying." Get them to answer it themselves.

"Another company quoted me less."

Don't "I'll match it" or "they're cutting corners."

Do "Every project is unique. What I'd like to do is walk the home with you, get photos of your meter, panel, and attic — make sure we have an accurate picture. Then I'll call my manager and we'll see what we can do." Earn the right to revisit. Don't drop reflexively.

"This feels like a lot."

Do "What would make you feel comfortable?"

This single question shifts the burden to them. They have to articulate what it would take. Most of the time they don't have a great answer — and they talk themselves out of the objection.

The Concept-Not-Negotiation Reframe

When a customer pushes back with a competitor's number, don't negotiate. Reframe to a concept conversation.

"Once I figure out your exact production and put it into the system, it'll tell me what your price would be today. I'm just trying to make sure the concept makes sense to you."

If they say "another company offered me 27¢":

"Yep, that's possible. It depends on your site. I have to put all that into the computer. Based on your site's performance, it'll tell me what price you can get."

You're not negotiating — you're educating. The site/production unknown defuses every counter.

Counter the Escalator Competitor

If the homeowner has a quote with a fixed-rate competitor:

"Same equipment, same EPC — just 4 cents difference whether escalator or fixed. I'll offer you the same program 2 cents less than they did. Would you rather save this year, or save next year?"

Often this lets you write a higher EPC than the competitor — because they revealed their price first.

The Trust Check

When resistance feels personal — when the rate objection is too heavy for what's actually being asked — pause. Ask directly.

"Do you feel like Trinity is a company you can trust?"

This often surfaces the real objection. The rate was a proxy for "I'm not sure about your company." Address that, and the rate dissolves.

Part 8 · Holding the Line

Negotiation Discipline

What you negotiate matters as much as whether you negotiate. The lever is the kWh rate, not the financing rate.

Wrong Move

  • Open below your floor
  • Drop the financing rate
  • Discount because the customer pushed
  • Race competitors to the bottom

Right Move

  • Hold the kWh rate at the open
  • If you need flexibility, move the kWh rate
  • Keep the 2.9% financing intact
  • Earn the discount through site/walk-around

Why this matters for the whole team

Underselling at the open eliminates margin and trains the customer base to expect lower numbers. The next rep who knocks that street has been undercut — by you.

"You're hurting everybody around you, not just yourself."

The Walk-Around Close

When a competitor comes in lower, don't drop the price. Earn the right to revisit it.

1

Acknowledge

"I understand you've received another quote. Every project is unique, and pricing depends on factors specific to your home. I can't speak to what another company may or may not be including in their number."

2

Differentiate

"What I can tell you is that we prioritize transparency. We don't want to call you three months from now saying we found a structural issue and the price has to go up. That's not how we operate."

3

Inspect

"Let's walk your home together right now to get photos of the meter, main panel, and attic structure. If I'm confident there won't be additional scope, I'll get my manager on the phone and see what we can do."

4

Reframe

"This gives us both confidence. If the home qualifies cleanly, there may be flexibility. But I'd rather be honest with you now than promise a price and come back with problems. That's what integrity looks like."

Reframe the competition entirely. Compete on expertise, transparency, and risk reduction — not dollars per watt. The moment you compete on price, solar becomes a commodity and you lose your differentiation.
