Win the Comparison

The customer is going to shop. Good. You do not win the comparison on the shingle or the price. You win it on what no cheaper competitor can match: a company still here in 25 years, that answers the phone, and that proves it cares before they ever sign.

You Are Your Customer's Best Reference

When a customer asks for references, do not get defensive. Concede the obvious: any company can hand-pick its three happiest customers. Then pivot to the proof a competitor cannot fake.

What three references actually prove

  • Nothing durable. Every company, good or bad, has three thrilled customers it can point you to.
  • Hand-picked is not proof. A cherry-picked list tells the homeowner what the company wants them to hear, not what is true.
  • It dodges the real question: will this company still be standing when the roof needs them.

What actually proves Trinity

  • Results at volume: roughly 15,000 homes a year, not a handful you were handed.
  • Employee tenure: people stay decades here, not the industry's roughly 24 months. That is the tell.
  • The logic the customer feels: a company that treats its own people this well is the one that will treat them right too.
In the home"Any company can give you the 3 happiest customers they ever had. I will not do that. What makes us who we are is the results, and how long our people stay. We have folks here 20-plus years, when the industry average is about 2. A company that takes care of its own people that long, I never worry about how they treat you."
Ask the room: a customer demands references before they will talk numbers. How do you concede the point and pivot to tenure in two sentences, without sounding rehearsed?
Why is employee tenure a stronger reference than three happy customers?

AnswerAny company can produce three thrilled customers, so the list proves nothing durable. Employee tenure (people staying decades vs the industry's roughly 24 months) cannot be faked and signals that a company treating its own people well will treat the customer well too.

Profitability Is a 25-Year Promise

A roof warranty is a promise to show up for 25 years. A company that is not profitable is making a promise it already knows it cannot keep. This is how you turn "the other guy was cheaper" into "the other guy may not be there."

Cheaper is not always an advantage

The competitors who folded did not have an unfair edge. They had an unaffordable price and rep pay they could not sustain. The low number was the warning sign, not the deal.

The 5-year reality

Roughly 90% of roofers are gone within 5 years. A warranty is only as alive as the company behind it. When the contractor is gone, so is the coverage.

Why profit is protection

A company has to make money to keep its word for 25 years. Profit is not greed here, it is the thing that lets the promise survive long enough to matter.

Rep · handling the price"It is not that they have an unfair advantage. It is an unaffordable price. Every roof we put on is a 25-year promise to show up. If a company is not making money, they are making a promise they already know they cannot keep. About 9 in 10 roofers are gone in 5 years. The cheap one is the one most likely to disappear."
Feel, felt, found: "I get why the lower number feels safer. A lot of homeowners felt that way, then found the cheaper company gone when they finally needed the warranty. The promise only counts if the company is still here to keep it."
Ask the room: how do you make the point that an unprofitable competitor is a risk, without it sounding like you are bashing them or guessing about their books?
Why does an unusually low competitor price actually weaken their warranty?

AnswerA 25-year warranty requires the company to still exist in 25 years, which requires profit. An unaffordable price and unsustainable pay are why roughly 90% of roofers are gone within 5 years, so the cheapest bid is often the one most likely to leave the homeowner with a dead warranty.

Quality, Price, Service: Pick the Trade-Off

Every contractor offers three things: quality, price, and service. Nobody is best at all three at once, because the math does not work. Hand the homeowner that frame and let them choose which one they are willing to give up.

The triangle, said out loud

  • Quality, price, service: pick the 2 you want most.
  • Nobody delivers best quality and best service at the lowest price. If someone claims all three, one of them is not real.
  • The cheapest bid is buying down quality or service, usually both, even if the quote does not say so.

Where Trinity chooses to live

  • Best quality and best service, at a fair price that is not the lowest on the block.
  • That trade-off is deliberate, because the parts you cannot see are the parts that fail a cheap roof.
  • Apologize once, not for 25 years: the price stings today, bad quality stings for the life of the roof.
In the home"Any roofer can give you 2 of 3: quality, price, service. Nobody gives you the best quality and the best service at the lowest price. The math does not work. So I will be honest with you. I would rather apologize to you once today, for a price you wish were lower, than apologize for the next 25 years for quality and service you have to live with."
Role-play: the homeowner says "your quality is great but I can get it cheaper down the road." Walk the triangle, then land the apologize-once line.
What are the three things every contractor trades between, and which does Trinity refuse to give up?

AnswerQuality, price, and service. Nobody delivers the best of all three at the lowest price, so the customer picks a trade-off. Trinity holds quality and service and accepts not being the cheapest, because "I'd rather apologize once today than for 25 years."

Name the Price Gap Before They Do

If the customer raises the price objection first, you are on defense. Put it on the table yourself, inside your own presentation, and you control the frame. You are not the cheapest, you are not the most expensive, you are the best value.

Say it first, on purpose

Build it into the company intro: "We are not the cheapest on the block, and we are not the most expensive. What we do is the best value." Now the price talk happens on your terms.

Sell the value, then the number

You are salespeople, not servicemen. Tell them what the roof is worth and why, then tell them what they pay. A roof slipped in as "here's my cost" trains them to shop on price alone.

Get into their head, not their pocket

People already pay up for value: the car in the driveway, landscaping, even coffee. Stop walking away from a sale over a small monthly gap. Match the value to how they already buy.

Rep · company presentation"Before we even look at numbers, let me be straight with you. We are not the cheapest roofer on the block, by any stretch. We are not the most expensive either. What we do is give you the best value for the service we provide. So when you compare quotes, you are comparing what each one actually buys you."
Stay honest. Frame value with facts about what your roof and warranty include. Do not claim a competitor is a scam or guarantee they will fail. Let the homeowner weigh the trade-off and reach their own conclusion.
Ask the room: where in your current presentation does the price gap come up? Is it because you raised it, or because the customer did? How do you move it earlier?
Why raise the "we're not the cheapest" point yourself instead of waiting for it?

AnswerNaming the price gap first, inside your own presentation, keeps you in control of the frame: not cheapest, not most expensive, best value. If the customer raises it first you are on defense, reacting instead of leading the comparison.

Who Answers the Phone in Year 12?

A warranty is a phone number. The only question that matters is whether someone picks up when the roof leaks years from now. Hand the homeowner the questions that expose whether the cheaper roofer can still answer.

Ask the other rooferWhy it changes the comparison
Are calls answered 24/7?Trinity answers around the clock and gets out in 24 to 48 hours. Many small contractors only show up on a day they do not have paid work.
Who actually does the work?Are they installing it themselves or subbing it out? Are they manufacturer-certified with a direct warranty relationship, or just buying off a shelf?
How does a claim work?With Trinity, one call and we handle everything, including filing with the manufacturer. Do they hand you a phone number and walk away?
Will the warranty outlive the owner?A 50-year-old roofer writes a 25-year warranty. Who climbs your roof when he is 75? Trinity has been here 32 years and is not going anywhere.
The through-line: You are not asking the homeowner to take your word that Trinity is better. You are handing them the questions to ask the other roofer. Most local contractors cannot answer these in their own favor, and the customer discovers the gap themselves.
Ask the room: which of these four questions does your competitor most hope the homeowner never asks? How do you plant it without sounding like an attack?
What is the single question that exposes a contractor's longevity risk?

Answer"If a 50-year-old roofer gives you a 25-year warranty, who honors it when he is 75?" It surfaces that a one-person warranty depends on one person still being in business, while Trinity at 32 years is an institution that outlives any individual roofer.

Proof Beats Any Rebuttal

Two trust objections kill comparisons: online complaints and "what happens when something goes wrong." You do not argue these. You show them. Scale the reviews with an image, and prove your service with a live phone call.

"I read some bad reviews online."

Scale it with one image. "We do about 15,000 homes a year. Picture a packed stadium of those customers, and a little circle of maybe 60 complaints in the back row. Would you rather listen to that small circle, or to the whole stadium? A review is often one rep who dropped the ball, not the company." Feel, felt, found: a lot of folks felt uneasy about reviews, then found the scale told the real story.

"How do I know you'll show up when there's a problem?"

Dial your hardest customer on speaker. Call your toughest existing customer right there in the living room, on speaker. Let them vent. They will still say "I know you'll take care of me," and they may even hand over a referral. Showing up when things go wrong cannot be argued, only proven.

Win-the-comparison checklist

  • Conceded that any company has three happy references, then pivoted to tenure.
  • Framed profitability as the thing that keeps a 25-year promise alive.
  • Walked the quality, price, service triangle and let them pick.
  • Named the price gap first: not cheapest, not most expensive, best value.
  • Handed them the four questions to ask the other roofer.
  • Scaled reviews with the stadium image and offered live proof on speaker.
Stay honest. The stadium image and the live call are about your own track record, not knocking the competitor. Never put words in another company's mouth or promise a specific claim outcome. Let real proof do the work.
Role-play: a homeowner pulls up a one-star review on their phone mid-sit. Run the stadium reframe live, then offer the speaker-phone proof.
How do you handle "I read bad reviews" without sounding defensive?

AnswerScale it with one image: roughly 15,000 homes a year is a packed stadium, against a small circle of maybe 60 complaints, and ask which they would rather listen to. A review is often one rep, not the company. For the deeper "will you show up" fear, prove it by calling your hardest customer on speaker.

  • Quality, price, service: pick the trade-off. Apologize once today, not for 25 years.
  • Name the price gap first: not the cheapest, not the most expensive, the best value.
  • A warranty is a phone number. Hand them the four questions and ask who answers in year 12.
