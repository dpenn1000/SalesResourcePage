What we install, why it works, and how the layers fit together.

Trinity sells one shingle: IKO Dynasty. Know the spec sheet cold, know the components above and below it, and you can answer any technical question a homeowner can throw at you.

Key features at a glance

ArmourZone Nailing Strip
Reinforced fabric band across each shingle. IKO invented it; GAF (StrikeZone) and Owens Corning (SureNail) are imitations. Allows fewer fasteners while maintaining a higher wind rating.
130 MPH Wind
Achieved at the standard 4-nail pattern -- competitors typically need 6 nails to hit the same number, putting more holes in the deck.
Class 4 Impact
Hail-rated. Many insurance carriers offer homeowner premium discounts for Class 4 shingles. Ask the customer to verify with their insurer.
Ceramic-Coated Granules
Color stays true. The granules don't bleach in the sun the way standard mineral coatings do. The roof looks like it did at year one.
Algae Resistance
Built-in additives. No maintenance required, no cleaning schedule to keep a warranty valid. Competitor algae warranties typically require proof of cleaning every 2 years.
True-Square Coverage
3 bundles cover exactly 100 square feet. Industry standard from competitors is 98.4 sq ft per 3 bundles -- you buy more, get less. With Dynasty, the math is honest.
The 4-nails-beats-6 talking point. A homeowner may assume 6 nails is "more secure" than 4. The opposite is true at the same wind rating. Fewer nails means fewer roof deck penetrations -- fewer potential leak points over the life of the roof. ArmourZone is what makes 4 nails enough.
Part 2 · The Components

What's Underneath the Shingle

A roof is a system. The shingle is the layer the homeowner sees -- but what fails a roof is almost always something below it. Know the four layers and you can speak intelligently to any inspector or competitor's quote.

The decking -- what holds it all up

The decking is the structural surface the roof is built on. Three common types in our market:

OSB
Oriented Strand Board. Wood strands compressed with resin under heat and pressure. Strong when dry -- but swells when wet. Compromised OSB needs replacement before a new roof goes on.
Plywood
Layered wood veneer, cross-grained. More dimensionally stable than OSB. Common on older homes. Holds up well to moisture exposure that would ruin OSB.
Space (Plank) Decking
Older homes built with individual planks rather than sheet goods. Often has gaps between boards. Requires careful evaluation -- sometimes needs a sheathing overlay before a new roof can be installed.

Ice & Water Shield

A self-adhering membrane installed at the eaves and in valleys. Seals around nails. Stops water from backing up under shingles when ice dams form. Critical in the Northeast.

Synthetic Underlayment

Replaces traditional tar paper. IKO Stormtite is what we install. Lighter, stronger, won't tear in wind during installation, and provides a secondary water barrier under the shingles.

Starter Strip

First course at the eave. Pre-applied adhesive bonds the first row of shingles in place against wind uplift. Skipping or substituting starter is a shortcut competitors take -- it's also one of the first things to fail.

Why this matters in the field. When a homeowner says "another roofer quoted me less," the difference is almost always in components, not shingles. Ask: Are they replacing bad decking or covering it? Are they using ice & water shield or just felt paper? Synthetic underlayment or 15-pound tar paper? IKO starter or rip a starter from a regular shingle? The answers tell you whether the quote is comparable.

A complete IKO system, layer by layer, from the structural deck up to the cap.

Continuous ventilation along the ridge. Works with soffit intake to drive attic airflow.

Why the system matters more than the brand

IKO is vertically integrated -- they manufacture the shingles, the underlayment, the starter strips, and the ridge cap. One standard, one quality control process, one warranty. Most competitors mix-and-match components from different manufacturers, which is exactly what you don't want when something fails and the finger-pointing starts.

Part 4 · Low-Slope & Flat

Flat Roofing Systems

Shingles need pitch to shed water. On flat or near-flat sections -- additions, dormers, garages -- we install a different system entirely. Two options depending on slope.

SBS Modified Bitumen (Mod Bit)

Two-layer membrane system. Used on low-slope sections -- 2/12 pitch and some 3/12.

  • Base layer -- self-adhering membrane bonded directly to the decking
  • Cap layer -- adhesive-backed top sheet with granulated colored surface that mimics shingles
  • 25-year manufacturer warranty on the two-layer system

Visually transitions cleanly to the shingled pitches above it.

EPDM Rolled Rubber

For dead-flat surfaces -- 0/12 and 1/12 pitch where Mod Bit can't shed.

  • Rolled out as a single piece on smaller roofs, seamed on larger
  • Requires complete removal of the existing roof down to the deck
  • New decking, then fiber board screwed down, adhesive applied, EPDM rolled out and smoothed
  • 30-year Carlisle warranty when installed to spec and registered
Flat-roof red flag. If a competitor's quote applies shingles to a section under 3/12 pitch, walk away. Shingles will leak on low slopes. Mod Bit or EPDM is the only correct call. It's a sign they're cutting corners or don't know better.
Part 5 · Why Ventilation Matters

Attic Ventilation

An unventilated attic cooks the underside of the roof. Heat trapped under the deck shortens shingle life by years and grows ice dams in winter. Ventilation is intake plus exhaust -- both are needed.

How a vented attic works

Cool air flows in through low intake vents (soffit, drip-edge), rises as it warms, and exits through high exhaust vents (ridge, gable). Continuous airflow keeps the deck temperature down in summer and prevents condensation buildup in winter. Skip intake or skip exhaust and the system stops working.

Ridge Vent

Continuous vent along the peak of the roof, hidden under the ridge cap. The primary exhaust. Most efficient when paired with soffit intake.

Soffit Vents

Perforated panels under the eaves. Primary intake. Pulls cool air in low so it can rise through the attic and exhaust at the ridge.

Gable Vents

Louvered openings in the gable ends. Cross-ventilation: cool air in one side, hot air out the other. Common on older homes; can supplement or substitute for ridge venting.

Box Vents

Static low-profile vents installed in the field of the roof. Used where ridge venting is impractical (hip roofs with limited ridge length).

Off-Ridge Vents

Wider vents installed near the ridge but not on it. Alternative when a continuous ridge vent isn't a fit.

Turbine Vents

Wind-driven exhaust. Older style, still effective. Increasingly uncommon on new installs but still seen on existing homes.

Field rule of thumb. For a vented attic to work, every penetration into the attic needs to be air-sealed -- bath fan ducts, light fixtures, top plates around walls. Otherwise conditioned air from the home leaks into the attic, defeats the ventilation, and creates the moisture problems the system was designed to prevent.
