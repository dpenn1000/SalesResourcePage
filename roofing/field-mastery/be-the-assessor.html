Be the Assessor

Reps pitch roofing the way they pitch solar, and they lose. A roof is not a savings argument. It is a fact you can see, touch, and show. Diagnose it physically, make the problem concrete, and the customer can no longer kick the can down the road.

Walk In as the Whole-Home Consultant

Your introduction sets your authority before you say a word about the roof. "The solar guy" can talk about panels. A whole-home consultant can talk about the house. Earn the second title at the door, because you cannot diagnose a roof from inside the solar box.

"Trinity Solar, here about your panels"

  • Boxes you in. You are now the solar expert and nothing more.
  • Roof talk feels like an upsell, bolted onto a solar visit.
  • No standing to assess. Why would a solar rep be looking at their decking?

"Trinity Total Home, your whole-home consultant"

  • Sets you as the home expert, not just the panel person.
  • The roof is now in scope from the first handshake.
  • Authority is established before the conversation starts, so the assessment feels expected.
At the door"Hi, I'm with Trinity Total Home, your whole-home consultant. I look at the whole picture: the roof, the energy, how the house is holding up. By the time we sit down, you already know I am not just here about panels."
Ask the room: say your normal door intro out loud. Does it make you the solar guy or the home expert? Rewrite it on the spot.
Why introduce yourself as a whole-home consultant instead of "the solar guy"?

AnswerHow you introduce yourself sets your authority. "Trinity Solar" makes you a solar-only expert, so roof talk feels like an upsell with no standing behind it. "Whole-home consultant" establishes you as the home and construction expert from the first handshake, which is the platform you need to assess and sell a roof.

"How Do You Feel About Your Roof?" Then Go Silent

Do not open with "how old is your roof." Open with feeling and history, then stop talking and let them fill the space. Most owners genuinely do not know the age, the layer count, or the last repair, and that gap is your opening.

Lead with feeling

"How do you feel about your roof?" An open question that invites worry, history, and stories. Then go silent. The first person to talk owns the answer, and it should be them.

Ask the history

"Do you know the history of this roof?" Most owners do not. Same as a car: you own it 10 years, you know when you did the tires and the transmission. There is real value in knowing the history, and most of them have none.

Why it works

The unknown is the discomfort. When they cannot answer, they feel the gap themselves. You did not tell them they have a problem. You helped them notice they do not even know.

In the home"Before we get into anything, how do you feel about your roof? [silence] And do you know the history of it, when it was last done, how many layers are up there? It's like a car. After 10 years you know when you did the tires and the transmission. With a roof, most folks have no idea, and that's worth talking about."
Ask the room: who is uncomfortable sitting in silence after a question? Drill it: ask, then count to five before anyone is allowed to speak.
Why open with feeling and history instead of "how old is your roof?"

Answer"How do you feel about your roof?" and "Do you know the history of this roof?" are open questions that surface that most owners do not know the age, layer count, or last repair. Going silent makes them sit in that gap and feel the discomfort themselves, which opens the conversation. A flat age question gets a one-word guess and closes it.

Read the Roof on the Walk-Up

You are diagnosing before you knock. The roof tells on itself from the ground, and calling out one accurate tell makes you a roofing expert in two seconds. Lead with an assumptive condition question, not a permission question.

What you see from the groundWhat it tells youWhat to say
Exposed shingle edges, granule lossFiberglass mat is showing through. Usually a 15 to 20 year roof, often older."Any idea how old this roof is? With the edges showing like that, we are usually looking at 15 to 20 years."
Curled or cupped shinglesA hot, poorly ventilated attic. Bad ventilation can cut a 30-year roof down to 5 to 8 years."That curling tells me the attic runs hot. That alone can take years off a roof."
Fish-mouthing or humped shinglesShingles set too tight on the last install. A workmanship tell."See how those are humping up? Whoever did this set them too tight."
Dark streaks or stains on the fasciaDrainage or condensation escaping where it should not."Those stains on the trim mean water is getting out somewhere it shouldn't."
On the walk-up"Any idea what condition the roof is in, any idea how old it is? If the edges of the shingles are exposed like that, more than likely it is in that 15 to 20 year range, if not older."
The move: name the tell out loud before you sit down. One accurate read on the walk-up buys you the authority to ask for the attic, the photos, and the close.
Ask the room: pull up a street-view of a real appointment. What can you call out from the ground before you ever knock?
What does an exposed shingle edge tell you, and how do you use it?

AnswerExposed edges and granule loss mean the fiberglass mat is showing through, usually a 15 to 20 year roof or older. You read it on the walk-up and lead with an assumptive condition question ("any idea how old it is? With the edges showing, we are usually looking at 15 to 20 years"), which establishes you as a roofing expert before you sit down.

Diagnose Before You Prescribe

A doctor does not hand you a diagnosis and a bill before examining you. Do not quote a roof from the kitchen table. Get up in the attic, use the moisture meter, take the photos, then come back down and report what you found. The proof is what closes, not the number.

  1. 1. Get in the attic

    Do not be lazy. The decking, the rafters, and the underside of the roof tell you what the surface hides. This is where you find the mold, the soft wood, and the daylight.

  2. 2. Use the moisture meter

    Read the wood, do not guess it. A moisture meter on the decking turns "your roof is old" into a number the customer cannot argue with.

  3. 3. Photograph everything

    Shoot it inside and out. Attic and decking, dark or cracked wood, soffits, fascia stains, vents, the surface. The camera carries the proof back to the table.

  4. 4. Come down and report

    "Here is what you have going on." Now you are a professional delivering findings, not a salesperson delivering a price.

Rep · running the inspection"You don't go to the doctor and have him give you the diagnosis and the bill before he checks you out. Give me a few minutes. I'm going up in the attic with my moisture meter and I'm taking some photos. Then I'll come back down and walk you through exactly what I found."
Ask the room: who is quoting from the table without going in the attic? Walk through the last roof you sold. Did you diagnose it or describe it?
What is the doctor analogy, and what are the four steps?

AnswerA doctor examines you before giving a diagnosis and a bill, so do not quote a roof before inspecting it. The four steps: get in the attic, use the moisture meter, take photos inside and out, then come down and report what you found. The physical proof is what closes, which is why you diagnose before you prescribe.

Create the Problem: Point at the Dip

Stop pitching roofing like solar. You are not selling savings here, you are showing a risk that is already in the house. Make the defect concrete and emotional. You control how deep and how personal it goes, so go there.

Pitching it like solar (it fails)

  • You describe the roof in numbers and abstractions.
  • Nothing is concrete, so the customer kicks the can.
  • You told, you did not show, and a roof you cannot see is easy to ignore.

Showing it like a roof (it lands)

  • You point at the specific sag and make them look up at it.
  • You make it personal: whose bedroom is under that dip?
  • You show the photos from the attic, the soft wood, the stain. Now it is real.
In the home"See those dips up there? Whose bedroom is under that? That looks like soft wood to me, it looks like it could cave. Is her bed close to where that dip is? Yeah, then we'd better talk about that. Let me show you what I found in the attic right above it."
Show, do not tell. Reps lose the roof when they try to pitch it like solar. Get strong at physically showing the customer, inside and out, why they need to make the change. The photo and the finger pointing up beat any paragraph you could say.
Ask the room: take one real roof from this week. Make it emotional in one sentence. Who is sleeping under the worst part of it?
What does "stop pitching roofing like solar" mean in practice?

AnswerSolar is a savings argument, but a roof is a physical risk you can show. Instead of describing the roof in numbers, point at the specific sag, make it personal ("whose bedroom is under that dip?"), and show the internal and external damage from your photos. When you show instead of tell, the customer cannot kick the can. When you only describe it, they will.

Peel the Onion Until You Hit a Foothold

Customers did not study for this appointment, and they never did this before. If you skip-stone across one question, you stay on the surface and they walk. Go 3 to 5 follow-ups deep on a single thread until you find an anchor point they cannot walk away from.

Surface answer: "The roof's fine, it's a little old but it's fine."

Do not accept the first answer. Peel it:

  1. "When was it last done, do you have any record of it?" (most do not)
  2. "Any leaks, stains on a ceiling, anything in the attic you've noticed?"
  3. "That dip over the back bedroom, how long has that been there?"
  4. "If water got behind that wall, who would even know until the ceiling came down?"
  5. "What happens to that room, and to her, if we wait another two winters?"
In the home"If you're skipping across the top of the water, you never get to any depth. So I go three to five questions deep on one thing until I find a foothold, an anchor point they can't walk away from. That's where the real conversation is."
Stay honest while you go deep. Show real damage and ask real questions. Do not invent a problem that is not there or guarantee an outcome you cannot promise. The dip, the moisture reading, and the photos are the truth. Let the customer reach the conclusion from what is actually on their roof.
Role-play live: a customer says "the roof's fine." Peel it 5 questions deep without sounding like an interrogation. Make each question land softer than the last.
Why go 3 to 5 questions deep instead of moving on after the first answer?

AnswerCustomers did not study for the appointment and have never done this before, so the first answer is a surface answer. Skipping across one question keeps you on top of the water. Going 3 to 5 follow-ups deep on a single thread takes you to a foothold, an anchor point they cannot walk away from, which is where the real decision lives.

The Field Manual

Key Takeaways

  • Introduce as the whole-home consultant, not the solar guy. It earns you the right to talk roof.
  • Open with "how do you feel about your roof?" and "do you know the history?" Then go silent.
  • Read the roof on the walk-up. Exposed edges mean 15 to 20 years, curling means a hot attic.
  • Diagnose before you prescribe: attic, moisture meter, photos, then report. A doctor examines first.
  • Create the problem. Point at the dip, ask whose bedroom is under it, show the damage inside and out.
  • Peel the onion 3 to 5 questions deep until you hit a foothold they cannot walk away from.
