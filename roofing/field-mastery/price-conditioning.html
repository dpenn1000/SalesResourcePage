Part 1 · The Foundation

Why Price Condition?

Most homeowners replace a roof once or twice in a lifetime. They have no reference point. If you don't set one, they will -- usually a number their neighbor paid a decade ago, or a quote they only half-remember.

Either you condition them, or they condition you

Customer price conditioning sounds like:

  • "My neighbor got a quote for half that."
  • "I was told it should be around $5 a square."
  • "That's too much."
  • "I can't afford that."

Some of us let this happen even in solar -- where the customer is already paying more to the utility than what we're presenting. That's how strong price conditioning is. As the professional, it's on you to control the conversation.

What price conditioning actually is

The act of setting expectations before you set a price. By the time the number lands, the customer already has context -- the market, the problem, the alternative, the payment. The number isn't a shock. It's a confirmation.

"The customer has absolutely no context. So we need to give them context." Ken Ebbert
Part 2 · The Order Matters

The Sequence

Six moves, in this order. Lead with the big number and the customer panics, then negotiates you down from it. Lead with the sequence and the total is just confirmation.

1

Feeling

What does a new roof mean for them? Peace of mind. Sleep at night. Insurance secure. Family protected. Not water damage at 3am.

2

Problem

What happens if they don't? Insurance cancellation. Roof failure. Water damage that costs $20K-$100K+ to remediate. The cost of doing nothing is rarely zero.

3

Market

What does a quality roof cost in their area? Anchor on the going rate before you say what we charge. Most homeowners massively underestimate this number.

4

Trinity Price

What we charge -- in the context of the market and the problem. Premium product, real warranty, real financing. Often below market in our region.

5

Payment

The monthly number. A range -- something like $230 to $330 a month depending on home and scope. This is the number they live with.

6

Total

Now reveal the total. It's just math at this point -- payment times months equals total. They've already accepted the payment. The total is a formality.

The mistake we keep making: leading with the big number. The big number scares people. Then they spend the rest of the conversation negotiating you down from it. Reverse the order and the conversation flows naturally.

Part 4 · The Hidden Lever

The Cost of Waiting

Customers want to delay. They think delay is free. It's not. Price the cost of waiting, not just the cost of doing.

+10-14% per year

Roofing prices are climbing 10-14% annually. On a $30K roof, one year of delay is $3K-$4K extra. Two years and you're paying for a small car.

+$3-$4K emergency repair

When the old roof finally goes, the patch to stop the bleeding before the full replacement runs $3K-$4K on average. Add insurance deductibles and the hotel for the spouse who is not letting them sleep on the couch.

Cost of delay: $6K to $8K

That's the math. Waiting one year costs $6K-$8K in price increase plus emergency repair. The $279 a month you would have been paying this year is cheaper than the cost of waiting.

Use the math out loud: "You'd pay roughly $3,500 more next year just because the price went up. Plus $3,500 if your roof fails before then. The monthly payment we're talking about is less than that. Delay isn't free -- it's the most expensive option on the table."

The prospecting unlock: 40% of US homes statistically need a new roof. Only 7-14% replace one each year. The pool isn't price-sensitive shoppers -- it's homeowners who don't know they need it, or are kicking the can. Bring them the math, bring them the urgency. There are unlimited customers.
Part 5 · Anchor in Payments

Range Over Point Price

A point price invites yes or no. A range invites a conversation. And it invites the customer to anchor on the lower end -- which they will.

People think in payments, not totals

Nobody says "I bought a $45,000 car". They say "my payment is $650 a month." A roof is the same. A $35,000 number is abstract and scary. $275 a month is something they can compare to things they already pay.

"Mr. Customer, our customers typically spend somewhere between $230 and $330 a month, depending on the size of the home and what we need to do. Which end of that range are you hoping to land at?"

Ask them which end they want -- they'll always say the lower one. Now you have a budget number. You're working within a payment they've already agreed to.

Lead with the Total

  • "This is going to be $35,000."
  • Customer panics. Number doesn't exist in their daily life.
  • You spend the rest of the call defending it.
  • They negotiate down or stall.

Lead with the Payment

  • "Around $275 a month."
  • Customer compares to other monthly bills. Concrete.
  • If they say yes to the payment, the total is a formality.
  • The total is now just how we got there.
Don't think in dollar jumps. The difference between $10/sq and $10.50/sq on a 30-square roof is roughly $20 a month. Another dollar a square is $30-$35. These are not big jumps for a customer thinking in payments. Be willing to grade in fifty-cent increments.
Part 6 · Let Them Make Your Case

Discovery as Price Conditioning

The most powerful conditioning you do isn't a statement -- it's a question. When the customer tells you the problem, they own the problem. When they own the problem, they need the solution.

Ask, listen, let them make the case

Before you talk about features or price, ask what's been going on with the roof. Then shut up. They tell you about the letters from the insurance company, the leak last spring, the neighbor whose roof collapsed. By the time they're done, they've made the case for replacement better than you ever could. Now all you do is present the solution.

The discovery cascade

Build a cascade of questions where each answer creates the next question. Don't tell them anything you can ask them.

"When was the last time you had this looked at?"
"How many years ago did you get a quote? What was the price back then?"
"What do you think the price would be now compared to back then?"
"Have you talked to anyone who's done a roof recently? What did it run them?"
"Why did they need to do it? Do you think they should have done it sooner?"

By the end, the customer has told you: prices are higher than they thought, roofs fail, and waiting makes it worse. You didn't tell them any of that. They convinced themselves.

Bill's question -- the affordability frame

"Mr. Customer, if you had to spend $3 or $4 grand today, how would you pay for it?"

If they say "I couldn't" -- great. That's a price condition you can use:

"Right -- so you can't afford a $3K-$4K emergency repair. Could you spend $300? Because that's the monthly number on the new roof. And that's instead of the repair, not in addition to it."

The unaffordable thing isn't the new roof. It's the alternative.

Part 8 · The Mindset Shift

The Reframe & Self-Talk

Some reps struggle with the price because they're going in thinking the customer's problem is the price. It isn't. The customer's problem is way bigger than the price -- and that's the conversation you're actually having.

"You are not selling a $36,000 roof for $12 a square. You are selling a $279 a month payment that avoids a $40,000 to $45,000 problem for the customer." Ken Ebbert

Apologize once -- the mentor line

"Mr. Customer, I'd rather apologize to you today once for the price than for the next 25 years for the quality and service. Isn't that what you'd prefer?"

A premium price is a one-time apology. A bad install is a 25-year apology. Frame it that way and most customers stop arguing for the cheap option.

The discount math -- it cuts three ways

Every dollar discounted comes out of three places:

  • The company's earnings -- Trinity's ability to fund 24/7 service, training, and warranty fulfillment
  • Your earnings -- your commission shrinks with the price
  • Trinity's ability to keep its promises -- the company that can't make the math work in year 5 won't be there in year 25 to honor a warranty

When you discount reflexively, you're price conditioning the entire market that we're cheaper than we are. That hurts every rep behind you on the next street.

Self-talk: confidence comes from belief

The reps with 50%, 60%, 70% close rates aren't tricking customers. They believe what they're selling solves a bigger problem than what it costs. That belief takes the shackles off.

If you walk in thinking "I hope they can afford this," you'll act like it. If you walk in thinking "I'm bringing this customer a $279 payment that prevents a $45K disaster," the conversation feels different -- because it is different.

It's not impossible. It's unaffordable. Those are different problems. We have the most affordable program in the market because we have real financing -- our average competitor has no financing, or 12 to 24 months same-as-cash, or 5-year terms at $400-$600 a month. Our payment is the affordability story.
Part 9 · What You're Up Against

The Competitive Reality

Most roofers cannot do what Trinity does on financing. Knowing this turns every "let me get other quotes" into a price-conditioning opportunity.

No Financing

A check or cash up front. The customer has to come up with $30K-$40K.

12 or 24 Months SAC

Same as cash, kicking the can. After the promo period, the customer still owes the full balance.

5-Year @ $400-$600/mo

If they offer financing, the term is short and the payment is double ours.

The financing line

"What's the monthly payment on the other quote? Most roofers don't offer financing at all -- and the ones that do are usually 5-year terms running $400 to $600 a month. We can spread it over a longer term so your payment is around $275. Same roof, half the monthly hit."

You don't have to win on price. You win on monthly. The financing is the moat.

Part 10 · Worked Example

The Roleplay -- Cost of Waiting in Action

What it sounds like when you put it all together. The rep prices the cost of waiting before naming a price for the work, then closes by making the monthly payment cheaper than the alternative.

Rep

If you were to make the decision to get a new roof not today, but over the next year, the reality is you'd pay around $43,000 to $44,000 -- because roofing prices have been going up 10 to 14% a year. My concern is, you mentioned you're a little budget conscious, you've got kids like me. Is that fair?

Customer

Yeah, definitely budget conscious.

Rep

Okay. Can you afford to pay 10 to 14% more for the same thing just because you delayed the decision a year?

Customer

No, I don't want to.

Rep

For your roof, that's $3,000 to $4,000 extra you'd pay just for delaying. Does that seem worth it?

Customer

Absolutely not.

Rep

Here's the other piece. Your roof has separation, unevenness, and granular loss -- you saw the photos. What usually causes someone to finally replace is a leak. When that happens, the emergency repair to stop the bleeding before the full job runs $3,000 to $4,000 on its own. So between the price increase and the emergency patch, you could spend $6,000 to $8,000 just trying to avoid this decision. That doesn't make sense for you, right?

Customer

No, I cannot afford that.

Rep

The monthly payment we have for you is $279. That's actually cheaper this year than what waiting would cost you. Doesn't that make more sense?

Customer

Yes -- when you put it like that, that's the cheaper way.

What just happened

The rep talked about a lot of numbers -- $43K, $4K, $8K -- before ever naming the price of the work. Each one the customer reacted to and agreed with. By the time the rep said "$279 a month," the number wasn't a price tag. It was the cheap option compared to everything else they'd just discussed.

That's price conditioning. The price was the answer to a problem the customer had already accepted.

"Don't ever just say a number. Talk prices, talk ranges, talk values -- so the customer is conditioned to understand what we're even talking about." Ken Ebbert
