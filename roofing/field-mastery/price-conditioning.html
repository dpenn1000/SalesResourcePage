Price Conditioning

In every price conversation, somebody is conditioning somebody. Either you set the context before the number lands, or the customer sets it for you. The professional leads it: build the value, float a market range, and deliver your real number as relief, not shock.

Somebody Is Always Price Conditioning

A roof is a 20, 30, or 40 thousand dollar decision a homeowner makes once or twice in a lifetime. They have no reference point. If you do not give them one, they bring their own, and now they are conditioning you.

When the customer leads it

  • "My neighbor got a quote for half that."
  • "I was told it should be $5 a square."
  • "That's too much." / "I can't afford that."
  • The shackle: you spend the rest of the appointment defending a number you never framed.

When you lead it

  • Set expectations before you set the price. Context first, number second.
  • Build the value, float a range, read the reaction, then deliver the real quote.
  • The number stops being a shock. It becomes a confirmation of what they already accepted.
  • Every buying decision is made on feeling. Logic only validates the decision the gut already made.
Rep · framing the conversation"Most folks replace a roof once, maybe twice, in their whole life, so there's no reason you'd carry around a price for it. That's my job. Let me give you the real picture of what a quality roof runs, and then where we land."
Ask the room: name the last appointment where the customer conditioned you. What was the first price number said out loud, and who said it?
What is price conditioning, in one sentence?

AnswerSetting expectations before you set the price, so the customer has context (the market, the problem, the payment) by the time the number lands. If you do not lead it, the customer conditions you with a half-remembered quote or a neighbor's number.

The First Number Always Sounds Expensive

Almost nothing else in a customer's life costs what a roof costs, so whatever number they hear first feels expensive. Let them feel that moment of pain on a market range, never on your actual quote.

Lead with your quote

You become the expensive one. The number has no context to compare against, so it lands as a shock and they negotiate you down from it for the rest of the appointment.

Let the market take the hit

Float the going rate first. The customer reacts to that number, feels the pain there, and braces. Your real quote arrives after the bracing, into context.

Discovery does the conditioning

Make them say the numbers. When was it last looked at? What did it cost back then? Did anyone you know do a roof recently, and why? The cost of doing nothing comes out in their own words.

In the home"Any number I say first is going to sound like a lot, because you don't buy a roof every year. So before I tell you ours, let me show you what this costs across the market. I'd rather you have that reaction to their number than to mine."
Ask the highest bill, not the average. The same rule runs on the solar side: never ask for the average bill, ask for the highest. Once a customer says their July bill hits $400 to $800, every number under that feels good. You are conditioning with their own figures.
Ask the room: build a 4-question discovery cascade that gets the customer to say "roofs cost more than I thought" without you ever stating a price.
Why should your quote never be the first number said?

AnswerWhatever number a customer hears first sounds expensive, because nothing else they buy costs that much. If your quote is first, they react to it and negotiate down from it. Let the market range absorb that reaction so your real number arrives into context.

Float a Range So the Price Lands as Relief

Build the range with discovery and a quality frame, set the market high, let the customer settle toward the bottom, then deliver your real number under where they braced. Because they expected $330 a month, $279 lands as a gift instead of a shock.

How the range plays out

  • Frame quality: "You don't want a roof that fails in 5 to 10 years."
  • Set the market: a quality roof runs $30,000 to $40,000, roughly $230 to $330 a month.
  • Ask which end they're hoping to land at. They always pick the lower number, and now you have a budget they named.
  • Deliver the real number, $279 a month, under where they braced.

Why payments beat totals

  • People think in payments. Nobody says "I bought a $45,000 car," they say "my payment is $650."
  • $279 a month compares to bills they already pay. A $36,000 total is abstract and scary.
  • Grade in 50-cent steps. A dollar a square on a 30-square roof is only about $30 a month, not a cliff.
  • Present the top-line first, let them react, then reveal the after-incentive number as the good news. Lead with the lowest number and you have nowhere to go.
Rep · floating the range"For a quality roof in this area, most homeowners are somewhere between $230 and $330 a month, depending on the home and what we find. Which end of that are you hoping to land at? ... Good. The number I've got for you is $279. So it's not $330, it's $279."
The mechanism, said plainly: if the customer braced for $330 and you say $279, you get the goodwill of relief. If you say $279 first, that is the only number they respond to, and there is no relief in it.
Role-play: float a $230 to $330 range, let the rep playing the customer pick the low end, then deliver $279 so it reads as relief. Then do it wrong (lead with $279) and feel the difference.
Why deliver the real price under where the customer braced?

AnswerIf they expected the top of the range ($330 a month) and you land at $279, the gap reads as relief and goodwill: "it's not $330, it's only $279." Say $279 first and there is nothing to compare it to, so it just sounds like a price. The range manufactures the relief.

You're Not Selling a Roof, You're Selling a Payment

The reframe that cures a rep's own price fear. You are not selling a $36,000 roof at $12 a square. You are selling a $279 a month payment that avoids a $40,000 to $45,000 problem. If you think the problem you are solving is the price, that is exactly why you struggle with price.

Price the cost of waiting, not just the cost of doing

Delay is not free. A $40,000 roof becomes $43,000 to $44,000 next year, $3,000 to $4,000 more, just for waiting. Add a likely $3,000 to $4,000 emergency repair when it leaks. Between the two, a customer can spend $6,000 to $8,000 trying to avoid the decision. The $279 a month is cheaper this year than either one.

Rep · pricing the delay"Can you afford to pay 10 to 14% more for the same roof, just because you decided a year from now? That's $3,000 to $4,000 extra. Plus the $3,000 to $4,000 emergency patch when it finally leaks. The $279 a month is cheaper this year than what waiting costs you."
Apologize once, not for 25 years. "I'd rather apologize to you today for the price you don't want to pay than apologize for the next 25 years for the quality and service you'd have to live with." A premium price is a one-time apology. A cheap install is a 25-year one.
Ask the room: a rep is scared to say the number. Walk them through the reframe out loud until the fear is gone. What is the actual problem they're solving?
What are you actually selling, and why does that fix price fear?

AnswerYou are selling a $279 a month payment that avoids a $40,000 to $45,000 problem and protects the customer's biggest investment, their insurance, and their family. The roof price is small next to the problem it solves. If you believe the price is the problem, you will act apologetic and give it away. Believe in the payment and the conviction transfers.

Good News Framing and the Ugly Roof

A roof is inevitable, so frame it as a gift, not a burden. And the customer with the crappy 10-year-old roof is your best prospect, not your worst, because they are the one you can prove the value to.

The good-news, great-news frame

Reframe the inevitable as help. They are going to need a roof no matter what. The great news is that because you are the one here, they have a way to help pay for it through the solar savings, instead of out of pocket later.

In the home"The good news is you're going to need a roof no matter what. The great news is, because I'm the one here talking to you about it, we have a way to help you pay for it, instead of you paying for it completely out of pocket."

Chase the ugly 10-year roof

Drop the age limits. The roof that looks crappy at 10 years is the number-one customer to pursue, because it is the one you can prove value to the most. Reps are selling new roofs on 10-year-old roofs so the site survey never blows up a deal at the end.

Rep · on the old roof"How much did you spend on this roof 10 years ago? That money's down the drain now, and here's why. Let's make sure the next one is the last one you have to think about."
On the solar side: lead with what solar can actually do for them. Only about 1 in 3 homes qualifies, so set the bar low. A customer who covers even a portion of their usage and pays for the roof through that savings still comes out ahead. Set the bar low, then over-deliver.
Ask the room: who walked past an ugly 10-year roof this week thinking it was too new to replace? What would the good-news frame have sounded like?
Why is the ugly 10-year-old roof your best prospect?

AnswerIt is the roof you can prove value to the most: "how much did you spend 10 years ago? That money's down the drain." Selling it proactively also means the site survey never surprises you at the end. Drop the age limits; if it looks bad, you can replace it, and you frame it as good news because they need a roof regardless.

The Price Is the Value: Live in the Bracket

If you do not defend your price with conviction, you undercut your own value, because the price is the value. Sell at $6 to $7 a square and you are in the dumpster, fighting chuck-in-a-truck on price alone. Live at $10 to $14 a square and the conversation becomes quality and service.

The bracket ($10-14/sq)Quality, service, reliability, warranty. You are the value guy in the high-end space.Nobody is even talking about Chuck-in-a-truck

Mercedes vs Lexus

The price signals the worth. If a Mercedes were $12,000 and a Lexus $25,000, people would assume the Mercedes was worth less. Price with conviction at $10 to $12 a square and the cheap guy is not part of the customer's decision at all.

Price in full plywood up front

Be the hero with a credit. Always price assuming a full plywood replacement. You would rather sell it and hand back a credit when you do not need it than call later asking for another $5,000. Hidden water damage in the plywood can cost $12,000 on top of the shingles, so name that number now.

Rep · pricing the plywood"I price every roof assuming a full set of plywood. If we open it up and don't need it, you get a credit and I'm the good guy. What I won't do is call you in two weeks asking for another $5,000, because that water you can't see is going somewhere, and that somewhere is your plywood."
Role-play: a rep keeps drifting to $7 a square out of fear. Coach them up into the $10 to $14 bracket, then defend it with conviction when the "customer" pushes.
Why does living at $10 to $14 a square change the whole conversation?

AnswerAt $6 to $7 a square you are competing on price alone against chuck-in-a-truck quoters, the dumpster. At $10 to $14 the conversation becomes quality, service, and reliability, and the cheap guy is not even in it. The price is the value: defend it with conviction or the customer assumes you are worth less, like a $12,000 Mercedes next to a $25,000 Lexus.

The Field Manual

Key Takeaways

  • Somebody is always price conditioning somebody. Lead it or the customer leads you.
  • The first number always sounds expensive, so never let it be your quote. Let the market take the hit.
  • Float a range, set the market high, deliver under where they braced. $279 reads as relief after $330.
  • Ask the highest bill, not the average, and talk payments, not totals.
  • You're not selling a $36K roof, you're selling a $279 payment that avoids a $40-45K problem.
  • Good news: they need a roof anyway, and the solar savings help pay for it. Chase the ugly 10-year roof.
  • Live in the bracket at $10-14 a square, not the dumpster. The price is the value.
  • Price in full plywood up front and be the hero with a credit, not the villain with a surprise bill.
