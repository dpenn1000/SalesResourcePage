After the Number

Price conditioning is everything you do before the number. This is what you do after, when they push back. The worst first move is to cut the price. Defend the value first, then make them earn any movement you give.

Cutting Your Price Confirms They Overpaid

The instinct is to drop the number and rescue the deal. It does the opposite. The moment you lower it, you answer their first question and hand them three worse ones. Defend the value before you move a dollar.

What dropping the price says

  • "Could I pay less?" You just answered yes, so the negotiation is not over, it just started.
  • "Could I pay even less?" If it moved once, they will push to move it again.
  • "Was it worth what you first said?" Now they doubt the original value, and you.
  • "Why not that price first?" The cut tells them you were testing what they would pay.

What defending the value says

  • The price is the value. Hold it with conviction and the number reads as worth, not as a starting bid.
  • Price up gives you room. Selling higher leaves space to move later for a real reason, not from panic.
  • Sell cheap and you signal cheap. A low volume price tells them you are not worth much.
  • Stand first, then earn. Any movement comes after they trade something, never before.
Rep · handling the price"When I lower my price, I have just confirmed you may have overpaid. Your first question was could you pay less. The next one is could you pay even less, and was it even worth what I first told you. So let me stand here and show you the value before we ever talk about the number."
Ask the room: a customer says "that's too much" and your hand goes to the price. Name what you do INSTEAD in the next ten seconds.
Why is cutting the price the worst first move?

AnswerIt answers their first question ("could I pay less?") with a yes, which only triggers worse ones: could I pay even less, was it worth the first number, and why not that price first. The cut makes them doubt the value and you, so the correct first move is to defend the value, not drop the number.

The 5 Moves Before You Ever Cut

When they push, you have a sequence to run, not a discount to give. Work these 5 moves in order. Most pushback resolves before you reach the last one, and you keep your value intact the whole way down.

  • 1. Question the question

    Quantify "too much." Make them put a number on it. "Too much compared to what, and by how much?" You cannot answer a vague objection, and often there is no real number behind it.

  • 2. Change the way to pay

    Test with a shorter, higher-payment loan first. Show the 5-year monthly, let them react, then keep the 20-year option in your pocket to release the same price at a lower payment.

  • 3. Divide the difference

    Shrink the gap to a daily number. A few thousand over 300 months is a dollar a day. "Is a dollar a day worth the difference between us and a chuck-in-a-truck quote?"

  • 4. Trade the value

    Make any discount earned. Sign today, give referrals, or bundle roof, solar, and battery together. They get a better number only when they give you something back.

  • 5. Resign for a reason

    If you must move, give a gift. Never "I'll lower the price." A privately owned company extends a gift for a genuine hardship, with a reason attached.

Rep · handling the price"Before I touch the number, I question the question, I change the way you pay, I divide the difference down to a dollar a day, and I look at what we can trade. Only then, and only for a reason, would I ever resign on price. I am not going to just drop it on you."
Role-play: a customer says "knock off three grand and I'll sign." Walk all 5 moves out loud, in order, before you give a cent.
Name the 5 moves in order.

Answer1) Question the question (quantify "too much"); 2) Change the way to pay (test a shorter higher-payment loan, hold the long loan in your pocket); 3) Divide the difference (a dollar a day vs a chuck-in-a-truck); 4) Trade the value (sign today, give referrals, or bundle products to earn a discount); 5) Resign for a reason (a gift, never "I'll lower the price").

Give a Gift, Don't Cut a Price

If the negotiation earns a real reduction, the words you use decide whether you keep your value or torch it. "We'll lower the price" tells them the first number was inflated. "We're giving you a gift" tells them they got something special.

"We'll lower the price"

  • Confirms the first number was soft. They assume they could have pushed harder.
  • Invites the next ask. A price that moves once moves again.
  • Cheapens the whole offer. If the value was real, why is it dropping?

"We're giving you a gift"

  • Tie it to a real hardship. The gift exists because of their situation, not because the price was wrong.
  • Own the reason. A privately owned company can be flexible and sometimes extend these gifts.
  • The price stays the value. The number was right; the gift is a one-time kindness on top.
Rep · handling the price"I never said we would lower the price. I said we are giving you a gift because of your financial situation. We are privately owned, so we can be flexible, and once in a while we get to do something like this. That is not the price changing, that is us taking care of you."
Why selling high matters here: a gift only lands if there was room above to give it. Price with conviction first, and the gift becomes a real gesture instead of an admission you started too high.
Ask the room: say the same $1,500 reduction two ways, as a price cut and as a gift. Which one would YOU feel better walking away with?
Why frame a reduction as a "gift" instead of a price cut?

Answer"Lowering the price" confirms the first number was inflated and invites the next ask, which cheapens the whole offer. A "gift" extended by a privately owned company for a genuine hardship keeps the price as the value: the number was right, and the gift is a one-time kindness on top. It only works if you sold high enough to have room to give it.

Your Reaction to a Low Quote Is the Message

When a customer drops a low competitor number and your face loses color, you have just told them to worry. Your reaction carries more weight than anything you say after it. Use it to put doubt on the low price, not on yourself.

The flinch

You go quiet, your face drops, you get defensive. Now the customer believes the low price is real and yours is the problem. You handed them the upper hand for free.

The flip

"Wow, that's a really low price. I don't understand how they could even offer that." Said with genuine surprise, it raises the question of whether that number is even valid.

The reason it works

Not everyone going out of business puts up a sign. A number that low usually means a corner getting cut, a company in trouble, or one rep who will not be there in a year.

Rep · handling the price"Wow, that is a really low price. Honestly, I don't understand how they could even offer that and stay in business. I am not saying don't use them. I am saying not everybody having a going-out-of-business sale puts a sign in the window. At that number, something is getting left out."
Ask the room: a customer reads you a price $4,000 under yours. Show me your face and your first six words.
What should your reaction to a low competitor price do?

AnswerIt should cast doubt on the low number, not on you. A flinch tells the customer their low quote is real and yours is the problem. Instead, react with genuine surprise ("wow, that's a really low price, I don't understand how they could even offer that"), because a number that low usually means a cut corner or a company in trouble, and not everyone going out of business puts up a sign.

The Low Quote Is One Weak Rep, Not "The Market"

One or two low numbers do not set a market, and they should not condition you. Every home is custom, so the "market" is whatever house you are standing in. The cheap quote is usually one struggling rep, not a company's real price.

Why "they're all the same" is false

  • You wouldn't get 3 quotes to paint a family portrait. A custom job is not a commodity, and a roof is custom to the house.
  • The low number is often one weak rep. A rep on a performance plan, chasing any signature, is not the company's market price.
  • Chasing it feeds the spiral. Where we win most of the volume, hitting low numbers keeps pushing the whole market down.

What to say instead

  • Name the gap first. "We're not the cheapest and not the most expensive. We give the best value for the work."
  • Quality, price, service: pick. Nobody offers the best quality and service at the lowest price. The math does not work.
  • Apologize once, not for 25 years. "I'd rather apologize today for the price than for 25 years of bad work."
Rep · handling the price"You would not get 3 quotes to paint your family portrait and call them all the same, because it is custom to you. Your roof is the same. That low number might be one rep in your town who needs a deal this week. It is not the market, and it is not what that work is worth."
Ask the room: a customer says "three other guys all came in lower." How do you reframe "the market" without bashing them?
How do you handle "three other quotes all came in lower"?

AnswerReframe what "the market" means: every roof is custom to the home, so you would not get three identical quotes to paint a family portrait. The low number is usually one weak rep who needs a signature, not a company's real price. Name the value gap first ("not the cheapest, not the most expensive, best value for the work"), and note that nobody delivers the best quality and service at the lowest price.

Perception Over Intention

Your intention can be great, but if the customer does not perceive a deal, you lose it. A small visible drop they can see beats a large value they have to take on faith. And the deal closes when they are saying yes to themselves, not to you.

Make the deal visible

Bundle the products and drop it a few dollars. A small visible reduction makes them feel they got a deal, even when the real value is in everything around it. They have to see it to feel it.

Perception beats intention

Great intentions you never show do not close. If the customer cannot perceive that you gave them something, your generosity may as well not exist. Show the deal, do not just mean it.

Yes to themselves

Set up the priorities early. Identify what is needed now vs later up front, so by negotiation time they are saying yes to their own decision, not caving to you.

Rep · handling the price"Let me bundle these together and take a little off so you can see the deal in front of you. My intention to take care of you does not mean anything unless you can feel it. And by now you are not saying yes to me, you are saying yes to the decision you already made was right."

After-the-number checklist

  • Defended the value before touching the number.
  • Questioned the question and quantified "too much."
  • Changed the way to pay before changing the price.
  • Divided the difference down to a dollar a day.
  • Made any discount earned through a trade.
  • Framed any reduction as a gift, with a reason.
  • Reacted to the low quote with doubt, not a flinch.
  • Made the deal visible so they could perceive it.
Stay honest. Cast doubt on a too-low number with questions, not accusations. Do not call a competitor a liar or promise they will go out of business. Let the customer reach the conclusion that a price that low has a catch.
Role-play: close the same deal twice, once where you only "intend" the value and once where you make a $10 bundle drop visible. Which one signs?
Why does perception beat intention at the close?

AnswerA customer only acts on what they can perceive. A great intention you never show does not close, but a small visible bundle drop makes them feel they got a deal. Pair it with setting priorities early, so by negotiation time they are saying yes to their own decision, not to you.

Key Takeaways

  • Cutting the price confirms they overpaid. Defend the value before you move a dollar.
  • Run the 5 moves: question the question, change the way to pay, divide the difference, trade the value, resign for a reason.
  • If you must move, give a gift with a reason, never "I'll lower the price."
  • Your reaction to a low quote is the message. Flip it, don't flinch.
  • The cheap number is one weak rep, not the market. You wouldn't get 3 quotes to paint a portrait.
  • Perception beats intention. Make the deal visible, and they say yes to themselves.
