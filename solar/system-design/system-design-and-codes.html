Part 1 · The Frame

The layout isn't a sales decision. It's the code.

Customers assume an empty roof section means we're holding back. The truth: fire code reserves that space so firefighters can climb, walk, and cut ventilation on a burning house. When you can name the rule, "why isn't it covered?" stops being an objection and becomes a trust-builder.

Why a rep needs this

  • Answer the roof question. "That gap is a fire-code pathway, required so first responders can reach the ridge." Calm, specific, done.
  • Set honest expectations. Lead with what the roof can hold. Codes explain the ceiling, so you never over-promise coverage.
  • Read your own design. Spot a layout problem (a panel over a vent, a missing pathway) before the customer or the AHJ does.
  • Sound like the expert. Naming R324.6 and the 33% rule is the difference between a salesperson and a consultant.

What this training covers

  • Design principles: why portrait beats landscape and what mixing is allowed.
  • The fire codes: pathways, ridge setbacks, access roofs, and egress under CT's 2021 IRC R324.6.
  • The RSA workflow: how a design moves from order to approved model in Genesis.
  • A live tester: three difficulty tiers to prove you know it cold.

Recall Why is part of the roof left open?

Fire code. Pathways and ridge setbacks reserve roof space so firefighters can access and ventilate the structure. It's a safety requirement, not a sales limit.

Part 2 · Design Principles

Portrait always wins, and the rules for mixing

When the same panel count fits either way, go portrait. It installs faster, uses less material, and looks cleaner. Landscape isn't banned, but it follows one strict rule.

Portrait · Preferred

Always preferred over landscape. If the same panel count fits, choose portrait: easier to install, less racking material, more aesthetic.

Acceptable

A portrait array with one landscape row, and only on top. Never put landscape on the bottom.

Unacceptable

Avoid "tetris": portrait and landscape side by side, or portrait stacked on top of landscape. One panel count usually fits cleaner in straight portrait.

Watch the auto-layout. Look for panels placed over obstructions (vents, conduit) or tucked under dormers. Those panels can't physically be installed; they have to be removed from the design by hand. Catch it before it ships.
Show a real layout on screen. Ask: is the mixing legal? Are any panels sitting over an obstruction or under a dormer?
Recall A portrait array has one landscape row. Where is the only place it can go?

On top. A single landscape row above a portrait array is acceptable; landscape on the bottom, side by side, or "tetris" mixing is not.

Part 3 · The Why Behind the Rules

R324.6 exists so firefighters can work the roof

CT follows the 2021 IRC, Section R324.6. Every pathway and setback traces back to one purpose: a firefighter has to climb a burning roof, walk it safely, and cut ventilation. Panels are set back from ridges and edges to keep those operations possible.

Access

Firefighters need a clear route from the eave up to the ridge to reach and work the roof.

Ventilation

Cutting a hole at the ridge lets heat and smoke escape. A clear ridge makes that cut possible.

Egress

A bedroom window over a roof is an escape route. Code keeps a clear path from it to the roof edge.

The governing text. "Photovoltaic systems shall be designed and installed in accordance with R324.3.1 through R324.7.2.5, the manufacturer's instructions, and NFPA 70." The pathways, setbacks, and access points below are how that gets met on a CT roof.
Recall What single purpose drives every pathway and setback rule?

Firefighter safety: access up the roof, ventilation at the ridge, and clear egress from bedroom windows on a burning structure.

Part 4 · Pathways

Two 3-foot pathways, one facing the street

Pathways are the clear lanes from the lowest roof edge up to the ridge. They're the most common reason a roof isn't fully covered. The rules are specific and worth knowing cold.

The core rules

  • At least two pathways, on separate roof planes, each not less than 3 feet (36 inches) wide.
  • One on the street or driveway side of the building. It does not have to face the street, just be on that side.
  • Every roof with solar needs a pathway on it or on an adjacent roof plane.
  • Each building gets a minimum of two; complex roofs may need more.

Where the pathway can sit

  • On the same roof plane as the array, or
  • On an adjacent roof plane, or
  • Straddling the same and adjacent planes.
  • Arrays may share a pathway as long as each array has one on its own or an adjacent roof.
  • Over solid structure capable of supporting a firefighter, clear of vents and conduit.
  • Along an edge, valley, or hip, kept to the side of the array. Carving a pathway through the middle of an array is a last resort, not the plan.
The single-slope exception. If the array is on only one slope of a single-ridge roof and the opposite slope has clear access with a roof access point, the eave-to-ridge pathway is not required on the array's slope. The same logic applies to one side of a hip or valley.
Draw a simple gable roof on the board. Ask a rep to place two compliant pathways and explain why each one qualifies.
Recall How wide, how many, and where must pathways sit?

At least two, each 3 feet (36 inches) wide, on separate roof planes, with at least one on the street or driveway side. Every roof with solar needs a pathway on it or an adjacent plane.

Part 5 · The 33% Rule

Ridge setback: 18 inches or 36 inches

The clear space below the ridge depends on one number: how much of the total roof area the arrays cover. Cross 33% and the setback doubles. The math is on total roof surface, not a single plane.

Arrays use 33% or less of all roofs
18″
1.5-foot setback on both sides of the ridge
Arrays use more than 33% of all roofs
36″
3-foot setback on both sides of the ridge
Genesis calculates it, you confirm it. The Genesis and Web Gen platforms have a built-in calculator that adjusts the required ridge setback based on roof space used. After any layout change, always Refresh Setbacks to confirm it recalculated. Edit the array up past 33% and forget to refresh, and the model is out of code.
Sprinklered homes get more room. If the dwelling has an automatic fire-sprinkler system (NFPA 13D), the threshold moves up: 18″ setback up to 66% coverage, 36″ above 66%. Most existing CT homes aren't sprinklered, so the 33% rule is the everyday one. (IRC R324.6.2.1)

Recall Arrays cover 40% of total roof area. What's the ridge setback?

36 inches (3 feet) on both sides of the ridge. Over 33% total coverage doubles the setback from 18 inches to 36 inches.

Part 6 · Access Roofs & Exceptions

When the normal rules bend: access roofs, height, egress, detached

Four special cases change what's required. Knowing them keeps you from mis-reading a design or over-explaining a limit that doesn't apply.

Access roofs

When modules are on only one slope and the opposing roof is clear, that opposing roof can serve as the access roof. It must be street or driveway facing with at least one ground access point free of doors and windows. If an access roof is used, pathways aren't required.

Height differences

If two roofs differ in height by more than 1 foot, they aren't "adjacent." Treat them as two separate buildings, and each one needs its own minimum of two pathways.

Emergency egress

Panels can't sit on the roof directly below a bedroom escape-and-rescue window, and a 36-inch (3-foot) pathway stays clear. It's the occupant's way out and the firefighter's way in.

Detached structures

Detached, non-habitable structures (a standalone garage, carport, or solar trellis) are exempt from roof access entirely. But if the structure has living space someone could sleep in, the rules apply. Low-slope roofs, 2:12 pitch (about 10 degrees) or less, are also exempt from access, pathways, and setbacks.

Quiz the room: a detached garage with a finished loft bedroom. Setbacks, yes or no? Why?
Recall When can pathways be skipped entirely?

When a qualifying access roof is used: modules on one slope only, the opposing roof clear, street or driveway facing, with a ground access point free of doors and windows.

Part 7 · The RSA / Genesis Workflow

How a design gets built: three action items, in order

Every RSA (Remote Site Assessment) is created in three tracked steps. They run in sequence, and each one's completion triggers the next. The action items in the platform tell you exactly where your opportunity stands.

Step 1

Preliminary Measure

Tracks the Eagleview / Aurora order

Not Started: order not placed or location not confirmed.
Requested: order placed.
Completed: order received, triggers the next step.

Step 2

Preliminary Layout

Tracks the Breakfast Bot 3D model

Not Started: in queue waiting on the 3D model.
On Hold: an issue is blocking the model.
Completed: 3D model created, triggers the next step.

Step 3

Remote Site Assessment

Tracks the Genesis / Web Gen design

Not Started: in queue, prioritized by appointment date and time.
On Hold: an issue is blocking the RSA.
Completed: the RSA model is built.

When something stalls, call RSA. If an action item completed but the next one never triggered, or a step sits in Not Started / Requested / On Hold as the appointment approaches, reach out to the RSA team. On a new or add-on opportunity, if you don't even see a Preliminary Measure action item, a tech issue likely blocked the trigger, so contact RSA.
Ask: your appointment is tomorrow and the RSA action item still reads "Not Started." What do you do, and who do you call?
Recall What are the three RSA action items, in order?

Preliminary Measure (Eagleview/Aurora order), then Preliminary Layout (Breakfast Bot 3D model), then Remote Site Assessment (Genesis/Web Gen design). Each completion triggers the next.

Part 8 · In the Field

The on-site checklist, the roof key, and tree photos

When you're at the house, the RSA model needs verifying against reality. A few habits keep designs accurate and tree removals credited.

  • Verify the right house was modeled.
  • Verify pitches; flag 5-degree-plus discrepancies, include photos.
  • Check obstructions were all accounted for, include photos.
  • Check shading against the model.
  • Note height issues and tree removals, or trees missing from the RSA, include photos.

Tree-removal photos that count

  • Never stump-only. A bare stump doesn't show where on the property the tree stood.
  • Include a reference point: the front of the house from the street, a shed, deck, or pool.
  • Before and after shots of the same area.
  • Mark removals on the overhead Genesis shot so the location is unmistakable.
