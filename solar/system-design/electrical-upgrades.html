Part 1 · Why This Matters

Be the advocate, not the salesperson who disappeared

The customer didn't buy a panel upgrade. They bought solar. When an electrician shows up and the power goes out for half a day, that surprise lands on you unless you got there first. Knowing the electrical work lets you set the expectation and stay the trusted voice.

What knowing this earns you

  • No surprise outages. "Plan for the power to be off most of the day" said at signing beats a panicked call mid-install.
  • Credibility on the roof. When you can name why the panel needs changing, the customer trusts the recommendation.
  • Fewer cancellations. Most post-sale fallout is a surprise the customer wasn't prepared for. Preparation is the fix.
  • A reason to call. The electrical walk-through is a natural touch that keeps you in the deal after the close.

What goes wrong without it

  • "Nobody told me." The customer loses a fridge of food or a work-from-home day they didn't plan for.
  • The electrician explains it cold. A stranger delivering bad news on install day, instead of you framing it weeks earlier.
  • The deal wobbles. Surprise plus inconvenience plus a number they didn't expect equals a cancellation risk.
Ask the room: when did a surprise on the electrical side cost someone a deal or a referral? What would have prevented it?
Recall Why does a rep need to understand electrical upgrades?

To set the expectation before install day. The electrical work, especially the power outage, is the most common post-sale surprise. Naming it up front keeps the rep the trusted advocate and protects the deal.

Part 2 · The Main Electrical Panel

The MEP: 100-amp vs 200-amp service

The Main Electrical Panel (MEP) is the box where the home's circuits live and where solar usually connects. Its size, 100-amp or 200-amp, decides how much room is left and which interconnection method the install needs.

100-amp service

  • 20 to 24 spaces: typically 16 full breakers plus 4 twins.
  • 30/42 max panel size: the ceiling on how many circuits the box can hold.
  • Less headroom. A 100-amp panel fills up faster, so solar more often needs a line tap or tap box.

200-amp service

  • 40 to 42 single-pole breakers, or up to 20 double-pole breakers.
  • 300 to 400 amps of breakers can live across all panels combined.
  • More headroom. A 200-amp service usually has room for a standard breaker connection.
Why the amperage matters to you. A full or undersized panel is the single most common trigger for added electrical work. When you see a small old panel, expect the conversation about an upgrade, and get ahead of it.
Recall Which service has more room for a standard solar connection?

200-amp. It holds 40 to 42 single-pole breakers and usually has busbar room for a standard breaker feed. A 100-amp panel (30/42 max) fills up faster and more often needs a line tap or tap box.

Part 3 · Interconnection

Three ways solar ties into the home

Every project connects one of three ways. The panel's available room and what else is wired in (a generator, a transfer switch) decide which. Each has a different impact on the customer's day.

Breaker / Busbar Feed No outage

Standard connection. Solar lands on a breaker in the panel. Trinity standard is the bottom right-most location for consistency.

Used when the panel has room on the busbar. The simplest, cleanest tie-in.

Line Tap No outage

When the panel is full, when a secondary power source like a generator transfer switch is present, or when the system is larger than the busbar will allow.

Efficient: no power outage required to complete the work.

Tap Box ⏱~2 hr

Box installed below or next to the meter to make the interconnection. Required when there are clearance issues with the panel, or when a whole-house transfer switch (manual or automatic) is present.

The connection is made in the tap box instead of the panel or transfer switch. Needs a couple-hour outage to complete the work safely. Same for 100-amp or 200-amp.

Read the cue. A generator, a transfer switch, or a packed panel all point away from the simple breaker feed and toward a line tap or tap box. Spotting it early means no surprises for the customer.
Quiz the room: which connection method needs a power outage? Which one is used when a whole-house transfer switch is present?
Recall When is a tap box required, and what does it cost the customer's day?

When there are clearance issues with the panel, or a whole-house transfer switch (manual or automatic) is present. The connection moves to a box below or beside the meter, and it needs a roughly two-hour power outage.

Part 4 · Safety Hardware

Rapid shutdown and overcurrent protection

Two pieces of hardware show up on essentially every project. They're code, not options. Knowing what they are keeps you from getting caught flat-footed when a customer points at a new box on the wall.

Manual disconnect (rapid shutdown)

  • Required on all projects. A visible switch that kills the array for first responders and service.
  • Within 10 feet of the meter and the inverter.
  • It's a safety device, not an add-on. Every customer gets one.

Overcurrent Protection Device (OCPD)

  • Protects the circuit from too much current.
  • Could be breakers or fused, depending on the design.
  • Standard on the install, handled by the design and electrical teams.
Recall Where does the rapid-shutdown disconnect have to be installed?

Within 10 feet of the meter and the inverter, on all projects. It lets first responders and technicians shut the array down quickly.

Part 5 · The Upgrade Menu

When the panel or service itself needs work

Sometimes the existing equipment can't carry solar, or it's simply too old. The scope on the contract will name one of these. The biggest difference to the customer is how long the power is out, so read the row by its outage.

UpgradeWhat gets replacedOutage
Panel Change The panel only, same amperage. Circuits are re-fed in the new panel. Standard is a 30-circuit panel; 24-circuit when space is limited. Done when the panel is in very poor condition. 4-5 hr+
Service Change The meter and riser, same amperage. Existing panel is re-fed. Done when the meter or riser is in very poor condition. up to 4 hr
Full-Service Change Meter, riser, and panel, same amperage. Done when all three are in very poor condition. 7-8 hr
Full-Service Upgrade amperage up Meter, riser, and panel, stepped up to the next amperage (60→100 or 100→200). Done when equipment is poor condition and overloaded. 7-8 hr
Service Change w/ Relocation Meter and riser, same amperage, panel re-fed. Plus the meter location moves; the utility chooses the new spot. up to 4 hr
Full-Service Change w/ Relocation Meter, riser, and panel, same amperage. Plus the meter relocates to a utility-chosen spot. 7-8 hr
"Change" vs "Upgrade." A change swaps old equipment for the same amperage. An upgrade steps the amperage up (60 to 100, or 100 to 200). Same install, different finish line: upgrade means the home gets more capacity.
Have a rep explain, in plain words, the difference between a panel change, a full-service change, and a full-service upgrade. Then ask: which two keep the power off the longest?
Recall A contract reads "Full-Service Upgrade, 100 to 200 amp." What should you tell the customer to expect?

A full-day-feeling job: the meter, riser, and panel all get replaced and stepped up to 200-amp service, with the power out for roughly 7 to 8 hours. The upside is a home with real capacity headroom.

Part 6 · Reading the Scope

Rigid mast, service drop, and the words on the contract

The contract scope uses a few terms that confuse customers. If you can translate them, you stay the expert in the room. The one that comes up most is the rigid mast.

Rigid Mast

When the scope says Rigid Mast, a rigid pipe mast is required by the utility to gain height for the overhead wires coming from the street. It raises the connection point so the wires clear the property to code.

Service Drop

The service drop is the overhead set of wires running from the utility's pole to the house. The mast gives those wires the height the utility requires.

Relocation

When a job includes relocation, the meter moves to a new spot. The utility, not the customer, chooses the location. Worth flagging early so it's never a surprise on the wall.

Same description, either amperage. The scope wording for these jobs reads the same whether the home is on 100-amp or 200-amp service. Don't let the matching language fool you; confirm the amperage separately.
Recall The scope says "Rigid Mast." What is it and why is it there?

A rigid pipe mast required by the utility to raise the overhead service-drop wires to the required height from the street. It's about clearance, not aesthetics.

Part 7 · The Conversation

How to set the expectation at the table

Knowing the work is half the job. Saying it cleanly, before install day, is the other half. Frame the outage as a planned, normal step, and the customer plans around it instead of panicking through it.

  1. 1. Name it early

    Bring up the electrical work at signing, not on install day. "Part of going solar is making sure your electrical can carry it. Here's what that looks like for your home."

  2. 2. Give the outage a number

    Tell them the range up front. "Plan for the power to be off about [X] hours that day." A number they can plan around beats a vague warning.

  3. 3. Explain the why

    "Your panel is older and full, so we're giving it room to handle solar safely." The reason turns a cost into an upgrade.

  4. 4. Help them plan

    Medications that need refrigeration, work-from-home, a home medical device: surface these and plan around them so install day is smooth.

The expectation-setting script

Say"One thing I want you ahead of: to add solar safely, your electrical panel needs to be brought up to spec. On install day the power will be off for about half a day while the team handles it. It's routine, we do it constantly. I just want you planning for it, not surprised by it."

Said three weeks early, this is a non-event. Discovered at 9am on install day, it's a cancellation risk. Same facts, completely different outcome.

Role-play: a customer with a 100-amp panel needs a full-service upgrade. Walk the four steps and set the expectation in under a minute.
Recall What's the one number you never want the customer to learn for the first time on install day?

The length of the power outage. Give them the hour range at signing so they can plan around it. A surprise outage is the most common electrical cancellation trigger.

The Field Manual

One-sentence reminders

  • The electrical work protects the deal after you leave. Get ahead of it.
  • 200-amp has room; 100-amp (30/42 max) fills up and needs a tap.
  • Breaker feed and line tap: no outage. Tap box: about two hours.
  • Rapid-shutdown disconnect within 10 feet of meter and inverter, every job.
  • Change = same amperage. Upgrade = next amperage up.
  • Full-service work means 7 to 8 hours without power. Say it early.
  • Rigid mast raises the service-drop wires to utility height.
