Part 1 · Why This Matters

Be the advocate, not the salesperson who disappeared

The customer didn't buy a panel upgrade. They bought solar. When an electrician shows up and the power goes out for half a day, that surprise lands on you unless you got there first. Knowing the electrical work lets you set the expectation and stay the trusted voice.

What knowing this earns you

  • No surprise outages. "Plan for the power to be off most of the day" said at signing beats a panicked call mid-install.
  • Credibility on the roof. When you can name why the panel needs changing, the customer trusts the recommendation.
  • Fewer cancellations. Most post-sale fallout is a surprise the customer wasn't prepared for. Preparation is the fix.
  • A reason to call. The electrical walk-through is a natural touch that keeps you in the deal after the close.

What goes wrong without it

  • "Nobody told me." The customer loses a fridge of food or a work-from-home day they didn't plan for.
  • The electrician explains it cold. A stranger delivering bad news on install day, instead of you framing it weeks earlier.
  • The deal wobbles. Surprise plus inconvenience plus a number they didn't expect equals a cancellation risk.
Ask the room: when did a surprise on the electrical side cost someone a deal or a referral? What would have prevented it?
Recall Why does a rep need to understand electrical work?

To set the expectation before install day. The electrical work, especially the power outage and any clearance fixes, is the most common post-sale surprise. Naming it up front keeps the rep the trusted advocate and protects the deal.

Part 2 · The Main Electrical Panel

The MEP, the sub panel, and how much room is left

The Main Electrical Panel (MEP) is the box where the home's circuits live and where solar usually connects. Its size, 100-amp or 200-amp, decides how much room is left and which interconnection method the install needs.

100-amp service

  • 20 to 24 spaces: typically 16 full breakers plus 4 twins.
  • 30/60 max panel size: up to 30 spaces, 60 breakers, the ceiling on what the box can hold.
  • Less headroom. A 100-amp panel fills up faster, so solar more often needs a line tap or tap box.

200-amp service

  • 40 to 42 single-pole breakers, or up to 20 double-pole breakers.
  • 300 to 400 amps of breakers can live across all panels combined.
  • More headroom. A 200-amp service usually has room for a standard breaker connection.
Why the amperage matters to you. A full or undersized panel is the single most common trigger for added electrical work. When you see a small old panel, expect the conversation about an upgrade, and get ahead of it.
Recall Which service has more room for a standard solar connection?

200-amp. It holds 40 to 42 single-pole breakers and usually has busbar room for a standard breaker feed. A 100-amp panel (30/60 max) fills up faster and more often needs a line tap or tap box.

Part 3 · Safety Hardware

Utility disconnect, production meter, and overcurrent protection

A few pieces of hardware show up on essentially every project. They're code, not options. Knowing what they are keeps you from getting caught flat-footed when a customer points at a new box on the wall.

Utility disconnect & production meter

  • Required on all projects. A visible switch that kills the array for first responders and service, plus a meter that measures what the system produces.
  • Within 10 feet of the meter and the inverter.
  • It's a safety device, not an add-on. Every customer gets one.

Overcurrent Protection Device (OCPD)

  • Protects the circuit from too much current.
  • Could be breakers or fused, depending on the design.
  • Sits within 10 feet of the point of connection, ahead of the service disconnect.
Recall Where does the disconnect have to be installed?

Within 10 feet of the meter and the inverter, on all projects. It lets first responders and technicians shut the array down quickly.

Part 4 · Interconnection

Three ways solar ties into the home

Every project connects one of three ways. The panel's available room and what else is wired in (a generator, a transfer switch) decide which. Each has a different impact on the customer's day.

Breaker / Busbar Feed No outage

Standard connection. Solar lands on a breaker in the panel. Trinity standard is the bottom right-most location for consistency.

Used when the panel has room on the busbar. The simplest, cleanest tie-in.

Line Tap No outage

When the panel is full, when a secondary power source like a generator transfer switch is present, or when the system is larger than the busbar will allow.

Efficient: no power outage required to complete the work.

Tap Box ⏱~2 hr

Box installed below or next to the meter. Required when there are clearance issues with the panel, or a whole-house transfer switch (manual or automatic) is present.

The connection is made in the tap box instead of the panel. Needs a roughly two-hour outage to complete safely.

Read the cue. A generator, a transfer switch, or a packed panel all point away from the simple breaker feed and toward a line tap or tap box. Spotting it early means no surprises for the customer.
Quiz the room: which connection method needs a power outage? Which one is used when a whole-house transfer switch is present?
Recall When is a tap box required, and what does it cost the customer's day?

When there are clearance issues with the panel, or a whole-house transfer switch (manual or automatic) is present. The connection moves to a box below or beside the meter, and it needs a roughly two-hour power outage.

Part 5 · Reading the Riser

SEU cable vs. rigid metal pipe mast

The riser is the run that carries the service wires from the utility's drop down to the meter. There are two kinds, and the scope tells you which. The default is SEU cable; a rigid mast is called out only when the utility needs more height.

When the scope says "Rigid Mast." A rigid metal pipe mast is required by the utility to gain height for the overhead wires coming from the street (the "service drop"). It extends higher than the roofline. If the scope doesn't call it out, the riser is standard SEU cable.
Recall The scope says "Rigid Mast." What is it and why is it there?

A rigid metal pipe mast that extends above the roofline, required by the utility to raise the overhead service-drop wires to the needed height from the street. The default riser, when no mast is specified, is SEU cable.

Part 6 · The Upgrade Menu

When the panel or service itself needs work

Sometimes the existing equipment can't carry solar, or it's simply too old. The scope on the contract will name one of these. The biggest difference to the customer is how long the power is out, so read the row by its outage.

UpgradeWhat gets replacedOutage
Panel Change The panel only, same amperage. Circuits are re-fed in the new panel. Standard is a 30-space panel; 24-space when room is limited. Done when the panel is in very poor condition. 4-5 hr+
Service Change The meter and riser, same amperage. Existing panel is re-fed. Done when the meter or riser is in very poor condition. up to 4 hr
Full-Service Change Meter, riser, and panel, same amperage, plus surge protection added. Done when all three are in very poor condition. 7-8 hr
Full-Service Upgrade amperage up Meter, riser, and panel stepped up to the next amperage (60→100 or 100→200), plus surge protection. Done when equipment is poor and overloaded. 7-8 hr
Service Change w/ Relocation Meter and riser, same amperage, panel re-fed. The meter location also moves (utility's choice), often because the old spot doesn't meet current code. up to 4 hr
Full-Service Change w/ Relocation Meter, riser, and panel, same amperage, plus surge protection, with the meter relocated to a utility-chosen, code-compliant spot. 7-8 hr
"Change" vs "Upgrade." A change swaps old equipment for the same amperage. An upgrade steps the amperage up (60 to 100, or 100 to 200). Same install, different finish line: upgrade means the home gets more capacity. Full-service work also adds surge protection.
Have a rep explain, in plain words, the difference between a panel change, a full-service change, and a full-service upgrade. Then ask: which two keep the power off the longest?
Recall A contract reads "Full-Service Upgrade, 100 to 200 amp." What should you tell the customer to expect?

A full-day-feeling job: the meter, riser, and panel all get replaced and stepped up to 200-amp service (with surge protection), power out for roughly 7 to 8 hours. The upside is a home with real capacity headroom.

Part 7 · Electrical Reviews

Clearance requirements: what has to stay clear

Before an install, the meter and equipment area gets reviewed for clearance. The utility and code require open space around the meter and a safe distance from gas. A blocked meter can stall a project, so spot it on the walk and flag it.

Keep it clear. Shrubs, debris, fences, and other structures can't crowd the meter. On the walk-through, picture the clear box around the meter and the gas distances. If something's in the way, flag it now, not on install day.
Pull up a real site photo. Ask the room: does this meter meet clearance? What would have to move?
Recall What clearances does the meter area need?

A 4 ft wide by 3 ft deep by 6 ft high clear access space (and a 3 ft by 3 ft working space) in front of the meter, kept free of shrubs and structures. Plus 3 ft to a gas meter, 5 ft from any gas regulator, and 10 ft from a propane fill valve.

Part 8 · The Field Review

What to capture, and the words on the scope

A good electrical review catches the things that drive scope before they become surprises. One photo in particular matters, and a couple of contract terms are worth being able to translate.

Translate the scope

  • Service drop: the overhead wires from the utility's pole to the house.
  • Rigid mast: a metal pipe that raises those wires to utility height.
  • Relocation: the meter moves to a code-compliant spot the utility chooses.
  • Same description, either amperage: the wording reads the same on 100-amp or 200-amp, so confirm the amperage separately.
Why the rusted-socket photo matters. Rust on the meter socket is a condition the utility and our crews need to see in advance. A clear bottom-up photo lets the scope get written right the first time, so install day isn't the day everyone discovers it.
Recall What's the one photo you never skip on a rusted meter?

A photo of the bottom of the meter socket. Rust there drives the scope toward a service or full-service change, and capturing it up front prevents an install-day surprise.

Part 9 · The Conversation

How to set the expectation at the table

Knowing the work is half the job. Saying it cleanly, before install day, is the other half. Frame the outage as a planned, normal step, and the customer plans around it instead of panicking through it.

  1. 1. Name it early

    Bring up the electrical work at signing, not on install day. "Part of going solar is making sure your electrical can carry it. Here's what that looks like for your home."

  2. 2. Give the outage a number

    Tell them the range up front. "Plan for the power to be off about [X] hours that day." A number they can plan around beats a vague warning.

  3. 3. Explain the why

    "Your panel is older and full, so we're giving it room to handle solar safely." The reason turns a cost into an upgrade.

  4. 4. Walk the meter area

    Check the clearances while you're there. If a shrub, fence, or propane tank crowds the meter, flag it now so it's handled before install day.

The expectation-setting script

Say"One thing I want you ahead of: to add solar safely, your electrical may need to be brought up to spec. On install day the power could be off for a few hours while the team handles it. It's routine, we do it constantly. I just want you planning for it, not surprised by it."

Said three weeks early, this is a non-event. Discovered at 9am on install day, it's a cancellation risk. Same facts, completely different outcome.

Role-play: a customer with a 100-amp panel needs a full-service upgrade and a meter that's crowded by a fence. Walk the four steps in under a minute.
Recall What's the one number you never want the customer to learn for the first time on install day?

The length of the power outage. Give them the hour range at signing so they can plan around it. A surprise outage is the most common electrical cancellation trigger.
