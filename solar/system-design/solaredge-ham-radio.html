Technical Brief · SolarEdge HD Wave & Amateur Radio

Solar & the Ham Radio Objection

A small slice of homeowners run amateur ("ham") radio, shortwave, or AM DX gear, and they've heard SolarEdge systems can raise the radio noise floor. They're not wrong, and it's not a dealbreaker. It's a known, well-documented, and largely fixable issue. Know the facts, qualify the concern, and lead with the solution.

Part 1 · The Concern

Who actually raises this, and why it matters

Most homeowners will never think about radio interference. But a licensed amateur-radio operator, a shortwave listener, or a serious AM-radio hobbyist may have heard, often from a forum or a club, that SolarEdge "wipes out the bands." Dismissing it loses their trust instantly. Acknowledging it and showing you understand the fix wins it.

Part 2 · The Physics, Plainly

What's actually happening on the roof

SolarEdge isn't a plain string inverter. It puts a small power optimizer (a high-frequency switching circuit) behind every panel, with the HD Wave inverter on the wall. Those optimizers chop DC power thousands of times a second. Switching circuits radiate radio noise, and the DC wiring tying all the optimizers together on the roof behaves like a big loop antenna that broadcasts it.

The source is the system, not one box

  • Optimizers do most of it. Dozens of small switchers on the roof, each emitting, tied together by wiring that radiates.
  • The inverter adds some too. The HD Wave is also a switching device, but the rooftop array is usually the bigger antenna.
  • Switching harmonics climb the spectrum. The fundamental switching is in the kHz range; its harmonics march up through 2–30 MHz (HF) and into low VHF.

Why "FCC certified" still caused issues

SolarEdge's inverter and optimizers were FCC-certified as individual components on a bench. The criticism from the radio community is that the whole rooftop system, all those parts plus the wiring loop, behaves differently in the real world than any single certified part. That gap is where early interference complaints came from.

Recall Is the inverter or the optimizers the bigger noise source?

Usually the optimizers plus the DC wiring that links them, which acts as a radiating loop antenna. The HD Wave inverter contributes, but the rooftop array is typically the larger emitter.

Part 3 · What Gets Hit

Which bands, and how bad

The interference is "broadband noise", a raised hiss across a wide range rather than one tone. It buries weak signals by lifting the noise floor. The worst impact is on the HF bands where most ham operating happens, plus the AM broadcast band.

The tell-tale fingerprint. Because the optimizers only emit while producing, the interference appears in daylight and fades at night. That sun-following pattern is exactly how a ham confirms a PV system is the source, and it's also the basis for two of the most common homeowner questions (next section).

AM broadcast and the lower HF bands (160m/80m/40m) take the worst hit, tapering toward higher bands and low VHF. The give-away is that the noise tracks the sun, present in daylight while producing, gone at night.

Part 4 · The Regulatory Reality

Why the manufacturer has to take this seriously

This isn't just goodwill. Solar electronics are FCC Part 15 devices, and amateur radio is a licensed service. That combination gives a ham real leverage, and gives the manufacturer a real obligation, which is exactly why SolarEdge built a resolution process.

The Part 15 bargain

  • A Part 15 device must accept any interference it receives.
  • It must not cause harmful interference to licensed radio services.
  • Every unit ships with that exact language printed in the manual.

What that means in practice

  • A licensed ham who documents harmful interference can require the source to be resolved.
  • The ARRL (the national amateur-radio association) and the FCC have pushed manufacturers on this.
  • SolarEdge responded by cooperating, shipping fixes, and updating hardware, rather than fighting.
Don't overstate or understate it. Don't promise "zero interference, guaranteed", and don't wave the concern away as imaginary. The honest, defensible line is: it's a recognized Part 15 issue, the manufacturer has a track record of resolving it, and here's the playbook. See Selling with Integrity.
Recall Why does a licensed ham have leverage a regular neighbor doesn't?

Solar electronics are FCC Part 15 devices, which must not cause harmful interference to licensed services. Amateur radio is licensed, so a ham who documents harmful interference can require the source to be addressed, which is why the manufacturer maintains a resolution process.

Part 5 · The Two Questions Hams Ask

"Can't I just shut it off?" and "What if I only operate at night?"

These are the two smartest questions a radio operator asks, and the answers are genuinely reassuring. Both work because the optimizers only make noise while the array is producing power. SolarEdge's SafeDC feature drops each optimizer to about 1 volt and stops the switching whenever the inverter is off or there's no sun.

"Can I shut the system down when I want to get on the air?"
Yes, it works

Turning the inverter off (its ON/OFF/P switch or the AC/DC disconnect) triggers SafeDC: the optimizers drop to ~1V each and stop switching, so the array's RF noise essentially stops. The operator gets a clean band on demand, perfect for a contest weekend or an evening net.

The trade-off: while it's off the system produces nothing, so they lose that solar savings for those hours, and monitoring/production pauses. Fine as an occasional move; not ideal as an everyday workaround. It can often be automated on a schedule.
"I only operate at night, am I fine?"
Usually, yes

No sun means no production, so the optimizers sit idle at ~1V and the inverter is essentially asleep. A standard grid-tied, PV-only array is effectively silent at night, which is a great fit for a night-operating ham.

The one caveat: if the home has battery storage (SolarEdge Home Hub / StorEdge), the battery can discharge at night, which keeps an inverter switching, so some RFI may persist after dark. Pure PV with no battery is the quiet-at-night case.

How to say it at the table

Say"Here's the good news for a radio operator: the panels only make noise when they're making power. Flip the inverter off and the system goes quiet within seconds, so you can shut it down for a contest or an evening net any time you want. And if you mostly operate after dark, a standard system with no battery is basically silent at night on its own, the sun's down, so there's nothing to make noise."
Role-play: a licensed ham says "so my only options are lose my savings or only operate at night?" Reframe it, both are easy levers, and mitigation (next section) usually means they don't have to choose at all.
Recall Why does shutting the inverter off, or operating at night, clear the interference?

The optimizers only emit while producing power. SafeDC drops them to ~1V when the inverter is off or there's no sun, so the switching, and the noise, stops. Caveat: a home battery can keep an inverter active at night, so battery systems may still emit after dark.

Part 6 · The Mitigation Playbook

How interference actually gets resolved

When an install does cause interference, there's a well-worn fix path, much of it pioneered with the ARRL and the FCC. Most cases resolve without removing the system. You don't need to memorize the engineering; you need to know a real, ordered playbook exists.

1
Confirm the source
Often a remote session: the installer shuts the system off remotely and the ham confirms the noise vanishes, proving the array is the cause before any parts ship.
2
Updated / spread-spectrum optimizers
Newer optimizers smear the switching noise across frequencies instead of concentrating it, lowering the peak interference. SolarEdge has drop-shipped replacements to resolve cases.
3
Ferrite suppression cores
Snap-on ferrite chokes / common-mode cores on the DC homerun cables, typically a few per cable set near the inverter, choke off the radiated noise. The right ferrite "mix" is matched to the affected bands.
4
Wiring & grounding technique
Twisting the DC homerun conductors together (instead of running them parallel) shrinks the loop antenna, plus enhanced optimizer/array grounding. Often the single most effective change.
5
Shielding & layout, as needed
In stubborn cases, shielded cable, inverter shielding/relocation, or distance from the ham's antenna close the remaining gap.
The reassuring track record. Under FCC and ARRL pressure, SolarEdge has cooperated, shipping replacement optimizers and ferrite kits and developing lower-noise hardware. The message to a worried operator: you are not the first, there is a process, and it works.

For a serious HF operator, set it up right the first time

  • Flag it early. Tell the design/install team the homeowner is a licensed ham so grounding, twisted homeruns, and ferrites are planned in, not retrofitted.
  • Set honest expectations. "We may need to add suppression and dial it in. That's normal and we've done it before."
  • Keep alternatives open. If the prospect is an extremely sensitive HF operator, it's fair to discuss design choices and manage expectations rather than promise perfection.
Discuss: what should a rep write on the work order when the homeowner mentions ham radio, so the install team plans mitigation in from day one?
Recall Name three mitigations that resolve solar RFI.

Any of: updated/spread-spectrum optimizers, ferrite common-mode chokes on the DC cables, twisting the DC homerun conductors, enhanced grounding, and shielding/relocation/distance. Step one is always confirming the array is the source.

Part 7 · The Field Playbook

Handling the objection at the table

You don't need to be an RF engineer. You need to acknowledge honestly, qualify the exposure, and route to the fix. Confidence plus honesty is the whole move here.

  1. 1. Acknowledge, don't dodge

    "You're right, some SolarEdge installs have caused radio interference. It's real and it's documented." Denial loses a technical prospect instantly.

  2. 2. Qualify the exposure

    "Are you a licensed operator running HF from the house, or is this something you read about?" Their answer sets the depth of the conversation.

  3. 3. Explain it simply

    "The panel optimizers are tiny switching circuits; the rooftop wiring can radiate that as noise on the HF and AM bands, only while they're making power."

  4. 4. Hand them the easy levers

    "You can shut it off for a contest any time, and a no-battery system is basically silent at night on its own."

  5. 5. Route to the fix

    "If it ever does interfere, there's a real playbook, newer optimizers, ferrites, grounding, that resolves the large majority of cases without removing anything."

  6. 6. Set it up right

    For a serious operator: "I'll flag on your file that you're a ham so the install team builds the suppression in from the start."

Promise the process, not perfection. Never guarantee zero interference. Promise what's true: an honest acknowledgement, easy on/off and night-time levers, and a proven mitigation path backed by the manufacturer. See Selling with Integrity.
