The program

Where the audit fits in Connecticut's solar program

Connecticut runs its residential solar through the state's Renewable Energy Solutions tariff, administered by Eversource and United Illuminating. The program pays the customer for the energy their system produces. To qualify a home for it and clear it for interconnection, the state requires a completed Home Energy Solutions (HES) energy assessment on file for the property.

That assessment is the energy audit, and CMC Energy is the partner who performs it. A technician walks the home, takes care of immediate efficiency upgrades on the spot, and documents the visit so the property is checked off the state's list.

Not an extra cost to the customer.

The audit is a covered benefit, not a line item on the proposal. For income-eligible households there is a dedicated no-cost program (the HES-IE application is linked below). Frame it the way it deserves: a free home upgrade that comes bundled with going solar.

Why it gates the install

No audit on file, no interconnection approval

Interconnection is the utility's sign-off that lets the system turn on and start producing. The HES assessment is one of the boxes the state and utility check before that approval comes through. If it is missing, the file sits, and the customer's brand-new system waits.

So we schedule it at the point of sale, not after.

When the audit is requested the same day the customer signs, it runs in parallel with permitting and engineering rather than becoming a surprise blocker at the finish line. Getting it done early is the single cleanest way to keep a job from stalling on the way to turn-on.

Common misread

Schedule it even if they think they don't need one

Two situations come up constantly, and the answer is the same for both: still get it scheduled.

"We already had an energy audit."

Maybe so, but it may be too old to count, or it may not be the assessment the program recognizes. CMC has to verify what is actually on file and confirm it meets the requirement before the property can be checked off. Put in the request and let them confirm.

"Our house is new, it doesn't need one."

A newer or efficient home still has to be verified and recorded. The program does not skip the step because a house looks new. CMC checks the home, documents it, and clears it off the list. Without that confirmation on file, the interconnection paperwork has a hole in it.

There's no version of this where skipping the call is the right move. Request it for every customer and let CMC sort out what the home actually needs.

The bigger picture

Why this matters beyond one install

Connecticut pairs solar with efficiency on purpose. A panel array offsets what a home uses, but a tighter, more efficient home uses less to begin with. The audit is how the state makes sure every solar home is also an efficient one, so the clean energy we add actually moves the needle instead of just covering waste.

For the customer, that is a lower bill and a more comfortable house. For the grid, it is real demand coming off the system. And for us, every audit we schedule cleanly is one more job that reaches turn-on without friction.
