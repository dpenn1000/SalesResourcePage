Part 2 · The Diagnosis

Root cause 1: a menu instead of a prescription

Leave three financing options on the table and the customer stops evaluating solar vs. utility and starts evaluating option A vs. B vs. C. Three decisions to make, and the easy fourth choice is "let me think about it."

What we say that stalls

  • "Here are your options: PPA, loan, or cash..."
  • "Quoted solar, battery, and a 22-square roof. Needs time to think."
  • "SAC roof vs. loan. PPA solar. Two Powerwalls. Need time to decide."
  • "Cash, finance, HELOC rough numbers..."

What closes

  • "Based on everything you told me, the PPA is the right fit for your family."
  • "PPA LR signed. HD one touch. Both on title."
  • "LR PPA signed. No issues. Discovery: fixed-income retirement, two-grandkid summer bills."
  • "Light reach PPA signed. Concept confirmed at the table."

Part 2 · The Diagnosis

Root cause 2: thin discovery means no prescription authority

You can't prescribe with conviction what you didn't earn the right to know. Notes that read "interested," "no," or "info only at the front door" aren't appointments. They're transactions of facts. A prescription requires permission and depth.

Discovery must uncover all five

  • The PAIN: what do they hate about the bill?
  • The FEAR: what are they protecting against?
  • The VISION: what does the solution feel like?
  • The TIMELINE: how long do they plan to stay? What's their urgency?
  • The DECISION: who makes it, and are they here?
If you can't answer all five, you haven't earned the right to prescribe. Confidence at the close is a downstream effect of depth in discovery. You prescribe because you know, not because you guessed.
Pick a recent "think about it" note as a team. Which of the five pillars is missing from it?

Depth in discovery. If you can answer all five pillars (pain, fear, vision, timeline, decision), you prescribe because you know.

Part 2 · The Diagnosis

Root cause 3: proceeding without all decision-makers

"Wife wasn't home." "Only one of them was home, they said they'd tell the other." Each is a 90-minute consultation that cannot end in a signature, run by a rep hoping the present spouse would sell the absent one.

Why it happens

  • We didn't confirm both will be present before the sit
  • We treat the appointment as the close instead of a chance to find a way
  • We accept "I'll tell them" as a close plan
  • The schedule says go, so we go

The rule

  • Confirm both decision-makers before the sit
  • If one is missing, run the concept only, not the full appointment
  • Reschedule. Make them need the follow-up to get the solution
  • Exception: a documented sole decision-maker

Concept vs. solution

Walking the concept of solar with the present spouse is fine, and often correct. Presenting a specific solution (system size, pricing, PPW, financing terms) without both deciders present is not. Coach the present homeowner on exactly what to say, and lock the go-back at a time both will be there.

Part 3 · The Framework

The D.E.A.L. framework

Four steps that earn the right to close on the first appointment. Run them in this order. Don't skip ahead.

D

Discover: pain and emotion

What do they hate? What keeps them up at night? What does cost control mean for their family? Your notes must show emotional content, discovery information, and a follow-up plan, not a parts list.

"Worried about retirement on a fixed income. The next rate hike scares them. Daughter is moving back in, bill is going up either way."
E

Eliminate options proactively

You make the product decision before the conversation. Moving in three years? PPA. Long horizon and an explicit cash request? Walk through ownership discovery. One option only, never a side-by-side menu at the table.

"Twenty-year horizon, cash on hand, asked about ownership unprompted, comfortable with the no-ITC tradeoff. Okay, we walk loan/cash. Otherwise, PPA."
A

Assert your authority

You have months and years of training in this. They have thirty minutes. Speak with the conviction that earned position deserves.

"Based on everything you've shared, the PPA is the right fit for your family. I'd be doing you a disservice recommending anything else."
L

Lock the decision today

Make inaction visible. "I'll think about it" assumes waiting costs nothing. Show them what waiting costs.

"Every month you wait at Eversource's rate is roughly $X more. We can lock yours today. What's holding us back from moving forward?"
Each rep recites D.E.A.L. from memory, then names the step they most often skip.
Recall What are the four steps of D.E.A.L., in order?

Discover (pain and emotion), Eliminate options (decide the product first), Assert your authority, Lock the decision today.

Part 4 · Earn the Right

Five pillars of discovery

Every appointment, every time. If your notes don't speak to all five, you walked out with information, not a relationship.

Pain
What do they hate about the bill, specifically?
Fear
What are they protecting against: fixed income, tuition, retirement?
Vision
What does the solution feel like to live with?
Timeline
How long do they plan to stay? What's their urgency?
Decision
Who makes it, and are they here right now?

Anchor questions that build the pain together

Ask them. Pause. Then ask the following question. The second answer is where the truth lives.

"How long have you been frustrated with your Eversource bill?"
"What would you do with that money if you could hold on to more of your after-tax dollars?"
"How does it feel knowing rates have gone up every year, and just went up again January 1st?"
"If nothing changes, where's your bill in five years? Ten?"
The following-questions rule. A rookie asks a question, gets an answer, and moves on. A pro asks the next question after the answer, and the next, until they actually understand. Listening is not waiting for your turn to talk.

Part 5 · The Product Rule

PPA is the default prescription

In a no-ITC environment for owned residential systems, the math has shifted. The PPA is the right answer for almost every CT homeowner, and the way we talk about it has to catch up.

Why the PPA wins now

  • No upfront cost, no financial barrier to entry
  • Fixed rate in a rising-rate environment that just jumped 13% on supply
  • The only product where ITC value still flows through, at the provider level, to keep pricing competitive in a no-ITC world for owned systems
  • Transfers with the home, adding marketability at no cost
  • The simplest decision for the homeowner to make today

Cash or loan, only when

  • The customer explicitly asks for it
  • Discovery confirms a long horizon, real liquidity, and an honest understanding that cost goes up without ITC on owned systems
  • You've built the emotional case for ownership after confirming the logistics actually work
  • It genuinely serves them, not just their stated preference
Compliance line: Do not cite the federal residential ITC as a benefit on cash or loan systems to homeowners. It is gone for owned systems. Talk PPA economics.
2026 tax-credit correction. For owned, loan, or cash solar, the homeowner federal tax credit ended after December 31, 2025. In 2026 only a third-party lease or PPA still captures that benefit, and it lives at the provider level, built into the rate. Do not promise a "30% federal tax credit" on a loan or cash deal this year.
Sell with integrity at the signature. The prescription has to be true. Never tell a customer solar erases their bill or covers everything, and never let urgency outrun what the home can actually do. Everything you say and write, all the way to the signature, can be used against you later. See Selling with Integrity.

The rule

You decide which product to present before you walk through the door. PPA is the default. Cash and loan are the exception, earned through discovery, not displayed as Door #2 on the table.

Part 6 · The Language

The exclusive prescription

Build the pain first. Then make them feel chosen, not sold. Switch out a few words and the same recommendation lands as a privilege instead of a pitch.

Anchor the cost of staying

Build the pain together: questions that earn the prescription.
"How long have you been frustrated with your Eversource bill?"
"What would you do with the money if you could hold on to more of your after-tax dollars?"
"How do you feel knowing rates went up every year, and just jumped again on January 1st?"
"If nothing changes, where's your bill in five years?"

Prescribe with authority

Language that makes them feel chosen, not sold.
"I've looked at everything you've shared, your usage, your goals, your timeline, and I'm going to recommend the best solution for you and your family."
"Not every homeowner qualifies for this program. It looks like you're a solid candidate."
"Most people I sit with, I wouldn't put on this program. For your situation, it's the right call."
"I'd be doing you a disservice giving you a bunch of options to sort through tonight."
One delivery rule: deliver the prescription matter-of-fact or celebratory, both work. What kills you is hesitation in between. Hesitation reads as doubt, and doubt reads as "you should think about it." Read the buyer: a Driver wants it matter-of-fact, an Expressive celebratory, a Connector reassuring, an Analytical backed by the numbers.

Part 7 · The Decoder

"I need to think about it": what it actually means

"Think about it" is almost never about time. It's unresolved doubt. The customer isn't asking for hours, they're telling you something is still in the way. Your job is to find what.

What it usually signals

  • You didn't uncover the real objection
  • They don't feel you understand their situation
  • They're comparing you to another company
  • You gave them too many options to decide between

What not to say

  • "No problem, take your time!"
  • "I'll call you next week."
  • "Here's my card if you have questions."
  • "Let me know when you're ready."

What to say instead

  • "I hear you. Help me understand, what's the main thing on your mind?"
  • "Is it the program itself, or something I haven't addressed?"
  • "What would need to be true for you to feel confident moving forward today?"
  • "What's the cost of waiting another six months at your current rate?"
Feel / Felt / Found"I hear you, and I get how you feel. A lot of folks I sit with felt that same pause before deciding. What they found was that the hesitation was almost always one specific question, so let's find yours together."

Diagnose the category

Once they tell you what's actually in the way, treat it correctly. Misdiagnosing the category is the most common closing mistake.

  • Trust gap: "I've heard bad things," "I need another quote." Address with specifics, transparency, and proof, not a louder pitch.
  • Information gap: "I want to do more research." Surface the specific unresolved question before you leave. Don't just reschedule.
  • Stakeholder gap: "Spouse not home." Don't run the solution. Run the concept and lock the go-back.
  • Financial framing: "That's a lot of money." Don't negotiate. Reframe to concept and site-specific math: "Once I run your exact production through the system, it'll tell me your number. I just want to make sure the concept makes sense to you."
  • Timing: "Maybe next year." Reframe as cost-of-waiting at the new January rate.
Ask the room: which category do we misdiagnose most often, and what does getting it wrong cost us?
Recall What is "think about it" really telling you?

Unresolved doubt, not a need for time. Surface the specific objection and diagnose its category (trust, information, stakeholder, financial framing, or timing) before you leave.

Part 9 · The Commitment

The path to 30%+

Three commitments, starting with your next appointment. None require working harder, knocking more, or carrying more leads.

01 · One solution only

Before every appointment, decide the product. PPA is default. Cash or loan only if discovery clearly supports it. Never present more than one financing option at the table.

02 · Both decision-makers, or reschedule

Confirm before you go. If the spouse is not present, do not run the full appointment. Stay concept-level. Coach the present homeowner. Reschedule and protect your time and theirs.

03 · Build the pain before the solution

Spend the first half of every consultation discovering, not presenting. Your notes should show what they feel, not just what they owe. If you wrote "interested" or "no," you didn't run an appointment.

Each rep commits out loud to one of the three for their next five appointments. The team holds them to it.
