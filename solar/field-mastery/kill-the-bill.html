Part 1 · The Power Move

The bill review is the most important moment in the appointment

Discovery wins or loses the deal. The bill is where discovery gets real. Walk the customer through their own bill line by line and they feel the cost in their gut, and teach themselves the problem in their own words.

"What if you could replace this with something that costs less and stays low?"The question behind every bill review

Why this earns the close

  • The bill is the contract. The customer already signed up to pay this every month for life. Solar isn't a new agreement; it's a better version of the one they have.
  • The bill is back-and-forth. They read usage off the bill while you tally. Two people, same conversation, same math. Collaboration, not a pitch.
  • The bill makes pain specific. "$185 last month" hits harder than "rates are going up." A real number from this house this year is the foundation.
  • The bill creates urgency. Show the highest months and the trend. The customer arrives at "this keeps going up" without you arguing it.
Ask the room: what's the last appointment you ran where you skipped the bill? What did it cost you?
Recall Why review the bill before quoting any number?

It makes the cost specific and felt, the customer names their own pain, and it earns the right to present a solution. The math closes, so you don't have to argue.

Part 2 · Headline vs. Effective

What they think they pay vs. what they actually pay

Customers see the supply rate at the top of the bill and think that's the cost of electricity. It's roughly 40% of it. The other 60% rides in charges most homeowners have never decoded.

~12-14¢/kWh

vs.
Real All-In Rate
30-35¢/kWh
Total bill divided by total kWh. What they actually pay per kilowatt-hour, about 2.5x the headline.

Calculate the all-in rate live

Don't tell them. Show them. Pull the bill. Add everything. Divide by total kWh.

Say"Let's do quick math. What's your total bill this month? And your usage? Now let's divide..."

When it lands at 30¢+/kWh, watch their face. The rate they've quoted friends for years was off by 2.5x. You didn't argue. The math did.

Connecticut context. CT averages ~28-30¢/kWh all-in, nearly double the national average. Tie urgency to a customer's specific bill at CT rates. That's real work. "Rates are going up" generically is not.

Part 3 · Anatomy of a Bill

The four charge buckets: what each one actually is

Every CT electric bill (Eversource and UI) breaks into the same four buckets. The colors below match the bill's own color-coded chart, so they line up at the kitchen table.

Supply~40%

The electrons themselves.

The cost to produce the electricity. Competitive, usage-dependent. This is the part that looks like the price of power (12-14¢/kWh).

Solar replaces nearly all of this.

Transmission~13%

High-voltage shipping.

Maintaining the towers and lines that carry power across the region. Federally regulated, usage-dependent.

Solar replaces most of this on the kWh you produce.

Local Delivery~33%

Last-mile poles, lines, meters.

Building and repairing everything between substation and house. PURA-regulated. Mostly per-kWh plus a fixed monthly charge ($9 to $13) that never goes away.

Solar replaces most of the variable portion. The fixed charge stays.

Public Benefits~13%

State-mandated programs.

Energy assistance, efficiency programs, renewable investment, grid-operator costs. Tiny line items that add up. Usage-dependent.

Solar reduces this with usage. Some programs persist on remaining grid use.

The bill's chart matches these colors. Both utilities print a bar at the top splitting usage into Supply, Transmission, Delivery, Public Benefits. Point at it: "see this chunk? That's just the electrons. This one? State programs." The visual makes the abstract concrete.
Quiz the room: which bucket does solar replace the most of? Which one never goes away?
Recall Which part of the bill does solar NOT replace?

The fixed monthly connection charge ($9 to $13), some mandated Public Benefits assessments, and any grid kWh drawn above what you produce.

Part 6 · Honest Expectations

What solar replaces, and what it doesn't

Solar gets the customer close to $0, not to $0. Setting honest expectations before they sign prevents post-install frustration when a small bill shows up in month 2.

What solar replaces

  • Most of Supply: your panels' electrons replace grid electrons dollar for dollar.
  • Most of Transmission: on the kWh you produce, no shipping cost.
  • Most variable Local Delivery: the per-kWh portion drops with reduced grid usage.
  • Most of Public Benefits: the per-kWh portion drops proportionally.
  • Net metering credits: excess banks and is drawn down at night and in winter.

What solar does NOT replace

  • Fixed monthly charge ($9 to $13): charged just to stay connected. Stays at any usage.
  • Some Public Benefits assessments: certain mandates apply regardless of consumption.
  • Grid kWh above production: long winters, EV spikes, heat pumps. Full retail on those.
  • The SB4 fee: a small recurring grid-services charge specific to CT solar customers.

How to set the expectation cleanly

Say"You'll have a bill. It won't be zero. You'll always have a small charge for the right to be connected. That's normal and fair. But the chunk you pay for the electricity itself, the number climbing every year? That's what we replace, and we lock it in for 25."

Said up-front, this prevents the most common post-install complaint: "I thought my bill would be zero." A customer who expects a $15 to $20 connection charge is happy. One surprised by it is a call to your manager.

Role-play: a customer says "so my bill goes to zero, right?" How do you answer honestly without losing momentum?
Recall Why set the "not zero" expectation before signing?

It prevents post-install frustration. A customer who understands the connection charge up front stays happy; a surprised one becomes a complaint.

Part 7 · The Field Technique

How to actually kill the bill at the table

Anthony Venditto's bill-review technique: the part of the appointment that earns the right to quote. The frame is two of you doing math together, not one presenting at the other.

  1. 1. Get the bill, persistently

    Before either of you sits, ask for the electric bill. No paper copy? Walk them to it on their phone. Don't move forward without it. The bill is the appointment.

  2. 2. Explain why

    "Two reasons: so we both understand how you pay for power now, and so I can size something that fits your usage." Collaborative, not extractive.

  3. 3. Make it back-and-forth

    They read usage off the bill; you tally. When they read the numbers out loud, the math is theirs.

  4. 4. Build the pain on why rates rise

    Demand growth (EVs, heat pumps, data centers) plus aging infrastructure plus inflation. Don't argue. State it and let them connect the dots.

  5. 5. Calculate the all-in rate together

    Add the four subtotals, divide by total kWh, watch it hit 30¢+. The friend-quoted headline is suddenly 40% of reality.

  6. 6. Connect to the future

    Point at the highest months. "What does this look like in 5 years? In 10?" Get them to say "this keeps going up." That's the bill killed.

"I want to make sure this part is super clear... the only thing that changes is how you're billed for power."North Star Pitch · bill-review transition

Questions to ask while pointing at the chart

  • "What was happening in your house during that highest month?"
  • "Is your AC the hardest worker here, or your heat?"
  • "What does this look like in 5 years if rates keep climbing?"
  • "If this peak were $400 instead of $307, how would that change things for your family?"
  • "Anything coming (an EV, a heat pump, a finished basement) that pushes these bars higher?"

The handoff to the close

Once the bill is killed and the customer has named their own pain, you've earned the right to present.

Say"What if we replaced the part of this that costs the most and goes up the most, with something that costs less and stays low?"

If discovery is complete, the answer is yes. What's left is design and math, and they're already in the math headspace, because you just did math together.
