Part 1 · The Power Move

The bill review is the most important moment in the appointment

Discovery wins or loses the deal. The bill is where discovery gets real. Walk the customer through their own bill line by line and they feel the cost in their gut, and teach themselves the problem in their own words.

"What if you could replace this with something that costs less and stays low?"The question behind every bill review

Why this earns the close

  • The bill is the contract. The customer already signed up to pay this every month for life. Solar isn't a new agreement; it's a better version of the one they have.
  • The bill is back-and-forth. They read usage off the bill while you tally. Two people, same conversation, same math. Collaboration, not a pitch.
  • The bill makes pain specific. "$185 last month" hits harder than "rates are going up." A real number from this house this year is the foundation.
  • The bill creates urgency. Show the highest months and the trend. The customer arrives at "this keeps going up" without you arguing it.
Ask the room: what's the last appointment you ran where you skipped the bill? What did it cost you?
Recall Why review the bill before quoting any number?

It makes the cost specific and felt, the customer names their own pain, and it earns the right to present a solution. The math closes, so you don't have to argue.

Part 2 · Headline vs. Effective

What they think they pay vs. what they actually pay

Customers see the supply rate at the top of the bill and think that's the cost of electricity. It's roughly 40% of it. The other 60% rides in charges most homeowners have never decoded.

Total bill divided by total kWh. What they actually pay per kilowatt-hour, about 2.5x the headline.

Calculate the all-in rate live

Don't tell them. Show them. Pull the bill. Add everything. Divide by total kWh.

Say"Let's do quick math. What's your total bill this month? And your usage? Now let's divide..."

When it lands at 30¢+/kWh, watch their face. The rate they've quoted friends for years was off by 2.5x. You didn't argue. The math did.

Connecticut context. CT averages ~28-30¢/kWh all-in, nearly double the national average. Tie urgency to a customer's specific bill at CT rates. That's real work. "Rates are going up" generically is not.

Part 3 · Anatomy of a Bill

The four charge buckets: what each one actually is

Every CT electric bill (Eversource and UI) breaks into the same four buckets. The colors below match the bill's own color-coded chart, so they line up at the kitchen table.

Supply~40%

The electrons themselves.

The cost to produce the electricity. Competitive, usage-dependent. This is the part that looks like the price of power (12-14¢/kWh).

Solar replaces nearly all of this.

Transmission~13%

High-voltage shipping.

Maintaining the towers and lines that carry power across the region. Federally regulated, usage-dependent.

Solar replaces most of this on the kWh you produce.

Local Delivery~33%

Last-mile poles, lines, meters.

Building and repairing everything between substation and house. PURA-regulated. Mostly per-kWh plus a fixed monthly charge ($9 to $13) that never goes away.

Solar replaces most of the variable portion. The fixed charge stays.

Public Benefits~13%

State-mandated programs.

Energy assistance, efficiency programs, renewable investment, grid-operator costs. Tiny line items that add up. Usage-dependent.

Solar reduces this with usage. Some programs persist on remaining grid use.

The bill's chart matches these colors. Both utilities print a bar at the top splitting usage into Supply, Transmission, Delivery, Public Benefits. Point at it: "see this chunk? That's just the electrons. This one? State programs." The visual makes the abstract concrete.
Quiz the room: which bucket does solar replace the most of? Which one never goes away?
Recall Which part of the bill does solar NOT replace?

The fixed monthly connection charge ($9 to $13), some mandated Public Benefits assessments, and any grid kWh drawn above what you produce.

Part 6 · Honest Expectations

Lead with what solar can do

Not every roof is a fit. Roughly one in three homes even qualifies. So we lead with what we can help a customer with, set the bar low, and over-deliver.

What solar can do for a qualified home

  • Replaces the costliest part of the bill: the supply you'd otherwise buy from the grid, dollar for dollar on the power your panels make.
  • Takes on most transmission and variable delivery on the kWh you produce, the charges that climb every year.
  • Locks in the rate on that power for 25 years, while the utility number keeps rising.
  • Banks extra production through net metering, drawn down at night and in winter.

Set the bar low, then over-deliver

  • Coverage depends on the home. Roof, shade, and usage decide how much we can help. Focus on what we can do, never on what we can't.
  • Even a piece is a win. You know how it can feel to wonder whether it's worth it. Plenty of homeowners felt exactly that, then found that even covering 20% of their usage left them ahead and glad they moved.
  • A small utility charge stays for the right to be connected to the grid. That's normal and fair, and worth saying up front.

How to set the expectation cleanly

Say"I hear how you feel. A lot of homeowners feel unsure it's even worth it. The folks I've helped felt the same way, and what they found was that even covering a piece of their usage left them ahead. Here's what I can help you with: the part of your bill that costs the most and climbs the most, we take that on and lock it in. You'll still have a small charge for being connected, and that's fine."

Lead with what you can do and set the bar low, and the customer is happy when you over-deliver. Promise full coverage and you've set a trap for month 2.

Sell with integrity. Never promise that solar erases the bill or covers everything. We set the bar low, then over-deliver. Everything you say and write at the table can be used against you later, so let the words match what the home can actually do. See Selling with Integrity.

Part 7 · The Field Technique

How to actually kill the bill at the table

Anthony Venditto's bill-review technique: the part of the appointment that earns the right to quote. The frame is two of you doing math together, not one presenting at the other.

  1. 1. Get the bill, persistently

    Before either of you sits, ask for the electric bill. No paper copy? Walk them to it on their phone. Don't move forward without it. The bill is the appointment.

  2. 2. Explain why

    "Two reasons: so we both understand how you pay for power now, and so I can size something that fits your usage." Collaborative, not extractive.

  3. 3. Make it back-and-forth

    They read usage off the bill; you tally. When they read the numbers out loud, the math is theirs.

  4. 4. Build the pain on why rates rise

    Demand growth (EVs, heat pumps, data centers) plus aging infrastructure plus inflation. Don't argue. State it and let them connect the dots.

  5. 5. Calculate the all-in rate together

    Add the four subtotals, divide by total kWh, watch it hit 30¢+. The friend-quoted headline is suddenly 40% of reality.

  6. 6. Connect to the future

    Point at the highest months. "What does this look like in 5 years? In 10?" Get them to say "this keeps going up." That's the bill killed.

"I want to make sure this part is super clear... the only thing that changes is how you're billed for power."North Star Pitch · bill-review transition
Each rep walks the six steps on a real bill in 90 seconds. Where do they rush? Where do they skip the back-and-forth?

Part 9 · Discovery's Real Job

Create the problem before you offer the solution

Reps sign far fewer deals than they could, and it is almost never the design or the price. It is that the customer never felt they had a problem. People do not buy a solution to a problem they have not admitted out loud. Killing the bill is how you build that problem so it is theirs, not yours.

"On an island 3 hours from a tsunami, someone offers you a life raft for $1,000. You're not saying let me think about it. If you're not convincing them there's no alternative, they don't think they have a problem, and that is your biggest problem."The life-raft frame · create the problem

The tsunami and the life raft

A $1,000 life raft is an easy no on a calm beach and an instant yes 3 hours from a tsunami. Same raft, same price. The only thing that changed is whether the buyer feels the danger. You sign a fraction of your appointments for the same reason: the customer never felt the wave coming. They are fighting complacency and a monopoly they have lived with for years, so the bill feels normal. Your job in discovery is to make the cost feel like impending doom, then be the raft.

  • No felt problem, no sale. A perfect design at a great number still gets "let me think about it" when the customer is sitting on a calm beach.
  • The bill is the wave. Everything in this deck, the all-in rate, the four buckets, the highest months, exists to make the danger real and specific.
  • Then offer the raft. Once they feel the cost, what you can do for their home lands as relief, not as a pitch.

Make them name the villain

People do not buy a solution to a problem they will not admit they have. So get the customer to say out loud how much they hate the utility before you introduce anything. When the frustration comes from their mouth, the emotion is theirs and the rest of the appointment is you helping, not selling.

In the home"How do you feel about what you pay every month? When that bill shows up in the summer, what goes through your head? CT has some of the highest rates in the country. Does that sit right with you?"

Let them harp on it. The longer they talk about how much they resent the cost, the less convincing you have to do later. You are not putting words in their mouth, you are handing them the microphone.

Sell what it buys, not the panels

Try to see how rarely you can say the word "solar" in an appointment. We do not sell a product and we do not sell an abstract reallocation of money. We sell the new refrigerator, the extra vacation, time and tournaments with the kids, money toward college, the hobby they keep putting off. Find what matters to this specific family and sell that. The system is just how they get it.

  • Ask the highest bill, never the average. The average is just a number. The highest bill is a memory with a feeling attached.
  • Make them feel it with you. "How did you cover that one? What did you do differently that month?" You do not care what the number is. You care that they feel it sitting next to you.
  • Price-condition with their own number. If you get them to say 800 in July, every number you present under that feels like a relief, because it came from their mouth, not your sheet.
In the home"Forget the average. What's the single highest bill you've ever opened? How did you feel when you saw it?"

Anchor on the utility, and your number shrinks

Before you ever show a price, make the utility the scary number. Production times the all-in rate across 25 years, with the escalator working against them, climbs past $100,000. That is money they have already decided to spend, they just never saw it added up. Put that on the table first and whatever you present next barely registers.

In the home"$100,000-plus is what you've already decided to hand the utility over the next 25 years, in the 5 minutes we just spent on your bill. Instead of throwing that away, here's what you could do with it instead."

Once the big number is the utility's, your monthly is small by comparison. The order matters: build the problem, anchor on their cost, then offer what you can do for the home.

Build the problem honestly. Creating the problem means making a real cost feel real, never inventing one or promising more than the home can do. The bill is true, the rates are true, the highest month is true. Lead with what solar can do, set the bar low, and over-deliver. See Selling with Integrity.
