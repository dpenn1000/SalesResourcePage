Part 1 · The Power Move

Why Killing the Bill Is the Most Important Moment in the Appointment

Discovery is where the deal gets won or lost. The bill is where discovery gets real. By the time you've walked the customer through their own bill line by line, they've felt the cost in their gut. They've also taught themselves what the problem actually is -- in their own words. That's how a customer commits to solar before you ever quote a number.

"What if you could replace this with something that costs less and stays low?" The Question Behind Every Bill Review

Why this earns the close

  • The bill is the contract. The customer already signed up to pay this every month for the rest of their life. Solar is not a new agreement. It's a better version of the one they already have.
  • The bill is back-and-forth. Have the customer read their usage off the bill while you tally on your device. Two of you, in the same conversation, doing the same math. That's not a sales pitch -- that's collaboration.
  • The bill makes pain specific. "$185 last month" hits differently than "rates are going up." A real number from this house, this customer, this year -- that's the foundation everything else stands on.
  • The bill creates the urgency. Show the highest months. Show the trend. Let the customer project forward. They'll arrive at "this is going to keep going up" without you having to argue for it.
Part 2 · Headline vs. Effective

What They Think They Pay vs. What They Actually Pay

Customers see the supply rate at the top of the bill and think that's the cost of electricity. It isn't. It's roughly 40% of the cost of electricity. The other 60% is delivered through a combination of charges most homeowners have never decoded.

Headline Supply Rate
~12-14¢/kWh
What they see at the top of the bill. What they tell their friends they pay. What they remember when shopping rates.
vs.
Real All-In Rate
30-35¢/kWh
Total bill divided by total kWh used. This is what they actually pay for every kilowatt-hour. About 2.5× the headline rate.

Calculate the all-in rate live with the customer

Don't tell them. Show them. Pull their bill. Add up everything. Divide by total kWh used.

"Let's do some quick math. What's your total bill this month? OK -- what was your usage? Now let's divide…"

When the number comes out at 30¢+/kWh, watch their face. That's the moment. The headline rate they've been quoting their friends for years was off by a factor of two and a half. You didn't argue. The math did.

Connecticut context. CT averages around 28-30¢/kWh all-in -- nearly double the national average. That's the strongest urgency lever you have. A rep who ties urgency to a customer's specific bill at CT rates is doing real work. A rep who says "rates are going up" generically is not.
Part 3 · Anatomy of a Bill

The Four Charge Buckets -- What Each One Actually Is

Every CT electric bill (Eversource and UI) breaks into the same four buckets. The colors below match the way the bills themselves are color-coded -- so when you're sitting at the kitchen table reading along, the colors line up.

Supply ~40%

The electrons themselves.

The cost to actually produce the electricity. Not regulated -- based on competitive procurement, dependent on usage. This is the part that looks like the price of electricity (12–14¢/kWh).

Solar replaces nearly all of this.

Transmission ~13%

High-voltage shipping.

The cost to maintain the high-voltage towers and lines that carry electricity across the region. Federally regulated. Dependent on usage.

Solar replaces most of this on the kWh you produce.

Local Delivery ~33%

Last-mile poles, lines, and meters.

The cost for the utility to build, maintain, and repair everything between the substation and the house. State-regulated by PURA. Mostly per-kWh, but includes a fixed monthly charge ($9–$13) that doesn't go away.

Solar replaces most of the variable portion. The fixed monthly charge stays.

Public Benefits ~13%

State-mandated programs.

Energy assistance, energy efficiency programs, renewable investment, grid operator costs -- charges the state mandates that flow through your bill. Dependent on usage. The line items are tiny individually but add up.

Solar reduces this proportionally to usage. Some programs persist on whatever grid usage remains.

The bill's chart matches these colors. Both Eversource and UI print a horizontal bar at the top of the bill that visually breaks usage into Supply (blue), Transmission (green), Delivery (cyan), Public Benefits (orange). When you're walking the customer through it, point at the colored chart on their bill -- "see this blue chunk? That's just the electrons. See this orange? That's the state programs." The visual makes the abstract concrete.

Part 5 · Honest Expectations

What Solar Replaces -- and What It Doesn't

Solar doesn't get the customer to a $0 bill. It gets them close. Knowing exactly what stays and what goes lets you set honest expectations and prevent post-install frustration. Customers who understand this before they sign don't get upset when they see a small bill in month 2.

What Solar Replaces

  • Most of Supply -- the electrons your panels make replace electrons from the grid, dollar for dollar.
  • Most of Transmission -- on the kWh you produce, you don't pay to ship them through the high-voltage system.
  • Most of variable Local Delivery -- the per-kWh portion of distribution charges goes down with reduced grid usage.
  • Most of Public Benefits -- the per-kWh portion drops proportionally with usage.
  • Net metering credits -- excess production banks at the same effective rate, drawn down at night and in winter.

What Solar Does NOT Replace

  • Fixed monthly charge ($9–$13) -- the line item the utility charges you just for being connected to the grid. Stays whether you use 0 kWh or 5,000 kWh.
  • Some Public Benefits assessments -- certain state-mandated charges apply regardless of consumption.
  • Any kWh you draw from the grid above what you produce -- long winter weeks, an EV plug-in spike, a heat pump running hard. You pay full retail for those kWh.
  • The SB4 fee -- a small recurring grid-services charge specific to solar customers in CT.

How to set the expectation cleanly

"You'll have a bill. It won't be zero. You'll always have a small bill from the utility for the right to be connected to the grid -- that's normal and that's fair. But the chunk you're paying for the electricity itself? That's what we're replacing. The number that's been climbing every year? That's the number we're locking in for 25."

Said up-front, this prevents the most common post-install frustration: "I thought my bill was going to be zero." A signed customer who understands they'll have a $15–$20 utility connection charge is a happy customer. A signed customer surprised by it is a phone call to your manager.

Part 6 · The Field Technique

How to Actually Kill the Bill at the Table

This is Anthony Venditto's bill review technique -- the part of the appointment that earns you the right to quote a number. The frame: two of you doing math together, not one of you presenting at the other.

1

Be persistent about getting the bill

Just before either of you sits down, ask for the electric bill. If they don't have a paper copy, walk them through pulling it up on their phone or computer. Don't move forward without it. The bill is the appointment. Anything else is theater.

2

Explain why you're asking

"I ask for the bill for two reasons. First, so we both understand exactly how you're paying for power right now. Second, so I can figure out how much power you need every year and design something that fits." Set the frame: this is collaborative, not extractive.

3

Make it back-and-forth

Have the customer read their usage off the bill while you tally on your device. Two people, same conversation, same math. When they're the one reading the numbers out loud, the math is theirs.

4

Build the pain by talking about why rates must go up

The combined forces of demand growth (EVs, heat pumps, AI data centers, electrification) and infrastructure maintenance coupled with inflation guarantee the trend. Don't argue. State it plainly. Let them connect the dots.

5

Calculate the total utility rate together

Add the four bucket subtotals. Divide by total kWh. Watch the number come out to 30¢+. The headline rate they've been quoting their friends for years is suddenly 40% of the real number. That's the moment of clarity that makes solar an obvious yes.

6

Connect the bill to the future

Reference the highest months. Project forward. "What do you think this looks like in 5 years? In 10?" Get the customer to say "this is going to keep going up" in their own words. That's the bill killed.

"I want to make sure this part is super clear… the only thing that changes is how you're billed for power." North Star Pitch · Bill-Review Transition

Questions to ask while pointing at the chart

  • "What was happening in your house during that highest month?"
  • "Is your AC the hardest worker in this house, or your heat?"
  • "What does this picture look like in 5 years if rates keep climbing?"
  • "If this peak were $400 instead of $307, how would that change things for your family?"
  • "Anything coming -- an EV, a heat pump, a finished basement -- that would push these bars higher?"

The handoff to the close

Once the bill is killed and the customer has named their own pain in their own words, you've earned the right to present a solution. The transition is simple:

"What if we replaced the part of this that costs the most and goes up the most -- with something that costs less and stays low?"

If discovery is complete, the answer is yes. The only thing left is the design and the math. Both of which they're already in the math headspace for, because you just did math together.
