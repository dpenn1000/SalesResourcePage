The Rate Escalator

Part 2 · The 40-Year Story

What actually happened

The cost-shift narrative against solar works because the public has no historical baseline. Here is the baseline. Every notch on this timeline predates or is unrelated to residential solar.

1990 · CT pays ~10.7¢/kWh
About 24% premium over the U.S. average. New England already runs hot on rates because of fuel mix and capital intensity. No residential solar to blame.
1998 · Deregulation (PA 98-28)
CL&P and UI divest generation, standard offer set at 10% below 1996 levels. Brief reprieve through 2003. The competitive market that replaces it ends up clearing on the marginal cost of natural gas.
2005 · Katrina/Rita gas spike
Henry Hub averages ~$8.69/MMBtu. CT rates jump from 13.0¢ to 17.0¢ in a single year. Eversource's internal history flags 2005 as one of the two biggest step-ups on record.
2008 to 2012 · Shale era
Gas prices collapse. CT rates stabilize in the high teens. The wholesale market shifts decisively to gas-fired generation as coal and oil exit (40% of NE supply in 2000, less than 0.5% by 2023).
2014 · Polar vortex
Pipeline constraints into New England drive winter spot gas prices to $100+/MMBtu at the burner tip. CT ratepayers absorb it through the supply component.
2022 · Ukraine war and LNG shock
Global gas exports tighten. CT rates surge to 25.1¢ in 2022, 26.8¢ in 2023, 28.2¢ in 2024. The same period sees the most aggressive cost-shift messaging against solar, not because solar caused it, but because someone had to be blamed.
April 2026 · PURA interim relief
PURA's interim decision reduces the standard offer by ~4.3¢/kWh through at least September, mostly via Millstone/Seabrook contract savings. The relief is real but temporary; the structural drivers underneath are unchanged.
2026+ · Rate case + electrification + data centers
Eversource's pending rate case, EV and heat-pump load growth, and AI data center demand all point the same direction. ISO-NE forecasts and DEEP planning documents are consistent: load is going up faster than supply.
Recall What has actually driven CT's 40-year rate climb?

Gas-market shocks (2005, 2014, 2022), pipeline constraints, and steady utility capex, none of it residential solar. CT had only about 4% solar penetration through 2024.

Part 3 · Real Drivers

What's actually pushing rates up

Three structural forces. None of them are solar. All of them keep pushing one way.

1. Natural gas dominance and pipeline constraints

54% of New England's electricity in 2025 came from gas-fired generators. Because ISO-NE clears at the marginal price of the most expensive unit needed each hour, and that unit is almost always gas, every wholesale electron is priced as if it came from gas.

Connecticut sits at the end of the interstate pipeline system. When gas demand spikes in winter, prices at New England city gates diverge sharply from Henry Hub, sometimes by an order of magnitude. CT ratepayers absorb that through the supply component of their bill.

At the table"Your rate moves with the price of natural gas, not the price of solar. The last forty years of your bill is mostly the gas market and the pipelines into New England."

2. Transmission and distribution capex

The wires are a regulated monopoly. Under cost-of-service ratemaking, utilities earn a regulated return on every dollar of capital they put into the rate base. Storm hardening, asset condition projects, smart meters, EV chargers, transmission build-outs: every dollar invested is recovered from ratepayers with a return on top.

This part of the bill grows on its own schedule, independent of generation prices. It is why delivery charges keep climbing even when supply prices fall.

At the table"About 40% of your bill is delivery and transmission. That number goes up when the utility builds anything, and they always build."

3. The cost-of-service ratchet

Cost-of-service ratemaking is structurally biased upward. When a utility's costs go up, they file a rate case and get them back. When costs go down, those savings are often passed back slowly, partially, or only at the next rate case. The mechanism does not symmetrically push prices down.

It is why the only periods of meaningful rate relief in CT's history are policy interventions (the 2025 PURA decision) or commodity windfalls (the post-2008 shale glut). Neither is a market mechanism.

At the table"The system is designed to recover utility costs from you. It is not designed to keep your bill flat. The way you get flat is by stepping outside the system."

What the Public Benefits Charge actually is

The PBC is about 20% of the bill. It is not primarily a solar subsidy. It funds (in rough order): the Millstone and Seabrook nuclear contracts, transmission siting, low-income assistance, energy efficiency programs, and a smaller tail of renewable procurement. From May 2026 through at least September it is a credit, not a charge, because of PA 25-173 and the Millstone PPA savings.

Be proactive"It is not solar, it is mostly nuclear contracts and grid programs, and right now it is actually a credit." Don't bluff.
Quiz the room: a customer says the Public Benefits Charge is the "solar tax." What's the honest, one-sentence decomposition?
Recall Name the three structural drivers, and why none of them reverse on their own.

Gas-priced wholesale market, utility T&D capex earning a regulated return, and a cost-of-service ratchet that recovers increases faster than it returns savings. Relief only comes from policy or commodity luck, not the mechanism.

The trade-off, said plainly

Pick a higher escalator to start at a lower rate. Pick a lower escalator to start higher but compound slower. There is no "best" option in the abstract, only the option that fits how long the customer plans to be in the home and how much volatility they're willing to absorb.

What's not a trade-off: the choice between any of these and CT's actual trajectory. All three solar lines are below CT High by 2050. Two are below CT Base. The customer is stepping off the cost-of-service ratchet at one of three speeds.

Sell with integrity

The chart is a reframe, not a promise. Sell the trajectory, never a guaranteed endpoint, and never tell a customer solar erases their bill or covers everything. We set the bar low and over-deliver. What you say and write at the table can be used against you later, so keep the claims to what the home can actually do. See Selling with Integrity.

Recall What's the real choice the chart frames for the customer?

Not which solar line is "best" in the abstract, but whether to keep riding CT's actual trajectory or step onto a known line below it. Match the escalator to how long they'll stay.

Part 5 · Kitchen-Table Playbook

How to use this in the room

The chart is not a closing tool. It is a reframe tool. Open with it; do not pitch with it.

1

Open with the customer's bill, not the chart

Ask what they're paying per kWh on their last bill. Get it from them, do not tell them. Then mark it on the chart. Their dot is now on the page.

What's the rate on the supply side of your last Eversource bill? Okay, you're right here. That's about 27¢. Let me show you where that's been heading.
2

Walk the historical line, not the projection

Trace 1990 to 2025 first. The story is what already happened, not what might. Hit the 2005, 2014, 2022 events so the customer can match their own memory to the spikes.

Remember the polar vortex? That's this dot. Remember when the Ukraine war started and your bill suddenly went up? That's this one. None of those things had anything to do with solar.
3

Name the structural drivers without lecturing

Two sentences, maximum. Gas. Wires. The customer doesn't need the cost-of-service ratemaking explainer; they need the takeaway.

Your bill goes up for three reasons: natural gas prices, which we can't control; transmission and distribution investment, which the utility passes through to you; and the way the regulation works, which means costs go up faster than they come down.
4

Show the solar line at their rate

Toggle off the scenarios they don't need. Show one solar line that matches what you're about to offer. Don't bury them in three options at once.

If we lock you in here, at 18.35¢, with a 1.99% increase each year, this is your line. Notice it doesn't follow theirs.
5

End on the baseline, not the projection

The customer doesn't have to believe the projection to make the decision. They have to believe the history. The projection is a reasonable extrapolation, but the history is fact.

You don't have to take my word for the next twenty years. You already lived the last twenty. The question is whether you want to keep riding that line, or step off it.
Each rep runs the five-step chart open in 90 seconds. Where do they start pitching instead of reframing?
Recall Where do you open, and where do you end?

Open on the customer's own rate (get it from them), and end on the lived history, not the projection. They don't have to believe the forecast to believe the last twenty years.

Part 6 · Make It Believable

Four moves that make the rate stick

The chart frames the problem. These four delivery moves are how you make a customer feel it, believe it, and stop arguing the small number. Each one is a habit, not a script.

1. Write it down to make it believable

Anything you write on paper in front of a customer lands as more credible than the same words spoken. It is a free, universal trust move. When you walk the rate history or name a driver, put the key number on the page where they can see it.

Best use: have the customer predict their own future bill, then write that number down and circle it. Those are their words, not yours. Keep pointing back to the circled number and it becomes their reality. If they give you two numbers, write both and circle the higher one, because they fixate on what is circled.

At the table"You said in ten years you figure you'll be around 350 a month. Let me write that down. That's your number, not mine. So when we look at this line, that's what we're measuring against."

2. Flip "they haven't raised rates in years"

When a customer leans on a quiet stretch from the utility, do not argue it. Turn the stability into the warning. The longer a utility has gone without a hike, the closer and more likely the next one is. Pressure builds; it does not disappear.

At the table"That's actually the part I'd watch. The longer they've held off, the closer you are to the next increase, not further from it. Costs they haven't recovered yet don't vanish, they stack up and come back in a rate case."

3. Show what they WOULD have paid, not last April's bill

The most common deescalator after install, and a strong reframe at the table too. Customers compare their solar to last year's bill, but the utility rate may have climbed 20 to 30% since then, so it is apples to oranges. Compare current usage at today's utility rate against their solar rate. Show what they WOULD be paying right now with no solar, versus what they did pay.

At the table"Don't measure this against last April. Last April's rate could be 20 to 30% lower than today's. The fair comparison is what you'd be paying right now at this year's rate versus what you're locked into."

4. Less is more on the small escalator

The more you talk about the 2.9% increase, the more power you hand it. Over-explaining a small number makes it feel like a big one. State it plainly, tie it back to the circled bill, and move on. Confidence is quiet here.

At the table"Your rate steps up a little under 3% a year. That's it. Next to a utility line that's nearly tripled, that's the trade you want." Then stop talking and let it sit.
Hand each rep a blank sheet. Run the bill-prediction move live: get a number, write it, circle it, point back to it three times in 60 seconds. Then catch who over-talks the 2.9%.
Recall Why circle the customer's predicted bill, and why go quiet on the 2.9%?

A number you write and circle in front of them becomes their reality, in their own words, so you keep pointing back to it. And the more you talk about the small escalator, the bigger it feels, so state it once and let it sit.

Part 7 · Anti-Patterns

What not to say

This chart is credible because the data is sourced and the projections are defensible. It stops being credible the moment you treat it like a magic wand.

Anti-pattern 01 · Promising a specific 2050 rate
"Eversource will be at 75¢ in 2050. It's right here on the chart."
Fix: The chart shows scenarios, not promises. Say "if the last 35 years keep going, you're looking at something in this range." Sell the trajectory, not the endpoint.
Anti-pattern 02 · Blaming politicians or specific companies by name
"Eversource is screwing you. The governor is screwing you."
Fix: Talk about the system, not the actors. The customer's opinion of Eversource is not yours to set. The structural drivers do the work without you picking sides.
Anti-pattern 03 · Claiming solar caused or fixed CT's rates
"Solar would have prevented the 2022 spike."
Fix: Solar is one of the few forces pushing back, but at ~4% penetration through 2024 it cannot move the regional market. The honest claim is that their bill stops being exposed to it, not that the state's bill does.
Anti-pattern 04 · Hiding the PBC
"The Public Benefits Charge is the solar tax."
Fix: The PBC is mostly nuclear contracts, efficiency programs, and low-income assistance. From May 2026 onward it is currently a credit. If the customer raises it, give them the honest decomposition and move on.
Anti-pattern 05 · Overusing the chart
"Let me walk you through every line on this chart."
Fix: The chart is a 90-second tool. If you're still on it after 4 minutes, you've lost the room. Toggle most lines off. Show the customer's number, the historical trajectory, and the one solar line you're offering.
